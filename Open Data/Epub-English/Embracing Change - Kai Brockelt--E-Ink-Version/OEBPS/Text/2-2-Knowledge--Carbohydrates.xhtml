2.2 Knowledge:

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the most common organic compounds we find in our food. Although carbs or sugar are often associated with being overweight, they are the primary fuel our body and brain work on.

Carbohydrates come with an energetic value of 4kcal per gram.

Why is this important?

Carbohydrates are not all equal. In this chapter, you learn the differences between “fast sugar” and “slow carbs.”

You learn which types of carbs are beneficial for your health and how to avoid sugar spikes and cravings that might follow up.

This allows you to pick foods that slowly digest and keep you full longer.

What exactly are carbs?

Any carbohydrate is always a combination of sugars. The smallest carb unit is called “monosaccharide” in Latin, which roughly translates to “single sugar.” The most common single sugars are glucose, fructose, and galactose. Combining glucose with fructose builds a molecule called sucrose, commonly known as sugar. Combine three or more glucose molecules, and you get starch, the plant’s long-term energy storage.

The higher the complexity of combined sugars, the harder it is for our body to break down the construction into absorbable sugars. If the carbohydrate is too complex to be broken down by our digestive tract, we refer to it as fiber.

In natural foods, you usually spot all three types of carbohydrates together. Free sugar and starch are mixed into a compound of fiber.

Our ingestive track breaks down those molecules into single sugars. We can absorb these only if a carbohydrate is broken down into its smallest pieces.

Three frequent types of simple sugars

If you look at the smallest unit of a carbohydrate, the monosaccharide or single sugar, three types of sugars are most commonly found: Glucose, Fructose, and Galactose.

Glucose:

Glucose is the primary fuel of our brain. We can absorb glucose directly into our blood and transform into energy (ATP) from this form. Therefore, glucose lets your blood sugar spike fast.

Fructose and galactose:

Unlike glucose, those sugars cannot be directly used for energy production. They do not get absorbed into our blood directly but fructose and galactose must be restructured into glucose in the liver beforehand.

The combination of one glucose with one fructose creates sucrose or plain sugar. If you combine glucose with galactose, you get lactose, a sugar found in milk.

How to spot sugars on products?

Fructose, glucose, saccharose, dextrose… There are many different terms for sugars. If you read through the ingredients list and spot anything ending in -ose, you can be confident that this is another word for sugar.

You may encounter different terms for added sugars on the ingredients list of a product. It’s best to be aware of the wording so that can spot them easily.

You also find more complex carbs ending in -ose, including fiber. The most commonly known fiber is cellulose, which is the stuff that keeps plant cell walls stiff and robust. We make use of it to create paper or clothes. We still eat plenty of complex fiber from natural foods. It can support our digestion by feeding the microbiome living in our gut, as a carrier for chemical waste from our body, or simply by being firm enough to move stuff forward through our bowel.

Sugar in our blood

Since glucose is the primary fuel for the body and brain, we find plenty of it floating around in our blood. The sugar concentration is strongly connected to our body functions and needs to be within a healthy range. Too high sugar levels (Hyperglycemia) can damage vessels that deliver blood to our organs and increase the risk for diseases like stroke, heart disease, liver disease, and even neuronal damage.

On the other hand, a too-low blood sugar level (Hypoglycemia) announces itself with increased clumsiness, lack of concentration, or problems talking correctly. Most commonly, this happens if you overdose on insulin as a diabetic person, but also a long hunger period, certain drugs, and liver diseases can cause that. Generally speaking, this is not a problem under normal conditions, as long as you eat properly.

To avoid those problems, our body strictly controls blood sugar levels and tries to keep things in balance.

  • High blood sugar levels typically get lowered through the release of insulin. Insulin will open the cell walls for sugar to move into the mitochondria, where it gets used for energy production (ATP) and long-term storage through conversion to fatty acids.
  • Low blood sugar levels trigger the release of the hormone glucagon, the counterpart of insulin. It is responsible for dismantling glycogen and slowing down glucose consumption to raise blood sugar levels. Low blood sugar also is involved in the sensation of hunger.

Sugar absorption, insulin, and diabetes

As sugar is our primary energy source, our body is greedy and absorbs any sugar it can find - even when already having high glucose in the system. We don’t “pass” on excess sugar; we onboard all we can and then try to store it. A too high blood sugar level then gets lowered through the release of insulin.

The more complex a carbohydrate in our food is, the longer it takes to break it down into useful bits of single sugars. As a result, “fast sugars” get absorbed in no time and let the blood sugar levels spike high, where insulin gets released to regulate the peak down. “Slow” and complex carbs take longer to be dissolved into sugars and stretch the absorption over a long time. Consequently, the sugar levels don’t spike too high, and no insulin or not as much insulin needs to be released to regulate the levels back down.

Since high blood sugar levels are dangerous, the insulin response gets more drastic the higher the level tends to climb. A “sugar high” then gets regulated down faster with the help of more insulin. However, this can again lead to super low sugar levels, making us crave more.

Falling for sweets to act on hunger can end in a vicious cycle. High insulin levels get released, quickly lowering the blood sugar back to the hungry level. Despite having plenty of calories available, hunger kicks in again to bring blood sugar back up to normal. Each time that happens, we tend to crave more than we need. However, it is much more unlikely to spike blood sugar levels that high if sugar enters the bloodstream slowly.

Complex carbs enter the bloodstream after being broken down over time and won’t cause too high blood sugar levels. Less insulin gets released, and the blood sugar moves slowly. We stay full longer and don’t risk overeating calories, which end up on our hips.

Diabetic people can profit a lot from switching to more complex carbs. With a slowly moving blood sugar level, the need for insulin injections gets reduced drastically. If focusing on whole foods and complex carbs with much fiber, in some cases, insulin is not needed at all.

If you have diabetes, always check your blood sugar levels. And if you plan to change your diet, please involve your doctor to solve any doubts.

Glycaemic Index & Glycaemic load

Science has two tools to rate the quality of carbohydrates. Those are the Glycaemic Index and the Glycaemic Load. What sounds fancy in the first place mainly describes two simple things:

  • Glycaemic Index (GI): This is an indicator of how fast sugar gets absorbed into our blood. It is represented by the steepness of the blood sugar rise.
  • Glycaemic Load (GL): the impact from sugar absorbed from a given meal. Principally, the area covered by the graph represents the load.

Both are factors that influence our response to glucose levels. The faster we absorb sugar, the more likely we face a spike in blood sugar levels.That is described by the glycaemic index, which is between 0 and 100. The more sugar is absorbed at high rate, the higher your blood sugar can rise.

Put simple: If blood sugar is a punch in the face, the glycaemic index describes the speed of your hand moving, and the glycaemic load represents mass being moved for impact (your fist size)

The GI, or the „speed“ indicates how fast a carbohydrate spikes our blood sugar compared to pure glucose. A score of 100 means that carbohydrate is absorbed as quickly as pure glucose. A score of 50 would mean that the carbohydrate you’re looking at takes twice as much time to be absorbed.

The glycaemic load describes how much carbohydrate energy is available in food. A small treat with a high index but a low load might raise your blood sugar steeply, but if the amount of sugar provided is not too high, the glycaemic load is too low to trigger insulin release.

Eating fast sugars in high amounts (High GI + high GL) easily triggers insulin release for regulating blood sugar. This will bring you back to the hungry zone in no time, which creates cravings for more calories.

If you “flatten” the curve by either lowering the GI or the GL, you naturally can feed longer on your energy. Hunger attacks are less common when no excess sugar must be forced out of the blood by transforming into body fat.

Are syrups better for you?

Many syrups get sold as a “more healthy” or “more natural” alternative to sugars. But is that so? Let’s take a deeper look.

Syrups often consist of high amounts of fructose. That means that syrup needs to get absorbed through the liver to be transformed into usable glucose. A pure fructose syrup, therefore, has a relatively low glycaemic index of 23.

If you aim to avoid those nasty sugar spikes but do not want to skip the sweetness, then fructose syrups do have a benefit.

However:

If you regularly consume those types of sugars that go through the liver, you put much work into it. While not spiking blood sugar directly, high fructose consumption is associated with the development of increased insulin resistance3 in the long run and other adverse health effects.

When looking at the “nutrient gap” of processed foods, fructose syrup still is an empty sugar. You get 100% energy in this liquid, with only traces of other nutrients inside. So in this perspective, we still got sugar, an empty carb.

So be aware that neither sugar nor syrup has any nutritional value. Fructose and Galactose only get absorbed slower into the blood and can be used to avoid the sugary slap in the face if you accidentally take too much of it.

Get in more complex carbs!

Natural foods or natural food products contain carbohydrates in complex compounds. Unlike free sugars or starch, breaking down whole foods takes longer. Higher contents of indigestible fiber take away room for usable sugars, and the glycaemic load is relatively small compared to foods built on extracts.

Complexity also lowers the glycaemic index and slows down absorption. The smaller the bits and pieces in our digestive tract are, the more surface area is there to start digestion on. The more rough and natural a food is, the less dissolvable and absorbable a carbohydrate becomes. Despite not changing a load of carbohydrates, processing grains into fine flour still promotes faster absorption.

So the more complex and natural a food is, the more graceful the absorption. There are many ways of improving the glycaemic index without restricting your calories.

  • Swap plain pasta for whole grain pasta
  • try alternatives to polished white rice, and go for more colorful rice types
  • Find alternative sweets like whole grain cookies over plain ones
  • Eat grains directly in their whole natural form. There are plenty of options available. Quinoa, wheat, barley, rye, and oats… to name the most common ones.
  • Buy whole grain bread or bread stuffed with seeds.

Healthy alternatives to sugar

Some opinions out there try to sell you alternative sugar forms as more healthy variants to sweeten your meals. The most common misconception is swapping white sugar for brown sugar or syrups like agave.

Brown sugar contains a little more nutrients, bringing 0.25 fewer kcals per gram (out of 4.2). However, this gap and the little nutritional extra from the molasses don't need to be addressed and have no impact on an overall diet.

Liquid syrups often are just based on fructose. Again, we lack nutrients. Indeed, fructose does not go into our blood directly; it takes the long road through the liver, where it gets transformed into glucose first. This, however, does not improve the problem; it just shifts it toward another organ. The amount of energy and lack of nutrients practically stay the same.

Unlike refined sugars or syrups, molasses start bringing more nutritional value. You start finding things like Vitamin b6 and minerals like iron, calcium, magnesium.

From the world of products, that’s pretty much it. I recommend adding sweetness through whole and natural foods, mainly fruit. Brighten up a salad with berries, apples, or pear slices. You can add lemon or lime for sweet and sour. If you want to go strong on sweetness, you can replace refined sugar with dates in most recipes.

Should you ban sugar from your shopping list?

That’s not necessary. The keyword here again is moderation. I also sometimes get cravings and eat chocolate, cookies, and sweets. I also have sugar, syrups, and molasses available in my household. They are just a convenient thing to use to generate sweetness. A little pinch of sugar here and there won’t ruin a balanced diet.

If we look back at the recommendation of the WHO, we should consume at most 10% of our energy in the form of free sugars, ideally below 5%. Considering a diet of 3000 kcal/day - that still makes 300 kcal/day of free sugar, or 150 kcal if we try to hit the 5% respectively.

That is still 75g (37,5g at 5%) of free sugars. If you wonder how much that is, here is a photo of 75g of pure cane sugar next to a lemon for scale.

How much are 75g of sugar?

If you aim to lower your intake of free sugars, it helps to get an idea of how much sugar is hidden in which foods. You find 75g of free sugar in

  • Three snickers of 47g / 1.66 oz
  • Two and a half cans of cola
  • 200g / 7oz of chocolate chip cookies
  • 140g / 5oz of Nutella (6-7 tablespoons)

Sugar in fruit is not considered free unless the fruit is juiced or pureed. For comparison, here’s how much fruit to consume reaching 75g:

  • 4-5 medium-sized apples (~ 800g / 28oz)
  • 930ml / 31fl oz freshly pressed orange juice
  • 5-6 medium-sized bananas (~500-600g / 17–21 oz )
  • 1.5 kg / 52oz of strawberries
  • 1.25 kg / 45oz of watermelon
  • 1 kg / 35oz of pineapple

Do you find natural sweetness not sweet enough?

This is quite common. If your taste buds are used to getting the strong stuff, your sensation of sweetness from natural sources may appear dull. Don’t let this stop you, though.

If you transition away from free sugars, you might learn to enjoy natural sweetness better. See this as an opportunity, as it opens many doors toward foods you might not have liked before.

Sugar-bloated candy then might be perceived as too sweet, and you will avoid them automatically.

Conclusion

A shift to more natural and complex food is an excellent way to control your sugars and fast carbs. The more you replace free sugars with complex carbs, the longer lasting they will keep you full.

Replacing refined sugars with naturally occurring sweetness through fruit helps you to avoid blood glucose peaks and slowly recovers your sensation of sweet taste.

Going complex and whole-food-oriented gives you more micronutrients than the plain energy from carbohydrates. With whole foods and complex carbs, it is easy to hit the WHO recommendation to avoid high amounts of sugar, which also helps you keep your insulin in check.
