How to make a chili

Chili is a traditional tex-mex dish from Tejano people in Texas. It is a stew based on beans and tomatoes and delivers a decent punch of spice. You find versions with minced meat (chili con carne) or without (chili sin carne).

Like any stew, chili is a mix of solid ingredients and gravy. You can use a wide variety of vegetables in your stew. What turns a stew into chili is the selection of its base ingredients: Beans and tomato sauce. Also typical for tex-mex and Mexican food is the use of limes and cilantro.

To get a decent stew boiling, you usually follow the same steps:

  1. Fry onions, some vegetables, and meat to produce roast aromas through the Maillard reaction.
  2. Add stock, tomatoes, and spices to change the fry into a soup.
  3. Add everything solid and slowly boil until tender. Serve hot with fresh herbs (cilantro) and lime.

Basic ingredients and food groups

Cooking chili makes it easy to incorporate all five food groups. Chili is based on beans and diced tomatoes in the first place. I like to use fresh tomatoes if the season gives them a strong flavor. Black beans give the dish a tender texture, are nutrient-dense, and are packed with antioxidants.

Sweet potato adds beta carotene and a good amount of carbs. Corn, onions, and garlic round up the picture. As a side, you typically eat bread, rice, or nachos. Add avocado on top for your selection of healthy fats.

We already cover most of the food groups with just the base ingredients. Only nuts are missing here. This is a quick fix, as crumbled walnuts, cashews, or almonds can quickly be sprinkled on the plate if needed, allowing you to go full circle on just one dish.

Creating the flavour profile

As always, we intend to create a complete flavor profile. This time, the focus is on salty and sweet from stock and vegetables. A touch of bitter is always there. Lime rounds up the picture and adds a good punch of sourness. Umami comes naturally from tomatoes, and the stock. If more is needed, you can add soy sauce.

The five tastes comes from

  • Salty: The stock contains a decent amount of salt. Adding more to the dish might not be necessary. If you want to add some, try adding a dash of soy sauce, as this adds umami and gives a more round experience than just plain salt.
  • Sweet: Soft sweetness comes from the tomatoes and sweet potatoes. When you sprinkle the lime on top of the dish, you add some more sweet and sour highlights.
  • Sour: The lime is our primary driver for sourness. As we do not add the juice into the sauce, each person can decide how much juice they add on top of the dish.
  • Umami: Mainly comes from the stock and the Maillard reaction; Add more through soy sauce.
  • Bitter: Not much bitter is present, and that’s good in this case.

Aromas

This chili deserves a broad range of aromatics to please your senses. Looking at the aromatic world map, we can make some safe picks from the category of Mexico. Those fit together very well. We can pick some high, mid, and low tones from here.

Overall, the focus on this savory dish should be put on the low tones. From low to bright, we got:

  • Cumin: Cumin is our low base. We can add plenty of it to give the general direction of where we want to go.
  • Paprika: We add a dash of paprika to the dish to add more complexity in the lows. Cumin and paprika are far enough away from each other to be able distinguishable. We add a little paprika for more complexity.
  • Dried oregano: Further up on the scale, I would place oregano on the low to mid-range. It’s excellent to close the gap to mid-bright tones.
  • Garlic: garlic perfectly sits on top of the low tones and builds a bridge between the midrange and bright aromas.
  • Lime: while the sour taste of lime is strong, its aromas in the juice deliver a relatively soft profile. To intensify the lime aromatics, try grating some peel into the dish. The peel contains oils that dissolve most of the aromatic compounds. If you want to do that, buy organic and adequately wash the peel to avoid pesticides in your dish.
  • Cilantro: We can add cilantro to the dish at the very high end of the scale. If you don’t like cilantro, do not worry! You can skip or replace it. The scale below is densely packed; you won’t create a gap if you cut it.

Spice spice spice.

When it comes to spice, we have plenty of options to choose from. Spicy ingredients, particularly when fresh, deliver more than just capsaicin - they come with their own taste and aromas.

As we previously learned, each spicy ingredient can add more or less fire to the plate. From a slight tingle to an intense burn, we add the following.

  • Jalapeños: If you can, try adding fresh jalapeños. If you cannot find them in your local stores, canned and sliced jalapeños will do the trick here, too. In both cases, they onboard another level of fruitiness and bright aroma to the dish. Consider this when building up the aromatic scale of this dish.
  • Chili flakes: I picked them up in my local Asia store. They add a medium burn to the dish and can sum up to a solid burn if you add plenty. As this is dried chili, we should ensure the dish contains some oil (it does!) and give it enough time to unfold its profile fully.
  • Cayenne pepper: This powder is strong and should be handled carefully. A quarter teaspoon is a good amount to start with. The brave ones can add more or find other chiles and peppers with more hellfire. Cayenne delivers a soft aromatic profile with some smokey notes.

If you are inexperienced with spice, reduce the kick by skipping the more pungent ingredients. After adding them, give them 3-4 minutes to develop properly. Then, taste it and decide if you need more.

Cooking spicy for mixed audiences

When serving spicy dishes to other people, you should be extra cautious while handling spice. Some may not be able to handle the burn very well, while others crave more. Especially when cooking for kids, the dish should be spice-free. You can make a delicious chili without spice if you satisfy your tastebuds and your nose.

To allow everybody to fine-tune the spice to their needs, separate a small part of the stew and add the burn there. When ready, scoop back as much spice onto your plate as needed. In this way, you can serve a spice-free experience and hellfire at the same time.

It should be added to the stock relatively early to give the spice enough time to develop. Ideally, your spicy stock should be primarily liquid, so it is easier to incorporate it into the main plate when serving.

The ideal time to separate the sauce is after adding tomato, stock, and aromatics to your pot, and before adding more firm ingredients like beans and potato. Stir well to evenly combine everything before you take out a couple of scoops for the extra spice.

Add all spicy ingredients into the sauce and bring it to a soft boil on low to medium heat. Stir frequently to avoid burning the sauce. While the main pot is now simmering to soften the potatoes, the spice pot now has 25 minutes to develop.

Easy ways to change this recipe

If I had to define the essence of chili, it is a thick stew based on a spicy tomato sauce and beans, packed with plenty of fruity aromas. It's a versatile dish that allows for various ingredient choices. From the type of beans to the selection of vegetables, spices, and even the addition of protein like tofu, tempeh, or sautéed mushrooms, you can tailor your chili to suit your preferences and dietary needs.

Here are some ways to bring variation into this recipe to get you started. Apply each change individually, or make them happen altogether. It will be a good chili.

Swapping side dishes

Try cooking some rice to accompany your chili. It can help handle the spice. Or you serve chili together with nachos. Make sure to chop the potatoes into smaller pieces so you can pick them with the nachos easily.

Going meaty

If you fancy the taste of meat, you can add around 10 oz or 300g of minced beef to the chili. Alternatively, add some chicken strips or, if you like, plant-based meat.

Following this recipe, add it to the pot after frying the onions. Fry and stir occasionally until the good old Maillard reaction kicks in, adding a brown color to the surface. Then continue adding further ingredients.

Don’t like cilantro?

If you cannot stand the taste of cilantro, do not worry! There are plenty of alternative fresh herbs that fit splendidly to your chili. For a bright, oniony taste, try chopping some scallions or chives. Basil works perfectly here. Parsley also fits this mix pretty well.

More variety in vegetables?

Of course, you can add more types of vegetables. Try out bell peppers, courgette/zucchini chunks, mushrooms, and more. Carrots as well make a great addition.

Is more umami needed?

Also, this can be fixed. First, if you add meat, you also add umami. You can reach the same effect by tossing some mushrooms into the mix before adding the saucy parts. If it’s just about the taste, try picking one of the following:

  • More soy sauce instead of broth: like this, you can add more umami without adding new ingredients to the list.
  • Tomato concentrate paste: Concentrated tomato is quite intense in its taste. Adding a spoonful of tomato paste will enrich your dish with a fruity mix of umami, sweet and sour.

More protein?

With its base ingredients, this dish has a relatively low protein content. But do not fret; we also can change this. The classic go-to answer would be preparing a chili con carne. There are vegetarian options too. Try crumbling a block of tofu to replace ground meats. Pat the block dry and then squeeze it into small crumbles using your hands or a fork.

You can also toss in more legumes, like green peas. They fit into the mix just fine. You can also prepare dried soy protein to support the meaty experience while adding a decent amount of protein.

Wrapping it up

Once you get a grip on the basic principles behind this recipe, you can easily make it yours. There are many possibilities to twist it into your perfect chili experience. So let’s get cooking!

Black beans & sweet potato chili

Prep time

15 mins

Total time

45 mins

Servings

4 Plates

Course

Main

Cuisine

Mexican, Tex/Mex

Calories

506 kcal

Equipment

Duch oven, big pot or wok

Ingredients

Base ingredients

1 onion (diced)

3 garlic cloves (minced)

1 can black beans (rinsed)

14 oz / 400g tomatoes (diced)

2 cups / 500ml broth

1 sweet potato (12oz / ~350g , diced)

1 avocado (diced)

1 whole grain baguette

Aromatics

2 tsp cumin

1 tsp sweet paprika

1 tsp dried oregano

fresh cilantro

lime wedges to taste

Spice

10 slices jalapeños (canned)

2 tsp chili flakes

¼ tsp cayenne pepper

Instructions

  1. Heat the olive oil in a Dutch oven, large pot, or wok over medium-high heat. Add the diced onion and sauté until soft and translucent, about 5 minutes.
  2. Add the tomatoes, minced garlic, broth, cumin, paprika, and dried oregano to the pot and mix well.
  3. If you want to add spice separately, take out one-quarter of the food and put it in a small pot. Add the jalapeños, cayenne pepper, and chili flakes and heat individually on low to medium heat.
    Otherwise, add the spice directly into the main pot.
  4. Add the drained and rinsed black beans and diced sweet potato to the pot and stir to combine.
  5. Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce heat to low and let simmer for about 20-25 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the sweet potato is tender and the chili has thickened. Add salt /soy sauce (salty + umami) and pepper to taste.
  6. Serve the chili hot with diced avocado and chopped cilantro. Decorate with a lime wedge to add the final touch of acidity to the dish.
