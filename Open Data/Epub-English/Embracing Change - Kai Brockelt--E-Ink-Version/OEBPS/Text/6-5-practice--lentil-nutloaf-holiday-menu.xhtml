Between pragmatic and planned: balancing time management

In the kitchen, time is usually your rival. As we cook, we encounter many deadlines that must be managed correctly. When lacking attention, things can burn, overcook, delay, or be delayed. The further you up your cooking game, the more critical time management becomes.

Here, we aim to create a menu to find exactly this challenge and master it. Crafting a holiday menu is a challenging task repeating every year, so it’s perfect to use this to go into the profound topic of time management.

Crafting all of this alone requires you to take care of multiple things:

  • Preparing the nut loaf: This requires chopping, stir-frying, combining, and baking of ingredients.
  • Making a gravy: The basic flow is chopping ingredients, sautéeing in the pan, filling up the sauce with liquid, and finally simmering.
  • Making baked potatoes: You need to wash and cut the potatoes, season them, and finally roast them in the oven.
  • Making a dip requires chopping, juicing, combining, and blending.
  • Steaming broccoli: It’s no rocket science, but hard work chopping and steaming are todos that ask for your time and attention, too.

Tackling all these tasks at once can become overwhelming. Here’s where time management and some planning come in handy.

Optimizing time management

When things get messy, time can quickly become a critical factor. Proper planning and execution can save you tremendous time and nerve if done right.

We can start by understanding how a professional kitchen typically self-organizes itself:

In a restaurant, everything is designed to serve multiple dishes from the same order simultaneously, ideally. But how is that done? The keyword is parallelization!

Big kitchens are organized in “sections,” often aligned to preparation methods. You could encounter a baking section, a banquet section for steaming or roasting, a bar and drinks section, etc.

Each section is responsible for various tasks that can be executed quickly and repeatedly. When preparing an order, a plate might pass multiple sections to completeness. If you know how long each step takes roughly, you can start doing the math to find out when to start which dish so that all dishes are ready simultaneously. This calculation typically happens with the help of a computer system, but it’s nothing too tricky to do on a piece of paper.

While multiple employees take care of the stations in a big kitchen, your kitchen space is limited. Nevertheless, approaching parallelization is critical to managing time.

Let’s assume you work all alone and look at how a single person can apply logic and common sense to be better organized.

Classifying the complexity of the to-dos

Let’s first look at each step you do and how much of your attention it requires. Try to organize your steps by the degree of attention they need:

If you think of your cooking steps as active, attentive, or passive, you can spot steps you can let happen in parallel.

  • Active cooking steps cannot be parallelized. However, you can cook actively while a passive cooking process is rolling. With experience, you can also parallelize this with attentive cooking.
  • Attentive cooking steps can be parallelized to some degree: You can handle multiple pots simultaneously. While one pot boils pasta, there might be a hot pan frying while you also have a sauce simmering. Each pot wants some of your attention, but handling multiple of those in parallel is easy. However, moving between active and attentive tasks can provoke mistakes, as your attention span gets high demand.
  • Passive cooking steps can run in the background: Theoretically, a passive step only requires you to get active twice - To start and end it. If the start and end do not conflict with any other task, you can parallelize these steps freely.

Planning the cooking flow from the end

If you know the steps needed to create a dish and how much attention each step might require, you can start organizing the preparation steps so they all come together.

In our case, we have multiple dishes with multiple steps. If, for example, you make the loaf, there will be chopping and cutting (active cooking), stir-frying (attentive cooking), and baking time (passive cooking). In this example, all steps are required to be executed in a sequence.

We can organize the tasks in a timeline and qualify the attention needed for each step. For each step, draw a box, with the width indicating their time requirement. You can color them differently for the attention required: Red: active cooking, Yellow: attentive cooking, and Green: passive cooking.

As another dimension, you can estimate the helping hands. You can do double the work at the same time if you cook in a pair. This allows the running of two active processes simultaneously.

  • Make an inventory of all todos and qualify them. How long this process might take (time defines how wide you draw the box). How demanding this process is (attention defines the box's color).
  • Place all dishes in parallel on your timeline. This shows the theoretical optimal time that you can reach at full parallelization. You can achieve this by having one chef assigned to one flow each, but not always when cooking alone. This helps spotting time conflicts of tasks asking for a good share of attention that run in parallel.
  • Spread out the tasks to have realistic parallelization and see how you can distribute the tasks to avoid conflicting tasks overlapping. This will lead to a more realistic but somewhat pessimistic timeline that we can optimize later. Active tasks (red) can only exist once per chef at a given time. You can put passive tasks in parallel with passive tasks (green).Try paralleling/stacking attentive tasks that you can handle simultaneously. (Yellow). Focus on filling the passive time from baking, resting, etc, with more active tasks.

If we could do everything in parallel, we would require a total cooking time of 65 minutes. This assumes perfect parallelization and, for a home scenario, is unrealistic. But it is a reasonable estimate that tells us the minimum time we have to spend on one dish.

While an experienced chef should be able to handle these steps in parallel (no reds overlap), it is obvious that things can become stressful quickly. Considering the number of tasks involved, it becomes clear that certain things must go in sequence, mainly “active cooking” tasks.

This leads us to a pessimistic but more realistic view of the time required. You can assume this time if you work on this independently while mindlessly following instructions. According to this timeline, 65 minutes become 90. In this timeframe, you should be able to manage everything on your own.

Saving time through anticipation and synergies

The biggest time killer, most probably, is being unorganized. And you can tackle that through anticipation. Applied correctly, this saves time by combining similar tasks and synergizing the preparation of multiple dishes containing the same steps.

Let’s go by the example of chopping and preparation:

Most dishes require food to be prepared with a knife. While the time needed for chopping depends on your skill or equipment, the process is as efficient as possible when you don’t interrupt it. This means you lose time if you chop one item and then run to the fridge to get the next one. If you finish chopping and then search for a container before you can take care of the next step, you again lose time. You lose time if you need a spice from your rack, but first, you must browse and lift ten other spices before you find it.

Each time you interrupt your activity to pick some item, you slow down cooking. Anticipation and preparation can minimize that. So get prepared for each step logically to be most efficient:

  • Start by gathering all the ingredients you want to process, enough containers to store them in, and a bin to put your waste in on the fly.
  • Kickstart things you have to wait for anyway before you move to active cooking steps. I talk about things like preheating the oven, boiling water in a kettle, heating pans, etc…
  • Throw together similar tasks from different dishes/components. Especially in chopping, doing all dishes together avoids unnecessary interruptions. If planned properly, the preparation time can be reduced far below the sum of each dish. How much depends on your degree of efficiency and organization. But Synergies can save up to 50% of total time or more if your general working speed is efficient.

Assuming our working space is ample enough to do all the chopping and preparation steps in one go, we can save significant time by avoiding back and forth between the preparation of various components. It is wise to estimate your skill level and working speed to highlight potential areas of savings:

I am a chaotic person with decently trained chopping and preparation skills. Hence, my most significant time savings are fixing the chaos and working preparedly. We can be intentional about this and use it to guess the time saved. This reduces my preparation and active cooking time to 15 minutes, leading to a total preparation time of 70 minutes.

Remember that this is my estimate based on my cooking experience. I recommend you play through the planning once in your head and adapt the times to your skill level to produce the most realistic estimate.

Making use of free time

Looking at the optimized timeline, we can see time windows where no activity or attention is required. Everything that is supposed to roast is in the oven, and the other steps need only a little share of that free time.

These time windows are perfect opportunities to execute supportive tasks in your kitchen. In professional kitchens, chefs often use “downtime” efficiently to complete general tasks that do not aim to prepare a dish directly but enable clean and efficient working for when the next order comes in. Identify tasks that can be done during downtime, such as cleaning, organizing, or preparing for the following steps:

  • Prepare and follow checklists: The core habit of anticipation is planning. Think logically about the next steps and what you require. If the situation gets messy, you might profit tremendously by externalizing your planning on paper.
  • “Mis en place”: Professional kitchens emphasize "mis en place," which means having everything in its place. This includes ingredients, equipment, and utensils. Use free time to prepare the next steps for straightforward execution. This can be part of a checklist.
  • “Clean as you go”: A tidy workplace enables efficient working. The more messy your workplace becomes over time, the less freely you can focus on the tasks. Think of this as the counterpart of mis en place. “Mis en place” takes care of everything you need to be present, and “clean as you go” ensures you only see what you need.

Plan ahead

Many menus have components that don’t require fresh preparation. Cold ingredients like dips, sauces, salads, etc., are less time-critical in preparation, as their appeal does not depend on hot serving.

If you plan a complex menu, try taking out steps from the time calculation when they are not time-critical. You can prepare them the night before or earlier the same day to reduce the overall complexity of your main cooking session.

Following this menu plan, you can safely prepare the cold dip the night before. That is one thing less to take care of in parallel with other steps. Just ensure proper storing to preserve freshness.

You can furthermore save time by pre-chopping things that stay fresh overnight. Think of chopping the broccoli, onion, mushrooms… Get them ready and store them in an airtight container. Once you get cooking, you just need to grab them and are ready to rumble.

You can apply the same logic to batch cooking: If, for example, you have multiple rice dishes to cook, there is no reason to cook rice twice. Do the job once in a batch to be most efficient here.

Be realistic

In theory, you can drive all these factors to perfection. That works if you have the time, space, and money available. Realistically, your kitchen space is limited, and you need to work with what you have. Try to match the approach with what you have at hand.

If you have limited working space, you can only prepare as far as the space allows. Swapping containers or ingredients might be needed on the go. Consider the best practices with the limits of reality to see which organizational strategy can help you realistically and which are only nice ideas that distract your flow.

Avoid emergencies in time management.

Following the good old “Murphy’s law,” we know mistakes will happen; it’s just a matter of time.

We can plan with mistakes if we accept that they occur occasionally against our will. While we could focus on the severity of a mistake (a chopped-off finger certainly is more problematic than a burnt meal), let’s focus on how they affect time exclusively.

If you boil down mistakes to how they show up in the flow, we know each mistake costs us time and adds complexity in managing things in parallel. A mistake or emergency usually has the quality of an active task to prevent further damage from happening. This means that once it shows up, you want to act quickly on it. This can spiral down to more emergencies.

Let’s draw a pessimistic scenario: Your pasta starts over-boiling while you are frying something and making a sauce in parallel. Naturally, you will try to lift the lid and clean the mess in real-time, but as you move your attention away from your pan towards your pot, you open the door for another emergency. If everything goes wrong, taking care of the boiling pasta might lead to searing your stir-fried goods. You now have two emergencies to handle while the third is in the making (the sauce is about to burn).

The more fires you have to put out, the less you are in control, and the more likely things will spiral into a disaster. Keeping control through planning can help with disasters. Avoid putting too many time-critical tasks close to each other. If you commit to a mistake while in an isolated step, nothing but a slight overall delay will happen. When executing the same mistake in a wild multitasking session, one wrong step can spark a wildfire.

Pulling the handbrake before escalation

If you feel like things are spiraling out of control, you should step back from your plans and regain control before you move on. Pulling the handbrake costs time, but coming back on track without starting over is a meaningful solution-oriented measure.

Pulling the hand brake means stopping all ongoing activity as much as possible so you have room to reorganize anew. Turn off or down your oven and stove, pause the microwave, and stop chopping. In most cases, a halted process can be resumed with no problem.

This allows you to clean up the mess and widen the organizational bottleneck. Return to full heat and speed once the main problem is resolved.

Organizing chaos: how to approach big jobs in a small kitchen

Looking back at the timelines, you can see my concluded time planning. But you cannot see why or how I came to these conclusions. Let’s put some light into that and look at how time-efficient cooking can happen here.

My assumed kitchen surface is relatively small, not allowing me to prepare all the chopping in one go. Nevertheless, the proper organization allows an efficient flow.

The working area should be wide enough to host one cutting board, surrounded by ingredients on one side and free containers on the other. Trash stays close to the working area. Anything that does not fit in this space waits to be used in a second free surface - the “hot swap” area.

Preparing everything for the chopping session sitting close to you allows you to move from one thing to another quickly. Instead of searching for the next ingredient, you can turn around and grab it. Hence the term “hot swap”.

If you look closely at the final timeline, you see that the chopping of the potatoes happens separately. This is done for two reasons:

  • To avoid oxidation or soaking of the potato: Cut potatoes turn ugly quickly when in contact with air. We can use a later time window to promptly cut the potato and move it to the oven before browning can happen.
  • To save more time through parallelization: There is a big window where nothing happens except for baking. That’s an excellent opportunity to save time by pulling active tasks here. This shortens the overall cooking time.

If you look even closer, you might find further potential to optimize the overall process, pushing closer to record time. But be considerate about stress levels. A bit of free time in between is wanted. This is a buffer to breathe. To let things take a bit longer. For bio breaks.

Remember that perfect never wins, but it can ruin everything in one go. Efficiency is not everything. Enjoyment should be the priority in the first place. Please keep that in mind.

The nut and lentil loaf

The loaf is the center of attention of this dish. The most aromatic and textural play can be found here, no doubt. The recipe aims to create a warming, meaty experience, as you expect from holiday meatloaves while benefitting from a whole foods ingredient profile as much as possible. We aim to do fat swapping and fat reduction, leading to a less caloric composition than its traditional counterpart.

Base ingredients, texture, and taste

With the main ingredients, we aim to cover two main goals:

  • We want that meat-like experience, covering texture, taste, and color.
  • We want to make sure to eat a healthy composition of food groups.

For the meat-like experience, we should focus on creating enough umami on the one side and an authentic meat-caky texture on the other. While finding umami is easy through mushrooms and soy sauce, getting the texture right depends on multiple factors. But also color and visual perception play a role:

A meat pie or loaf is primarily brown but has some red tint remaining. This plays a big part in visual perception and expectation. If I think of a meatloaf from ground meat, its texture is mostly even, but with some variations in bite. It should be slightly crumbly, and you do not necessarily need a knife to break it apart despite being chewy and spongy.

We can cover this with whole food ingredients. Lentils and oats serve as an excellent base for the dough. You get a complete protein, and the texture is fragile enough to create a lot of variation in bite when combined. Finely chopped mushrooms can imitate the spongy and chewy parts. I add shredded carrots to mimic the feel of soft fibers in the mouth.

To add a more grainy structure, ground hazelnuts and walnuts perfectly fit the ensemble.

The base color of this mix already turns out quite brown when baked. We can add red highlights through tomato paste and finely chopped fresh beetroot.

We can count on our repertoire of standard answers for extra umami: a generous dash of soy sauce and two spoons of nutritional yeast cover everything we could ask for.

As a special highlight, I add a small amount of dried cranberries. This creates a pleasant, sweet, and fruity surprise that shows up on every third bite or so and changes the overall taste perception when that happens. Nice!

Aroma

First I add garlic and onion to the loaf. Roasted onion belongs in a loaf! There is no way around it! And the garlic gives that welcoming, warm touch to everything – perfect for a cold season dish!

Now, let's turn our attention to the aromatic scale.

The loaf is focused on low aromas and barely reaches the bright zone. This is intended, as we reserve that space for the gravy. We can fill this space with thyme (ideally fresh!), bay leaf, nutmeg, and oregano. Other spices that would fit the mix could be paprika, rosemary, or cumin. But we want to fill the space effectively. If you wish to add one or more, I recommend swapping them for another.

Binding

With the starch from the oats, we should have enough binding power in place. This, however, only accounts for a cake scenario when everything is smooth. We aim for variation in bite and require some uneven textures. To play safe, let’s add some ground flax.

How well the dough binds depends mainly on the glue-to-weight ratio. In our case, starches are the glue and are supposed to hold the weight of the bits together. The effectiveness of binding, therefore, strongly depends on how large the bits you cut from mushrooms, onions, and beetroot are. The finer you chop, the more surface area is available for the starch to glue to.

To blend or not to blend?

Before turning on your food processor, test how the dough behaves. If you see it sticks and does not crumble easily, you may bake a firm and cuttable loaf. In this case, blending would reduce texture variety, but not much more. Blending is one way to increase the inside surface without tossing more binders if you are unsure if it will stick.

The idea is to blend carefully. Larger chunks break down in size quickly at low speed. If you blend too fast or long, you will bake a savory cake, not a loaf, as we imagine.

A good strategy is to blend parts of the dough; between one quarter and half. Then, it does not matter if it turns out smooth; combined with the rest, it will hide in the overall experience.

Balsamico and apple gravy

This gravy is meant to complement the loaf directly. A contrasting focus on sweet and sour taste, combined with bright aromas, is there to counterbalance the weight of the loaf. We aim for a simple gravy from roasted onion with apple that can be extended with red wine. The fried mushrooms add the cherry on the cake for textural play.

Base ingredients, taste, and aroma

It does not require much to make this sauce. We aim to complement the loaf in both taste and aromas and want to focus on the bright space of the spectrum. Again, we create a solid aromatic base by applying the Maillard reaction to onions before filling the space with stock. As we only fry onion, the outcome will focus sharply on the balsamic vinegar and apple.

The focus on taste combines sweet, sour, and umami. We create a sweet and sour emphasis through apple and vinegar. Red wine adds tannins for bitterness. The creamy texture complements crusty bits and blends the experience in one bite.

Try picking quality vinegar here, as cheap balsamico usually delivers weak aromas. The apple should be sweet.

The way you cut makes a big difference here.

Again, we face the topic of surface area. With the onions, we create aromas through roasting. The finer you cut the onion stripes, the more surface will touch the hot pan, ultimately delivering a more potent aromatic punch.

A similar scenario opens up with the apple. If you dice it finely, it will give more juice into the gravy directly. The result is generally sweeter and more fruity. Bigger apple chunks tend to hold juice in the core. This will be released when bitten, resulting in a refreshing splash of apple juice. If you like to emphasize both, add more apples.

Baked Potatoes

For a decent side, we will take it simple. We widen the variety of this plate by adding crunchy baked potatoes. Their texture adds more play to the overall dish. The salty focus in taste plays well with the loaf and gravy. However, the potatoes will get their creamy companion, too!

As we bake at 180° C / 356° F, we can safely use quality olive oil for these baked potatoes. In terms of aroma, rosemary will do. The rest comes through baking.

Tofu cream dip

The cream cheese tofu dip is a cold counterpart to the crispy potatoes. It complements the potatoes with a sour smack from lemon, earthy tones from tahini, and a refreshing onion spice from the chives. If you add nutritional yeast, you add a cheesy note full of umami.

By making this dip from tofu and sesame instead of condensed dairy, we changed the fat profile towards unsaturated while onboarding high amounts of protein. The more tahini you use, the creamier and nuttier the dip.

You can create a firm cream cheese like Philadelphia or a more runny sauce with these basic ingredients. While blending, add water gradually until you find the desired consistency.

Summing it up

Overall, we play two games of full taste, each with a variation in texture, temperature, and aromatic profile.

The nut loaf and gravy form a contrasting ensemble between crumbly and creamy textures and warming and refreshing aromas while staying in the classic appeal of typical holiday roasts.

The potato and dip combination repeats the whole taste experience. You got a decent Maillard browning on the potatoes serving umami; you can find salt in both components. Lemon provides sourness and brightness, tahini drives bitterness, and chives give a touch of onion spiciness.

And, well, the broccoli… It’s just broccoli. It cannot contribute to full taste anymore. Its aromas are decent and stay in the background. But still, adding it uplifts the game: it adds a crisp bite to the ensemble, which we otherwise would miss. If you find other vegetables more attractive, replace it!

Doing the menu step-by-step

0 Preparing the tofu dip

The tofu cream will be served cold; we can prepare it long before everything else. Doing so in advance will make the actual cooking session shorter and less complex.

Preparing the dip is as easy as blending things. Crumble the tofu, squeeze the juice of a half lemon, and blend with tahini, olive oil, and salt. Add water gradually until you reach the desired consistency.

Chop a handful of chives and combine them into the dip.

1 Chopping session

Preparation mainly consists of chopping and cutting ingredients. For the loaf, we aim to cut tiny pieces for better binding. Apply a fine cut to the onion, beets, and mushrooms for the loaf, grate the carrot, and grind the nuts and flax seeds. Boil and rinse the lentils as needed.

The gravy is supposed to contain bits of mushrooms, onion and apple. I recommend cutting the onion in fine stripes to provide as much surface as possible for roasting.

In terms of apple and mushrooms, it depends on what you prefer. A finer cut on the apple releases more juice into the sauce, making it fruitier.

With all items cut and prepared, we are ready to move on.

2 Cooking the dough for the nut loaf

Preheat the oven to 180°C / 350°F.

Heat oil in a large pan and fry the onions until transparent. Toss mushrooms, beets, and carrots and continue frying for three more minutes. Add all spices afterward.

Lower the heat and add lentils, oats, nutmeal, soy sauce, tomato paste, and nutritional yeast to the mix. Combine everything until even. If the dough is not sticky enough, blend a small part and combine it with the rest.

Prepare a baking mold with baking paper and transfer the dough into the mold. Use a spoon to properly squeeze the dough into all corners and fill all potential gaps. Smooth out the surface with the help of a spoon. Put the loaf on a lower tray in the oven and bake for 45 minutes.

3 Preparing the potatoes

While the loaf is in the oven, cut the potatoes into wedges. Cut slim wedges to ensure the potato will be well done on the inside after 25 minutes of baking. Cover the potatoes in oil and add rosemary and salt.

Move to a baking tray and distribute the wedges so they can evenly brown from all sides. Bake on the upper tray for around 25 minutes or until turning brown.

4 Creating the gravy

Preheat a pan to medium heat. Add oil and roast the onions until browning occurs. Add mushrooms and apple bits and continue sautéing for another 3 minutes. Add stock, soy sauce, vinegar, and wine. Add 1-2 tablespoons of cornstarch for thickening, and let the sauce simmer until everything else is ready.

5 Bringing everything together

The only thing left to do to this point is steaming the broccoli. Cut it into florets and steam for around 10 minutes if you haven't already. If the timing works out right, everything should be ready simultaneously and can be served together hot.

The dinner menu is ready to be plated.

Variations

As always, this is just the start of what you can do. Here’s some inspiration for you:

Deciding on the wine

The gravy asks for a punch of wine. The addition of bitter is excellent to round up the ensemble while contributing fruity aromas. However, as you learned, tannins interfere with the absorption of your minerals.

You must decide whether you want the floury bouquet or the more health-focused approach and use water or juices to fill the liquid.

I argue that on holidays, most people will complement a dinner like this with red wine anyway, so protectionism is not doing a good job here. Therefore, I would opt for pleasure. But in the end, it’s up to you and your loved ones to decide what’s best.

Playing with ingredients:

Especially the loaf and gravy invite doing this, as there is so much room for creativity in taste, texture, and aroma! Try adding pumpkin to the loaf—swap walnuts and hazelnuts, or go for different protein sources.

Or how about making an orange sauce instead of an apple vinegar gravy?

Deconstructing and repurposing components:

  • Develop the dip further: Utilize the cream cheese tofu dip as a flavorful spread for crackers or sandwiches. Experiment with different spice blends to create distinct spreads. Think also about color and try to give aroma and appeal a matching direction.
  • Stand-alone Potato Bites: Instead of baking large potato wedges, cut them into bite-sized pieces and roast them for a quick, crispy snack. Serve with the dip for a delightful appetizer as a stand-alone snack.
  • Gravy for everything: this gravy works well with other holiday dishes. But also, different gravy sauces fit the nut loaf perfectly. Try creating one from roasted garden vegetables or scrap vegetables.

Applying learned concepts to new ideas:

You can roll the dice and find new combinations with all the theories onboarded. You know the dimensions to look into. You can change the shape and appeal and the type of dish altogether. You can take taste combinations or aromatic scales from one dish and apply them purposefully to something new. You can intentionally use small or big modifications to tell your story. You just need to embrace the change.

Lentil Nut Loaf

Prep time

20 mins

Total time

65 mins

Servings

8 big slices

Course

Main

Cuisine

Western

Calories

320 kcal

Equipment

Pan, Oven, Mold

Ingredients

Base Ingredients

400g / 14oz lentils, cooked

100g / 3.5oz walnuts, powdered

100g 3/5 hazelnuts, powdered 150g / 5 oz mushrooms

one small onion

2 tbsp nutritional yeast, optional

100g / 3.5oz oats

carrot, grated (~ 80-100g / 3-4oz)

2 tbsp tomato paste

1 tbsp ground flax

2 garlic cloves

one small beetroot

50g / 1.75oz cranberries

Aromas & Spice

thyme (fresh)

bay leaf

3 tsp oregano

½ tsp nutmeg

salt

pepper

For Frying

oil

Instructions

  1. Preheat the oven to 180°C / 350°F, and cook lentils if needed. Blend the nuts into a powder.
  2. Finely chop the onion, beetroot, and mushrooms, and grate the carrot.
  3. Heat oil over medium heat in a pan and add the onions. Fry until browning starts. Then, toss mushrooms and beetroot into the pan. Add all spices.
  4. Lower the heat and add the remaining ingredients. Combine properly.
  5. Optional: if the dough is not sticky enough, take one part of the dough and blend it. Combine the dough again.
  6. Transfer into a baking mold and press it into the corners properly. Distribute evenly and smooth the surface with the help of a spoon.
  7. Bake the loaf for 45 minutes on the low tray of your oven.

5 mins

Total time

25 mins

8 Scoops

Course

Sauce

Cuisine

Equipment

Pot

Ingredients

one small onion

one small apple, sweet

150g mushrooms

2 tsp veggie stock powder

Instructions

  1. Slice the onion finely, and dice the apple and mushrooms.
  2. Fry the onion until brown.
  3. Add the diced apple, and mushrooms, and stir for around 1 minute.

  7. Add cornstarch for binding. Keep warm until served.

88 kcal

Equipment

Oven

Ingredients

1kg / 2lbs potatoes, small

olive oil

rosemary

salt

Instructions

  1. Preheat the oven to 180° C / 350° F, cut small potato wedges.
  2. Put the potatoes into a bowl; add oil and rosemary.
  3. Move the potatoes onto a baking tray and distribute them evenly.
  4. Bake for around 25 minutes on the upper tray, or until golden.

Creamy Tofu Dip

Prep time

5 mins

Total time

5 mins

Servings

8 Scoops

Course

Dip

Cuisine

Western

Calories

41 kcal

Equipment

Food Procesor

Ingredients

Calories and Protein

200g tofu, firm

2 tbsp tahini

½ lemon

1 tbsp olive oil

salt

water

Instructions

  1. Blend the tofu, tahini, lemon juice and salt.
  2. Continue blending and add water progressively, until the cream reaches the desired consistency.
  3. Chop chives and combine them with the dip. Store in the fridge until ready to be served.
