3.1 Mind:

Energy and Body Weight

Let’s talk about body weight and dieting.

A good body feeling comes from having every nutrient you need and is also impacted by sleep, sport, and self-perception. Psychology plays a big part and is responsible if you don’t feel comfortable with your body size.

The most commonly used indicator for measuring healthy body weight is the body weight and the weight-to-height ratio, called the “Body Mass Index” BMI.

Weight and BMI, however, only are indicators and don’t apply to every body type the same way. Using it to guess your caloric needs might mislead you: If you are overweight, it can lead you to calculate higher requirements than you might have. If you have much muscle, your BMI might tell you you are overweight, while you just are strong.

So it is important not to rely too much on that number and always to consider your lifestyle

Why is this important?

Dieting is not just simple math where you count calories. Our body has survival mechanisms that kick in when we run low on energy.

Understanding how our body reacts to a deficit in energy helps you put your agenda at a good pace. Whether you want to gain, lose or stay at your current weight and fitness level, knowing the long-term effects of your decisions helps you in your decision-making and archiving your goals.

Calories in, calories out?

The fundamental rule about changing your body weight is as simple as subtracting the calories you need from the calories you eat. If you need more than you supply, your body weight goes down; if you get more than you need, naturally, you will build up body fat.

No matter what diet trend or “magic trick” gets used to promise weight loss, it usually aims for a reduction in calories. Keto, for example, relies on the satiating effects of protein and fats; low-fat diets try to avoid those 9kcal per g as you find them in oils. Intermittent fasting shortens the eating window so that satiety won’t allow us to eat the “normal” amount as quickly. Certain dietary patterns may advertise more health benefits, but if it is about weight loss, this all falls back to a calorie deficit.

One calorie does not always equal one calorie.

The calories-in-calories-out rule follows the law of thermodynamics. So any calories you take in need to be burned somehow. But on the way “in,” the quality of your food has a say on how much energy effectively will be available for you to use.

Processed foods typically have higher caloric absorption rates because they are often stripped of fiber and other nutrients that slow digestion and make you feel full. That means your body needs less time and effort to make the energy available. On whole foods you chew more, your stomach produces more acid, and your bowel causes more rhythmic muscular contractions to move the chymus.

Consequently, a calorie from a highly processed product will effectively provide more energy than a calorie served through a natural and complex food, as your body needs to invest less to absorb it. All in all, focusing on whole foods is an excellent way of lowering your calorie intake. Fiber and minerals make absorption slower, and the complexity makes your body invest more. Considering these effects allows you to open a caloric gap without restricting the amount of food eaten. Even if the label indicates the same energetic impact, you have fewer calories available when going whole.

Opening the caloric gap the right way

One kilo of body fat roughly accounts for 7000kcal of energy. Unlike pure oil, body fat is still cellular and contains water and many other components. So it is expected that instead of 9000kcal per kilo of pure fat, you need to save less to eliminate that kilo in body fat.

While theoretically, it would be possible to drop a kilo of fat in around 2-3 days, in reality, your body will cross out that plan for you. With lower energy levels and lower blood sugar, your body will start burning more protein to maintain glucose homeostasis, blood sugar levels in the healthy range.

Why is that so? You need to have a certain amount of energy available to function. The burning of fat is a comparably slow process. After all, ketosis is a fallback mode if we lack carbs. If the energy produced from ketosis is insufficient to ensure a healthy energy level, your body must find a way to fill the gap and metabolize protein.

As a consequence, you would lose muscle instead of body fat. Ultimately, this will lower your basal metabolic rate or caloric needs. Where there is no more muscle to maintain, energy can be saved. Undercutting drastically will reduce your caloric need, and your caloric gap closes again. Opening up a new gap on an already lower caloric need is even more complicated.

On top of that, you naturally risk undernutrition as you avoid foods you might need for a proper nutrient supply.

A good approach is to align your diet step by step toward your caloric goal. Try not to undercut your daily caloric need by more than 15%, as this will signal your body to sacrifice more muscle and start bending the CICO (calories in, calories out) rule.

Changing the quality of your foods from processed to more natural can already open a gap big enough to start losing weight. Satiety from fiber, water contents (volume), and minerals (chemical signals) comes faster than satiety from pure refined extracts.

How to know how much energy you need

If you want to go down the rabbit hole of calorie counting, please be considerate. Again, replacing junk with proper foods often makes a deficit big enough to reduce caloric intake. Especially on a plant-based diet, you tend to need higher volumes of food to hit the same caloric goal. Getting the grip of that is way easier than day-to-day calorie counting and restriction.

You can still make use of the BMI to estimate your caloric need. If you are overweight, calculate a BMI from the “normal” range (18.5 to 24.9) for your height. If you hit higher weight through muscle, use the number calculated.

You can use your height and the body weight of a normal BMI to calculate your so-called basal metabolic rate (BMR). The result mainly indicates how much energy you need to stay alive.

Women: BMR = 447.593 + (9.247 x weight in kg) + (3.098 x height in cm) – (4.330 x age in years)

Men: BMR = 88.362 + (13.397 x weight in kg) + (4.799 x height in cm) – (5.677 x age in years)

With your BMR calculated, you need to adjust your calories according to your average activity level. If you consider yourself a couch potato who barely moves, multiply your BMR by 1.2; if you do moderate amounts of sports (~3 times per week) or generally are physically active at work or such, multiply by 1.5; if you are highly active daily, calculate with 2. Energy burnt directly during exercise can be added on top of that.

Since doing math is boring and error-prone, feel free to use an online calculator. But please consider that this calculation is an estimate, which cannot fully reflect every detail of your habits.

Track your habits for one week

To get a more precise and personalized estimate, you can to track what you eat during a week while comparing the change in your body weight. Try to eat your “normal” way without restrictions or special cake day occasions. Whatever the average for you counts.

Then compare your body weight. If your weight changed over a week, you might be in a deficit or excess. If there is no movement visible, you’re on the spot.

One kilo of body fat is worth approximately 7000 kcal. If, for example, your weight drops 350g in one week (or 70g daily), you already undercut 490 kcal in a day. (7 calories per gram of body fat x the change in grams).

Suppose you cut 490 kcal from a diet of 2500 kcal/day; that would represent a deficit of 19.6% - which is already quite a lot and potentially can cost you muscle if you don’t counter that with resistance training and growth signals for your muscles. Coming from a diet of 3500 kcal daily, this would still mean a loss of 14%, which is less agressive and is less likely to cost you muscle.

How to approach healthy body changes

Instead of battling restrictions and self-limitations, try onboarding smaller but persistent changes to your day-to-day lifestyle. Remember that you can control the CICO formula on both ends: burning more energy and restricting the supply.

Being more active can raise your daily need for calories and open a gap. Combined with a healthier choice of foods that deliver you natural satiety is an excellent way to go long-term. Whole foods are rich in fiber, minerals, and vitamins. They all support satiety without giving extra calories.

I recommend onboarding those changes gradually. If you force yourself into a program without feeling joy, it stays nothing but a program. Instead, we ask for a change in habits. Change one of your sweet items for a better version. If you can stick to it without problems, that’s a contributing change onboarded successfully.

If you’re new to sports, start with something small that you can endure. Each exercise still gives signals to grow muscle and burns energy directly. It doesn’t have to exhaust you to have an effect. Taking the stairs instead of the elevator, using the bike to work or school, or a 10-minute good morning routine… every small change counts.

Remember to aim for a small gap. Slow wins the race in the long run. You shouldn’t care too much about fast changes when striving to be healthy long-term. It doesn’t matter whether you drop 50g or 100g daily as long as you reach your goal ultimately.

Don’t take your scale as a reference.

Keep in mind that muscles are denser than body fat. 1.1g/ml versus 0.9g/ml, to be precise. A frequent look at your scale can easily be misleading if you assume any change visible to be fat-based.

If you start training, you might gain weight while still becoming lean and healthy. If you go on a strict diet, you might see more weight loss in muscle than overall body fat.

Having a weight goal in mind and doing everything to reach it may mislead you from your goal of being healthy. Listen to other factors too. Has your posture changed? Did you gain muscle? Do you generally feel more fit and fresh than before? Do your friends recognize any change? Does your waist circumference change? How’s your stamina doing? Are daily tasks more manageable?

These are all visible changes that will not necessarily be represented by the small number that your scale tells you. So be mindful of that. If you want to track, waist circumference is a way better measure.
