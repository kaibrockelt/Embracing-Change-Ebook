3.3 Habit:

Reading Shopping Labels

By now, you should have developed a decent understanding of what a healthy diet is. The more fresh and whole you choose over processed foods, the better your supply of essential nutrients. In a perfect world, you would be 100% buying whole natural foods with a decent variety across all available food groups.

Reality gives us a lot of packaged and processed foods, and it is not always possible (or practical) to go full fresh. Processed foods are often just part of our day-to-day, and there is no way around them.

This chapter looks into the world of packaging and processed foods. You will learn to read labels properly and find important information quickly, so you can better pick healthy products over unhealthy ones.

Why is this important?

When we buy food, we usually pick it based on our expectations of how rewarding (taste, texture) food might be and how beneficial (nutritious value) it is.

Processed food is designed to look good for us, so we buy it. The picture and packaging often look way better than what the actual product has to offer. If you understand how to find important information on the product label, you will be able to make more healthy picks within the world of processed foods.

This can positively impact your lifestyle by making healthier picks for similar products.

The psychology behind every food purchase

When we buy food, we usually pick it based on our expectations. We make a decision based on two aspects:

  • The emotional aspect: How rewarding/yummy is the food? (likability)
  • The intelligent aspect: How beneficial is the food? (nutritional value)

The expectation around the taste and texture of a given food is directly linked to our reward system. Both pictures and the smell of food can trigger appetite. We don’t even need to think about it for too long. If we glimpse food at the right moment, the craving for that item is high without any big consideration. When hungry, the satisfaction of eating stimulates your emotional reward system.

The information about whether a food is healthy is not emotional. If we pick food for its value, it is a rational, intelligent decision. This can be a fully informed move, but this can also be a wild guess or assumption.

The likability of food is usually the driver of our decision when we operate on auto-pilot. Nutritional value only gets important if we are actively mindful of it.

We are all exposed to marketing.

When dealing with processed foods, we don’t see the actual food and rely on what’s printed on the package. Picture and text usually aim for high likability by presenting a wonderfully made picture of something that represents the product. Most of the design work then goes into likability and emotional design.

There is no law saying that highly processed food cannot have an attractive design. There is also no law saying that the packaging cannot be more beautiful than the actual product. And then there is no law against using colors associated with health aspects (green, light blue etc.). And finally, there is no law against useless information on the package. But these things highly affect our perception and ideally should be ignored completely when making a purchase choice.

Priming

If any company processing foods need to sell their stuff (they all do), they better ensure the product gets bought. The last and most impactful touchpoint before the purchase is the packaging itself. Much marketing is happening directly on the product to influence that moment of choice.

One method found over and over again on food packaging is priming. The method of priming describes the creation of associations in our heads. For example, the words “nurse,” “doctor,” and “hospital” are closely connected, and if you add the term „pill“, it fits the context. If you think of „Party“, „Techno“ and „Rave“, the pill gets a different interepretation.

The same happens with packaging design. Terms like “organic,” “whole grain,” “low sugar,” “low fat,” “low sodium,” etc. are all strongly connected to healthy associations. Priming uses this association and tries to link a product to that term. The goal is to associate the product with the initial meaning of “healthy.”

And it works! Studies show36 that just by removing healthy terms from a package design, their health perception also worsened.

For us adults, this means we face many green colors, leaves, and trees, but also badges and verbal claims trying to wake up a relation between health and the given product. Kids get reminded of fun and adventure through comical mascots.

Reverse priming

The same effect also gets used to communicate that a product is free of something that has negative associations. You find labels like “MSG-free,” despite no scientific evidence around adverse effects. However, this claim works as there are still strong associations37 between MSG and headaches, nausea, and sweating.

The same accounts for the typical “bad fat/sugar makers” and often get exchanged for each other. A Product prints “low fat” on it? It might be high in sugars (look up a fat-free Vinaigrette). A “no added sugars” claim could mean unhealthy fats (look up Highkey Keto Chocolate Chip Cookies).

The idea, however, is always the same. The marketing points out what the product does NOT contain so that the health association gets built through the absence of something seemingly bad.

Whole ingredients over hyper-processed building blocks

It almost doesn’t matter what type of product you look at. You will always find similar products with very different qualities. Ultimately, how healthy processed food can be, depends on how much whole foods get packed into the final product. The more extracts are used, the worse the energy-to-nutrient ratio gets.

To understand the wide variety of food products, you should look at the ingredients list and the nutritional information label. Once you pick your product type, this is your base for comparison.

What you can read from the ingredients list

The ingredients list holds more information than just the plain names of the contents of your product. Food manufacturers must list a product's ingredients in order of predominance, meaning they are sorted from the greatest amount used first38 to the smallest amount last.

This gives you a great insight into the rough composition of a product. The earlier an ingredient gets listed, the more the product contains of it. Generally, you should look at what the main contents of the product are made of. In most cases, you find the main components in the first five positions of an ingredients list.

Since we are looking for nutritional value, let’s ignore the water content. Look for the first “caloric” items in the list. Are they one of white flour, sugar, and its relatives? Starch? Oil? Or are they primarily natural and whole food ingredients like oats, vegetables, nuts, or beans?

The predominance of the first ingredients is a tremendous help to find out how “whole” this product is and, thus, how much of the original nutrients from the food potentially are preserved.

Put simply: if you find mainly natural, whole ingredients in the pole positions of the ingredients list, you can assume that this product brings some nutritional value to the table.

What the nutrition facts can tell you

Most products carry this typical black-and-white nutrition facts label. From here, you can read how much this snack impacts your caloric balance for the day and what energy source this comes from.

The first thing you should notice on a label is the serving size. This indicates the amount of the product the nutrient facts are given for. Nutrient labels that list 100g values or other standardized measures like ounces or cups are great for comparison. Some labels provide those; some labels give recommended serving sizes. Some labels give both.

Especially with those labels that only show a recommended serving size, you can be suspicious about how much a portion is supposed to be. If the amount is ridiculously small, for example, 30g of cereal as a meal, you should upscale the stated amounts proportionally to estimate a comparable value. Commonly, high-caloric foods come with a small recommended serving size.

Next come the sections for carbs, fats, and protein. As you remember, carbs and protein deliver 4 kcal of energy per gram, while fat adds 9 kcal per gram to the scale. You will also find a percentage number pointing to how much the serving typically contributes to a diet of 2000 kcal.

Regarding protein, counting the grams you take daily is more common. In terms of fat and sugar, their relative composition is more relevant than the contribution to a fixed caloric goal.

In the section about fats, you should look if there is any trans fat listed. While most products nowadays have nearly zero trans fats, spotting those should be alarming. Next, you should see how much saturated fat there is compared to the total. Ideally, there should not be more than one-third of saturated fats in the fat’s total content.

You can calculate how many calories from fat this product contains:

Just multiply the grams of fat per serving (or based on 100g) by nine, and you get the total calories from fat. Divide this number by the total calories per serving or unit, and you know the percentage of calories coming from fat. Remember, we should aim a total fat below 30% of your total calories throughout the day.

Practically this means the further away the fat profile is from a desirable composition, the less you should take of it. If, on the other hand, the fat profile looks mainly unsaturated, this can promote your healthy fat profile. Just ensure to get only a little of it if you want to stay under the 30% of total fats.

The section on carbohydrates usually lists sugar under the total carbohydrates. Here you can look at the percental contribution to your RDA. As you remember, ideally, we stay under 10% of calories from added sugars, or even under 5% for additional health benefits.

If you want to find out how much energy comes from either carbs or protein, you can use the same calculation as with fat. Just use four calories as a base instead of nine.

Beneath the section about calories and macro composition, you usually find a small section with some highlights on vitamins or minerals. This usually is only a partial list and points out just a few nutrients. This information is generally only relevant if you are trying to fill a particular deficit.

Typical purchase traps

With all that said, let’s look for some typical purchase traps.

  • Low sugar / Sugar-free products
    The “low sugar” claim does not talk about fast carbs but exclusively about sugar. While being low on sugar, flour, or starch can be high. A look at the ingredients list and nutrition facts can help distinguish similar products. Typical candidates for the low sugar trap are granolas, chocolate & protein bars, and other baked goods.
  • Low-fat products
    Fat-reduced products often lack taste; therefore, you will find added sugar in the product to bring it back. You can typically find high amounts of added sugars in dressings, sauces, flavored yogurts, soups, instant dishes, and powders.
  • Salt
    As salt is an excellent preservative and taste promoter, you find it in many instant dishes, meat products, cheese, bread, and baked goods. Especially since we don’t need more than 5g of salt daily, the hidden salt contents of products39 can sum up in no time. Don’t be fooled by small numbers, and remember the upper limit.
  • Supposedly whole food products
    Keep your eyes open even on products you could assume are whole food products. Take peanut butter, for example. You only need peanuts and a blender to make peanut butter. Many products, however, have added oil, which boosts the caloric balance and lowers the product's protein and nutrient content.

Make use of food scanner apps!

I can imagine you don’t want to stand in the supermarket for hours making calculations to compare products. Luckily, technology has got you covered, and plenty of apps provide nutritional information when scanning a product’s barcode.

While all apps follow the same principle - you point your camera at a barcode and get information - there are still different approaches to what information is given. You can distinguish between apps that summarize and rate a product, and then some apps give you detailed insights into the nutrients. Ultimately, both approaches have their purpose, and I have no recommendation.

Apps that summarize, rate, and compare products can be a handy tool while on your shopping trip. You pick a product and scan it, and you get a strong indicator if a product fits your diet. And this is very convenient in most shopping situations.

Detailed apps, however, are convenient in professional diet planning. You can add several foods to your daily plate and see how nutrients pile up. This type of app is suitable for sports nutrition, allowing you to track your exact goals. On the other hand, those apps often come without ratings and recommendations, and you need to plan independently.

When scanning the same product with different apps, you might find two completely different ratings between the apps. This is because they use other rating systems. If you have one app putting much weight on the macro composition, nuts might be bad food due to their high-fat contents. A diet Coke with no added sugar can be rated as an excellent product, besides not adding any nutritional value.

Ultimately, use those apps as a tool of convenience rather than as the ultimate source of truth. Read their ratings and put some thought into it. Remember that you are scanning a single food individually, not a whole day of mixed eating.

Apps I personally use

Generally, I recommend finding an app that uses a food database from your country. This gives you the most precise nutritional values for your region (a potato grown in the Americas gets a different nutrient profile from its soil than a potato from Europe) and has the highest likelihood of your products being available in your database. Those apps, however, often require a paid subscription, as the food data gets collected by an institute or organization. On the other hand, open-source apps get their information through the community of users.

Nowadays, they also have mature food databases and are a good enough solution for most people, especially when they are free to use.

Here are the apps I use personally. To have it mentioned: I recommend those because I find them handy. I am in no way affiliated with the companies behind them. And yes, there are still plenty of other alternatives to choose from.

Yuka app

Yuka is a very straightforward food scanner app. They give the food a score out of one hundred (from poor to excellent), list the positives and negatives of the product, and, if available, recommend similar products with a better composition. This app works for cosmetic products as well.

Products can get added through users themselves, and new info gets screened and verified before being added to the database. Sadly, the app requires you to sign up with your email address before using it. That given, I’ve never received any communication from them in my inbox.

Currently, Yuca is available in the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Ireland, France, Germany, Belgium, Switzerland, Luxembourg, Spain, and Italy.

How I use it

Yuka is very convenient to use on shopping trips. Especially when going for new products, the comparison feature is neat. You scan something, and the app shows you alternative picks with better nutrient profiles.

Open food facts

Open food facts is a crowd-collected database of nutritional information about foods. Unlike with Yuka, there is no editorial staff in the background, as this is a 100% community-driven project.

The approach to using the app is very similar to Yuka. Point your camera at a barcode or use the search. Open food facts include several scorings. You get the nutriscore rating, the nova score (indicating how much whole foods it contains), and a packaging rating.

It also allows you to set up allergens alerts, so scanning a product containing specific allergens will highlight this information.

Thanks to their open approach, no registration is required to use the app. You can use their whole database freely without sharing personal data. What’s great about the open-source system is that you can easily contribute. Do you have some product info at hand that is still missing? You can add it right away. Information is unavailable in your language, but can you help translate it? The app will allow it.

How I use it

I love that the summary section of the app gives important indicators at first sight. The nutriscore points out the balance of nutrients, the Novo score indicates how much whole food is inside, and the eco score indicates the environmental impact. Nice! Below, you can also find labels that quickly highlight whether a food is vegan, vegetarian, or contains palm oil.

This is great for quickly scanning those check marks before putting the product into the basket.

Cronometer

Cronometer is a hybrid between a nutrition and a fitness tracker. It is not intended for product comparison but to work on your fitness and nutrition targets. Registration is mandatory, but the app itself is free. You can pay for a premium service that gives you more features and removes annoying ads.

The app asks you for some basics like your age, sex, height, and body weight and calculates your daily targets in terms of nutrients. You can override those settings to fit your needs afterward.

Once that’s done, you can add foods (and exercises) to your daily log. It allows you to track the amounts eaten and list your nutrients exactly. Unlike food scanner apps, Cronometer is not dependent on barcodes; you can add plenty of whole foods without packages through a search.

Cronometer uses multiple food databases and can find most foods available. However, some entries don’t list the complete nutrient profile. So be aware that some foods might bump up your macros, while the information around micronutrients may be missing.

The app allows you to create recipes as well. This is handy when you consume the same foods regularly. Instead of composing your meal every single time, save the composition as a recipe. Next time you eat the same dish, add the amount from the recipe to your log, and the app calculates the rest for you.

How I use it

While this app is intended to be used in sports nutrition to track your goals, I prefer it to look up foods and nutrients and verify and fine-tune my recipes. I start by drafting my rough idea of what I want to cook. With all ingredients added, the app calculates the nutrients on your plate. From here, it is easy to round up your recipe.

If, let’s say, something is hypercaloric, try replacing or reducing the component responsible for it. Are there big gaps in vitamins or minerals? Add some more variety in fruit and vegetables to your dish.
