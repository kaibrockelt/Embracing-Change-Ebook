Critical Nutrients

Knowing what micronutrients are at risk of a potential deficiency is essential when chasing healthy goals. Luckily, most nutrients are widely available across the food bench. This means we can focus on what’s potentially critical and avoid taking care of things that don’t require your attention.

We already know about essential fatty acids and ways to keep our protein intake adequate. This chapter will focus on minerals and vitamins that are critical or at risk of deficiency.

Why is this important?

Understanding potentially critical nutrients helps prevent deficiencies and the symptoms that might come along. Knowing where to find important nutrients can help you make healthier food choices. Low-level risk for deficiencies allows you to function fully and perform best.

What does “critical” mean in terms of nutrients?

A nutrient is critical when a high part of the population doesn't have a close to optimal supply and hence is at risk of a deficiency.

This does not automatically mean that, by definition, a deficiency is present per sé, as a balanced diet can be sufficient to supply all essential nutrients in adequate quantities under normal conditions. Alcoholism, disease, other conditions, or medication can lead to deficiencies in typically uncritical nutrients.

So “critical” means “at risk of deficiency” on a poor diet.

The recommendations for an adequate intake vary a bit from country to country. If you take selenium as an extreme example, you can find high amounts of selenium in the soil of North America. At the same time, it is less present in Europe, so the serum levels depend on your environment71. Depending on the supply, the recommendation can vary.

What is the recommended daily intake?

The RDI, or RDA, indicates the amount that should be consumed. Our daily need usually lies way below the recommendation for intake. Low absorption rates and other factors are considered in the recommendation, to provide sufficient amounts.

The recommended dietary allowances describe an intake of nutrients suitable for at least 97,5% of the healthy population. It is usually around 20% higher than the estimated average requirement (EAR), which hits the need for about 50% of a given population (the average need).

On the side of toxicity, the tolerable upper-level intake (UL) describes the highest safe intake level before seeing adverse effects for 97,5% of the population.

Besides the upper limit, a lowest-observed-adverse-effect level (LOAEL) is defined. This points to the minimal nutrient intake that causes toxic effects.

If your nutrient intake lies between the RDA and UL, this is a generally adequate intake.

How to interpret this information?

Don’t track every critical nutrient with microscopic precision. The idea is to understand how to pick the right foods and preparation methods to feel safe to end up on the right side of the scale. Nothing more and nothing less is intended.

Instead, I recommend taking one of those nutrition and sports tracker apps to determine which food is an excellent contributor to your daily goals and which is rather not.

Simply put, it is a safe pick if a particular food fills up your nutrient targets faster than your energy goals. Instead of tracking everything daily, you know which foods are good for you, and you will automatically integrate more of that into your diet.

The data shown is taken from the highest recommendation intake for males and females from the age of 9 upwards and the correspondingly lowest upper intake levels. That means the range between recommended supply and the upper limit may be more extensive for your age group than indicated here. This recommendation excludes pregnant women, lactating women, and children under 9. These numbers are based on the work of the National Institutes of Health72.

The descriptive information about a nutrient's purpose, deficiency, or intoxication is based on the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health articles. With each summary, you will find a link to the corresponding report for the case you are interested in reading further.

If you think you are at a deficiency or experience symptoms, get a check-up with your doctor. He will be able to verify your assumption and help you.

Do you need to cover everything, daily?

When seeing all those numbers, your reaction might be, “Wow, that’s much stuff to take care of.” But that’s not the idea. Instead of ensuring you hit every goal daily with precision, you should focus on the average supply throughout your week.

The goal should be to create the joy of eating whole and natural foods instead of creating Excel tables. If, for example, you assume that you did not hit your iron requirements throughout the last two days, you can be assured that it is ok if you get them in only on day 3, but in higher amounts.

In the end, it all comes back to the basic principle:

Eat whole, eat fresh, and eat with variety.

What about supplements?

Generally, I do not suggest using supplements containing all nutrients, as they may give you more than you need, which in the end can harm your health. Instead, when at deficiency, you should only supplement specific nutrients in the amount recommended by your doctor. The aim should be to fill the gap in your diet and not to overshoot your supply.

If you make the necessary changes to your diet, you may not need to take any supplements.

Critical Vitamins

Three vitamins are critical or potentially at risk of deficiency for a large part of the population. These are the water-soluble vitamins B2 and B12 and the fat-soluble vitamin D.

Vitamin B12

This vitamin, most known from discussions about veganism, indeed is critical. If you follow a vegan diet, supplementation is necessary, as plant food sources do not contain sufficient amounts of B12 to keep you healthy.

But also, when consuming animal products, a vitamin B12 deficiency risk rises with age. Deficiency is caused by inadequate intake, inadequate bioavailability, or malabsorption. As you cannot intoxicate yourself with vitamin B12, supplementing B12 is generally safe and can lower your overall risk of deficiency.

The ways of vitamin B12 into your bloodstream.

B12 is BIG. It’s the biggest vitamin of all. It is such a vast complex that there is no safe way through your stomach without some extra protection from destruction. A protein named intrinsic factor covers vitamin B12 on it’s way through the stomach. After B12 leaves the acidic environment, it can be absorbed in your intestine and transferred into your blood.

Knowing about the intrinsic factor is essential, as it is only available in small amounts at a given time. If you swallow a high dose of vitamin B12, most of it will dissolve in your stomach due to the unavailability of intrinsic factor. Suppose the same amount of B12 would arrive distributed throughout the day. In that case, the total absorption will be higher, as the intrinsic factor had time to replenish normal levels between the doses.

Small amounts of vitamin B12 can also diffuse through the mucous membrane in your mouth and land directly in the blood flow.

Once in your body, it can be stored in your liver. Your storage can be big enough to last 3-5 years. An inadequate intake, therefore, can show very late.

Types of B12 supplements

If you want to supplement vitamin B12, knowing how they should be taken is crucial. B12 dietary supplements usually come in the form of pills and lozenges.

B12 pills are designed to sustain your stomach's acidic environment but dissolve in your intestine's basic environment. The capsule itself takes over the job of the intrinsic factor.

If, instead, you buy lozenges, they are intended to be kept in your mouth until they dissolve. Here, the B12 is not taking the stomach way, but a small fraction of the B12 manages to enter the bloodstream the diffusion way. This is also why B12 lozenges usually contain 2000%-5000% of your recommended daily intake. If you swallow one, most of its content will be destroyed by stomach acid.

You can also go to the doctor and get a high dose of B12 as an injection, which is supposed to last over months.

Vitamin D

Vitamin D, or calciferol, is the sunshine vitamin. Technically it is not a vitamin but a fat-soluble prohormone. It mainly gets synthesized by animals and can be found in animal products. You can find smaller amounts of vitamin D in mushrooms. There is no vitamin D in plant food.

Vitamin D is vital in preventing many chronic diseases like hypertension, CHD, diabetes, and autoimmune diseases. It is directly linked to your bone mineralization, where you can find vitamin D acting with calcium.

We can synthesize enough vitamin D by exposing our skin to direct sunlight. Twenty minutes daily of direct sunlight on exposed arms and face is enough for most people. During sunny days, that is easily archivable.

The more UV light can touch your skin, the more Vitamin D you can synthesize. The amount of available sunlight, air pollution, your age, overweight, and skin tone, however, can reduce your vitamin D synthesis.

A rule of thumb: the brighter your skin, the more vitamin D can be produced compared to darker skin tones. Especially people with a dark skin tone living in cold and dark areas are at elevated risk of developing a vitamin D deficiency.

In winter, when we spend most time inside, and direct sunlight is rare, the exposure may not be sufficient to synthesize adequate levels of vitamin D. When filled, our Vitamin D stores last 2-3 months. The dark months can easily span over that time. Here, supplementation can be reasonable.

A deficiency of vitamin D can lead to fatigue and physical weakness. It can leave a feeling of depression and make you become ill more easily. The overall tone of the skin might become paler. These symptoms of the typical “winter depression” can be due to low vitamin D levels in your body.

If you compare vitamin D supplements, you will see that they are usually measured in IU - international units. This is because vitamin D is not a single molecule but a group of substances. What usually gets measured in your serum is the active form of vitamin D3.

This number indicates how much active vitamin D equivalents will be supplemented in one dose. So it does not show how much vitamin D is actually “in” the pill, but it indicates how much you can use it.

One international unit equals 0.025µg of active vitamin D. 1000 international units represent 25µg and are usually enough to hit your RDI.

Vitamin B2

Vitamin B2, or riboflavin, is deeply involved in energy metabolism, cellular respiration, and the production of antibodies. B2 can be found in most foods, but certainly in higher amounts in animal products, mainly dairy.

While B2 is not considered critical on an omnivore diet, it gets rated “at risk” on a vegan diet. You find high vitamin B2 in tempeh (fermented soybeans), mushrooms, almonds, and yeast. A sufficient supply is easily doable through a diverse alimentation and a little consideration of B2-rich foods.

Other good plant-based sources are:

  • oily seeds and nuts
  • green vegetables
  • mushrooms
  • whole grains
  • yeasts
  • fermented products

Riboflavin is super heat resistant but highly sensitive to light. Foods containing fair amounts of vitamin B2 should be stored in a dark place.

While a deficiency is rare, it most often occurs with general malnutrition and lacking other B vitamins. Symptoms of a shortage of vitamin B2 can be dry skin and lips, anemia, and swelling of the mouth and nose.

Critical Minerals

Of the many essential minerals you can find in food, only a few are critical. As with vitamins, a balanced diet can also fill all nutritional gaps in terms of minerals.

The minerals listed here, however, have more potential to become deficient than others. Hence, knowing where to find them allows you to put countermeasures on your plate to keep the risk of deficiencies as low as possible.

Iodine

Iodine is the most critical nutrient globally. It is the only micronutrient directly mentioned in the WHO recommendation for a healthy diet. This trace element is crucial to our thyroid and its hormones. They are essential players in the processes of growth and for your metabolism.

Especially during growth, a deficiency of iodine can lead to functional and developmental abnormalities and is critical. As an adult, you can develop a goiter, a typical swelling of the thyroid gland.

The iodine content of your food highly depends on the soil it is grown on. Depending on where your food is grown, it might contain sufficient amounts of iodine. This is often intransparent and unreliable as we don't know the location and conditions under which a product is grown.

If you want to rely more on natural sources, I recommend buying foods from the sea, like seaweed products (kelp, wakame, nori,..) or fish and seafood. Here, the iodine contents can be very high. Especially with algae and seaweed, the iodine contents can skyrocket.

Therefore it is vital that you take a good look if the iodine contents of a product are on the nutritional label. Only if you see the iodine contents on a product you buy can you know that the content’s controlled and correct.

If you do not like seafood or algae too much, you will find iodized table salt that you can use for cooking instead of regular “plain” salt. Considering that the iodine supply is below recommendation on average, picking an iodized salt is a quick and easy contribution to fill the gap.

Selenium

Selenium is a trace mineral that plays a part in DNA synthesis. Its availability is dependent on the soil food grows on. Generally, the soil is rich in selenium in Northern America, and the supply is stable.

In other regions, like central Europe, the soil does not hold high amounts of selenium. Selenium gets supplemented to animals for human nutrition. Science, however, still needs to be made clear about the overall selenium supply in human nutrition.

You find selenium in animal products, whole grains, and legumes. One exceptional food is Brazil nuts. Their selenium contents vary heavily, and you can overshoot your daily intake with just two nuts, or stay far below.

You can use Brazil nuts as a source of selenium. It is essential to keep their potentially high amounts of selenium in mind, so don’t eat them in too high quantities and not too often.

Iron

The supply of dietary iron is a global challenge. According to the WHO, iron is one of the most common deficiencies worldwide, where developing countries are impacted the most on average. Living in a developed country raises your stakes.

Iron’s primary purpose is to carry oxygen through your bloodstream in the form of hemoglobin. You can find iron in two forms: heme iron and nonheme iron.

Women have a higher need for iron due to menstruation. Injury, blood donations, or other reasons for blood loss can spike your iron needs.

Non-heme iron is found across plant and animal foods, and it is the simple form of iron, as it is bound in its ionized forms, Fe3+ and Fe2+. In the human body, this type of iron is found in proteins for storage (ferritin) and transport (transferrin).

You find heme iron exclusively in animal products. Heme is its “organized” form, bound in a complex for oxygen transport.

Heme and nonheme iron differ in terms of absorption. While heme iron gets absorbed at a rate of 15-30%, the absorption of nonheme iron lies between 2-20% and depends on two factors.

First, the body can up and down-regulate absorption of non-heme iron depending on the current supply. Therefore, intoxication through non-heme iron is difficult, as the absorption can be downregulated when a high reserve is reached.

The second “vault” for absorbing nonheme iron are other factors from your food. You can boost your iron absorption by adding foods rich in vitamin C and citric acids.

On the other hand, tannins, oxalic acid, and phytic acid hinder absorption by building indigestible complexes with dietary iron. The same happens with coffee and tea. I recommend keeping your coffee or tea at least one hour away from your meals to avoid this interaction and optimize your absorption rate.

Zinc

Zinc plays a significant role in enzymes for DNA replication and cellular growth. It supports protein synthesis and a functioning immune system. You find plenty of zinc in seafood, meats, poultry, and plant food groups of legumes, whole grains, and nuts.

While a zinc deficiency in healthy individuals is rare, it is more common in people with inflammatory bowel disease and kidney or liver damage. A lack can lead to a loss of smell and appetite, abdominal pain, lowered immune activity, delayed wound healing, and hair loss.

Calcium

You most likely already know that calcium is good for the bones. This is true, as calcium is a primary factor for mineralizing your bones. On top of that, calcium plays a vital role in blood clotting, together with the vitamins D and K. It furthermore takes part in signaling in muscles and nerves.

A deficiency of calcium is barely visible in the serum. If our blood calcium levels are low, calcium gets released from the bones to keep the levels up. Our bones build up calcium until around the age of 25. The better our calcium supply until then, the potentially stronger our bones will become.

After that age, the mineralization of bones slowly climbs back down unavoidably. Depending on your supply, you lose 0.5% to 2.5% of your bone volume year over year.

Being deficient over a long time, therefore, leads to osteoporosis faster. An acute deficiency can lead to muscle cramps, a feeling of numbness and tingling in the fingers, poor appetite, and abnormal heart rates.

Like iron and zinc, calcium can also be influenced in terms of absorption—more details in a later chapter.
