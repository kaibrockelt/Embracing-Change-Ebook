4.3 Habit:

Food Quality and Spoilage

Food quality and health benefits come from picking the right food in the first place. “The more natural and whole, the more beneficial,” you can say as a general rule.

Food's nutritional value is temporary and subject to natural decay. In this chapter, we look into the biological processes of food spoilage and how we can counteract that.

Why is this important?

After picking food or cutting a slice of meat, natural decay starts. The longer food has time to go bad, the fewer nutrients can be found inside.

Understanding how and why things spoil can help you preserve nutrients better and get more out of the money you spend on your food.

What is food quality?

When speaking of food quality in general, this can mean many things. Some may point to the taste and visual perception of food. Some may value the origin of the food or its rarity (think of caviar).

Technically speaking, these are all qualities that contribute to the experience of a meal. A meal that does not look or taste good is unlikely to be perceived as “quality food.” But the quality of the experience is not the same as when we talk about the food quality from a nutritional perspective.

When you look at things from the perspective of nutrition, food quality is measured by the following:

  • the contents and density of micronutrients, which are the 13 vitamins and the contents and accessibility of minerals.
  • the contents and ratio of essential fatty acids.
  • the glycaemic index, or how fast a portion of food may raise your blood sugar levels.
  • the balance between energy and nutrients provided. In comparison, healthy foods offer more nutrients per calorie.
  • The contents of secondary plant substances and fiber.

Naturally, you significantly impact food quality by choosing whole and grown foods over processed and extract-based foods. However, the quality of whole food also knows another enemy: decay.

Decay

As soon as food gets picked or cut, the natural decay process start occurring. That means the longer it takes for a food to be eaten since the moment it got picked, the fewer nutrients remain.

The main factors that influence decay over time are

  • the temperature the food is stored at.
  • the exposure to light.
  • the exposure to oxygen.

A rule of thumb in chemistry says that raising the temperature by 10 °C halves the reaction times. Put the other way around - the lower you keep the temperature, the longer nutrients stay intact. This is related to the normal breakdown of structures and enzymatic activity.

This is something you can test yourself at home. Take an apple, for example, and cut it into three pieces. Put one piece on your shelf, one in the fridge, and one in the freezer. You can check the time it takes for the piece on the shelf to go rancid and then review the others.

On the other hand, if you treat food for a long time at high temperatures, the loss of nutrients is also huge. But more on that will come in chapter 6.3. For now, you should remember that the cooler you store food, the longer it stays good.

Studies show68 that the higher the temperature of storage, the faster vitamin C decay occurs.

Some Vitamins, as well as Omega 3 fatty acids, are sensitive to light. You might have seen that omega-rich oils are often sold in dark bottles. This blocks the light to prevent the oil from becoming rancid too fast.

The role of bacteria, yeasts, and mold

Bacteria and yeasts don’t care about our role in the food chain. What is there to be broken down will be broken down - whether you throw the food or not. They mainly feed on macronutrients, so they will break down the energetic value of food while simultaneously releasing toxins.

You can distinguish between different groups of microorganisms depending on what food they like. Some bacteria or yeasts feed on fats and protein in animal products. Some prefer carbohydrates and impact plant-based foods. So let’s break down what you can encounter.

Bacteria

There are different types of bacteria feeding on different types of macros. You can spot them by smelling the food, as they typically release organic acids like ammonia and alcohol. So whenever your food smells like a night out or of ammonia, then it might be contaminated.

On animal products, you typically find bacteria from salmonella, E.Coli, and listeria families. On plants, you can encounter Escherichia coli bacteria or EHECs. Each of those groups can cause food poisoning69.

Those toxins can also build up on a prepared plate. Blanching your veggies and frying your meats properly should kill those bacteria.

Yeasts

The family of yeasts mainly feeds on glucose and breaks them down into Ethanol and CO2, as well as lactic acid. Like bacteria, they are not visible to the naked eye, so use your nose and smell your food. If there’s a funky alcoholic smell on it that might remind you of nail polish, most likely yeast has taken hold of your food.

We use yeasts for controlled fermentation, which is controlled decay. You know this from yogurt or cheese making. If, however, your orange juice starts smelling like your gouda, this usually happens uncontrolled and might be adding toxins to your food.

Mold

We all have seen food spoiled by mold. Whenever this green-to-white furry texture comes up on the surface, we know it’s mold taking its place there. Many people are tempted to cut around the “moldy area” and keep the rest of the food. But that’s a terrible idea.

The visible green dust is only a part of the mold. The rest lives as mycelium within the food and is invisible to the naked eye. Also, unlike bacteria and yeast, the mycelium of mold is heat resistant and cannot be just cooked off.

Mold mainly feeds on protein and releases toxins while doing that. One group, the aflatoxins, is highly cancerous70. You typically find those toxins in moldy nuts, seeds, grains, spices, and dry fruit.

Contamination

When mold, bacteria, or yeast move from one food to another, we call it contamination. And boy, this can happen quickly. You put one contaminated food in the fridge, and in no time, more and more food spoils rapidly.

The fastest transfer of spoil happens through your hands when you first touch an already spoiled food and then continue working on something else. But also a humid closed space like your fridge allows the easy spread of bacteria.

While freezing pretty much slows down the spread of pathogens to a stop, it won’t kill all of the pathogens sitting on your food. That means the party goes on right after defrosting, and the spoils can return to normal speed. Thus, heating frozen food above 70°C / 158°F is still necessary.

To minimize contamination, you should pick up some clean working habits. It’s almost impossible to prevent spoilage completely, but keeping an eye on certain things helps keep contamination in check:

  • Clean working: Ensure your hands and utensils are properly washed before using them. Also, washing your food can get rid of contaminating bacteria and yeasts that sit on the surface.
  • Separation and isolation: If you keep your foods in airtight containers, contamination between those containers is impossible. Locking the air contact locks contamination.
  • Keep the temperature in check: the same chemical rule that applies to decay also applies to bacteria and yeast. The lower the temperature, the slower the spoilage through bacteria, yeast, and mold. Keep the temperature of your fridge as low as you can.
  • Proper heating: what is dead cannot produce toxins. Make sure to heat your food for at least 70°C / 158°F for 2 minutes to ensure everything is as dead as possible.

Strategies for storage

Keep food in closed packages, boxes, or jars to prevent contamination. On the one hand, storing foods in sealed containers prevents insects like moths or fruit flies from feeding or laying eggs. On the other hand, if one of your food items turns bad, it prevents mold from spreading.

To preserve the quality of fats and vitamins, you should keep sensitive foods away from direct light and heat exposure. Oils sensitive to oxygen and light should be stored correctly in a closed container.
