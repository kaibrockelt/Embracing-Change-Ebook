2.3 Habit:

Whole Food Groups

It doesn’t matter what lifestyle you aim for: Being able to tell which food group offers you which benefits allows you to cook healthily intentionally. If you look back to what a healthy diet is, according to the WHO, you will find the five plant food groups mentioned as a part of a healthy diet: fruit, vegetables, legumes, nuts and seeds, and whole grains.

You should try to rely as much as possible on whole foods over processed foods, as in their natural form, they contain the very best amounts of their natural nutrients compared to products based on extracts. Ideally, try going around the circle once per day. Try to get in at least one food of each group throughout all your meals to benefit from their unique nutrient profiles.

Why is this important?

Knowing the food groups helps tremendously in understanding what you put on your plate daily.

Each food group has its unique nutritional properties, that you should try benefiting from as much as you can.

 

Why consume each group?

Each group differs in its nutritional profile. Nuts, for example, are high in calories, mainly driven by healthy fats. On the other hand, fruits and vegetables are low in energy but rich in certain vitamins and minerals.

Simply put, you won’t hit your caloric target just by eating fruits and vegetables. If you focus on only one group or two, you risk spiking certain nutrients while lacking others.

Knowing what your body needs and understanding where those nutrients are allows you to make an educated choice in the design of your meals. Especially if you aim for a plant-based diet, calories can become a bottleneck. Therefore it is crucial to be aware of the nutrient profile of each food group.

What should be considered whole food?

Eating Legumes, nuts, and whole grains like rice or quinoa daily may appear dull and part of the typical vegan stereotype.

But a whole food does not need to be in its whole form for you to benefit from its value. After all, cooking is a form of processing too. And so is chewing. Instead of asking yourself whether a product is processed, the question should be how much got stripped off from the original food to create that product.

In fact, many products are built on whole foods. If you read the ingredients list of a given product and find whole foods in the first positions, that often is a good pick and, at the same time, puts your checkmark on the circle of the food groups.

When you ask yourself, “How much is left from the original food,” you often find a reasonable estimate. Take soy, for example - Edamame and Tempeh contain whole beans. Tofu already loses vitamins in the cooking and dry squeeze process. Soy protein chunks, a meat replacement, lack most minerals and vitamins, leaving you with a ball of energy and not much added value. No wonder; they are an extract produced under high heat and pressure.

While tofu is already processed, it keeps most of the original nutrients intact. Soy chunks, on the other hand, are pure extracts like sugar, starch, and oil. They might tame your hunger but are barely beneficial in any other way.

Practically that means that you still can find healthy options in convenience foods. Just be sure to make the right pick more often. Take whole grain bread over a plain white one. Choose whole-grain pasta or one made from lentils. You can find burger patties built on veggies and beans. And peanut butter made from 100% peanuts is nothing more than 100% peanuts. Just be aware of what you are eating and check the ingredients list.

Whole grains

Grains have been important in the human diet for thousands of years. No wonder, as they are easy to store long-term and bring many essential nutrients to the table. Typically, grains are rich in complex carbohydrates and fiber. The frequent consumption of whole grains is associated with a reduced risk for coronary heart disease4, several cancers5, respiratory diseases & type 2 diabetes6, adiposity7, and overall lower morbidity8.

They play an essential role in the supply of complex carbohydrates and protein. Whole grains usually contain high amounts of minerals and b vitamins.

There are tons of different grains available out there to pick from. Red, white, or black quinoa, rice, amaranth, wheat, barley, millet, teff, oats,… If you have them in their whole form, soaking and boiling them is the most common way to prepare them. But you can also try to blend them into flours and use them as binders for doughs, burgers, “meat”-balls, and more.

Especially with wheat products, the degree of processing makes a huge difference in food quality. White flour is mainly made from the endosperm, rich in starch and protein. Most vitamins and minerals sit in the shell and skin and get polished off the grain before grinding.

Why you should not miss out on grains

Whole grains are a good source of complex carbs. They give you energy in a form that gets absorbed slowly over time. They have a fair amount of protein that, together with the protein of legumes, builds a high-quality full protein. Their high fiber contents promote good colon health.

Plain grains are boring.

I get that. And yes, I agree. Most grains in their boiled form come pretty tasteless. Best case, they are good enough as a side in their plain form. But that’s up to you to change it! Please use what you have learned about taste and try adding some!

Start frying them to put some of that sweet Maillard reaction into them (You will learn more about that later in the next section). Toss garlic. Toss spices. Herbs. Add ingredients that fulfill at least some of the five tastes. It doesn’t take much to take a tasteless whatever to something with a decent profile.

What about gluten?

If you are intolerant to gluten, there are some restrictions on what you can consume. However, you can still get many gluten-free grains and pseudo-grains. Rice, quinoa, amaranth, oats (if not processed along with wheat), millet, and buckwheat are some gluten-free grains that fit into a healthy diet. If you look for pasta, there is rice pasta (look in Asian stores!) or pasta made from lentils or chickpeas.

Legumes

Beans and lentils are brutally underrated foods. They deliver between 20-50% protein, are low fat, and have high fiber. Their contents of minerals and proteins can catch up with animal products while being on the lean side.

Especially on a vegan diet, legumes are a significant source of protein, as they contain high amounts of the amino acid lysine, which is a bottleneck in other food groups.

Not only for their protein content, legumes are fantastic. Their regular consumption is associated with a lowered risk for coronary heart disease and reduced risks for several cancers9. Despite containing fair amounts of carbohydrates, their low glycaemic index makes legumes an excellent food for diabetic people.

The second meal effect

Studies have shown10 that legumes and foods with a low GI positively impact the blood sugar rise in the meal they’ve been taken with and reduce the impact of a sugary next meal. This can even happen overnight.

On the one hand, this means that if you are on a healthy baseline with what you are eating, an exception here and there can be handled better. On the other hand, this is a beneficial tool for people with diabetes, as this gives an excellent way to reduce the overall insulin need.

Preparation of legumes

You can find legumes pre-boiled or in their dried form. If you want to buy them in their dry conditions, it is vital to soak and boil them properly before consumption.

In their raw form, you can find so-called protease inhibitors. Those are substances that hinder the digestion of protein. By boiling legumes for just 10 to 15 minutes, you can completely deactivate them. This is usually less than the recommended cooking time for a lentil or bean.

Similarly, you eliminate lectins, a group of substances that can cause several adverse digestive effects. Simply put, you're good to go if you boil your beans or lentils for at least 15 minutes when you buy them in their dry form.

Furthermore, they contain phytic acid, which builds complexes with minerals like zinc and iron and hinders the absorption of these nutrients. Soaking, fermentation, and germination can reduce their contents. Despite affecting the absorption of minerals, phytic acid is associated with anti-cancerogenic, anti-oxidative, cholesterol-lowering, and blood sugar-regulating effects11. So that’s both good and bad effects on one substance. I still recommend you properly soak them if you have the time.

If you do not like eating beans or lentils in their entire form, there are many ways to change them. Chickpeas get turned into hummus and falafel easily. Lentils and beans can be blent with some grains to form burger patties, meatballs, and more. Tofu and tempeh are very versatile ingredients. Many quality convenience products often contain legumes and are ready for preparation.

Nuts and seeds

Nuts and seeds are a group of foods rich in mono and poly-unsaturated fats. (More to that in the next chapter). Usually, you find between 40% to 70% fat content in both nuts and seeds.

In nuts, you typically find 10% to 20% of protein. Seeds go higher and deliver 17% to 37% protein content. Combine them with other protein-rich foods like grains or legumes to get the most out of this. Crumble nuts in your oatmeal. Make fresh hummus12 with sesame paste / tahini. Or garnish anything with seeds like pumpkin, sunflower, chia, and more.

The consumption of nuts and seeds is generally associated with preventive effects for coronary disease, diabetes, and adiposity13. You also find plenty of minerals like iron, calcium, zinc, and magnesium. They usually are rich in B vitamins and vitamin E.

Ways to integrate more nuts and seeds into your diet

The most obvious form of eating nuts is just snacking them. A handful of nuts between meals can help against little hunger or concentration lows.

But nuts and seeds can be integrated into other meals as well. Sprinkle them in whole or chopped form over salads, bowls, and cereals. Mix them into yogurts and enjoy the additional crunch. With the help of a blender, you can also powderise nuts and seeds. This nut meal can then go into dough or cereal dishes.

If you put enough blending effort into it, you can transform whole nuts into butter or cream-like purees, like peanut butter or tahini. And they can go as a spread, into sauces, and more.

Especially when thinking about dressings, I love using nut creams. Most nut creams are fat enough to blend into an emulsion like mayonnaise. Mixing them with acidity and water-based liquids lets you easily create a smooth and creamy sauce. Unlike oil, with even higher energy and zero nutrients, a dressing based on nuts is rich in minerals and vitamins.

Special mentions

Seeds are slightly more nutrient dense than nuts. But there are some exceptions from both groups that are noteworthy for their nutrient profile.

Brazil nuts

Brazil nuts contain a super high amount of selenium. On average, you need two nuts to cover your daily needs. The content of selenium, however, fluctuates so much (from 0.2mg to 512mg14) that with four nuts, you can both miss your recommended daily intake of 55µg and overshoot your upper limit of 400µg.

I see two consequences from this: Don’t binge eat brazil nuts. Some brazil nuts here and there surely won’t harm, but if you snack on many of them daily, it can lead to nausea and vomiting. Then, don’t rely on Brazil nuts as your sole selenium source. Whole grains and mushrooms stably deliver fair amounts.

Chia seeds

Chia delivers one of the best fat profiles and kills it in many essential minerals and vitamins. Adding just one spoonful of chia seeds to your daily meals already puts the checkmark on your essential fats. We will look deeper into the fat profile of certain foods in chapter 3.

Vegetables

“Eat your greens!”

Who hasn’t heard that sentence? Parents, doctors, and fitness trainers alike pray to eat your vegetables. Vegetables are undoubtedly healthy, and you should eat plenty of them. The energy density of vegetables typically ranges from 10 kcal (salad) to 70 kcal (potatoes) per 100g.

Generally speaking, most vegetables contain high amounts of water and therefore are low in energy while still nutritious. They do differ in their nutrient profiles a lot, though. That’s why you find them organized in families. It is not necessary to remember all the details about each food group. It is more about getting a feeling for the variety in them. I recommend you go for them; they are all good for you!

Here I want to highlight food groups that stand out from the others thanks to their properties making them unique.

Starchy root vegetables

Potatoes and Sweet potatoes contain high amounts of starch and therefore are energy-rich. You can consider them closer to legumes and grains than to a salad. This doesn’t mean you should not eat them. But don't consider them contributing to the amount of fruit and vegetables consumed due to their high caloric impact.

Leafy greens

Spinach, kale, rucola, chard,... Leafy greens are a fair source of minerals and vitamins. Spinach itself, however, contains oxalic acid, which binds complexes with the minerals iron, zinc, and calcium and hinders their absorption. Despite the rumors, spinach is just an “okay” iron source. Kale, for example, is way lower in mineral-binding compounds and fits this purpose better.

Cruciferous vegetables

Cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, and more carry more protein than other vegetables. They contain high amounts of carotene, vitamin K, vitamin C, and minerals like potassium, iron, calcium, and zinc. But what distinguishes cruciferous vegetables from others is their high content of the secondary plant substance glucosinolate15, known for its preventive effects against cancer.

Glucosinolate is a substance that needs to be activated by a plant enzyme. You can make that happen by cutting the vegetable or consuming it raw. The more time you give your broccoli or Brussels sprouts after cutting, the better glucosinolate can develop. Forty minutes are ideal, but it’s not a “must.”

Start your cooking session by cutting those vegetables first and then moving on with the rest of the preparation. Depending on what you do, this gives plenty of resting time.

Once in your body, glucosinolate stimulates your detoxification enzymes. In other words: eating those greens is a natural body detox. Prolonged exposure to heat, however, diminishes the contents of glucosinolate again. A hot and sharp fry is recommended here. Like that, you also preserve most of its crispiness. And nothing’s more boring than over-boiled broccoli.

Onion vegetables

Onions, garlic, scallions, and more all belong to the same family, the allium genus. They are particularly associated with strong preventive effects against certain cancers, especially in the gastric tract16 and prostate cancer17.

When cut or chewed freshly, onion vegetables produce allicin by activating its corresponding enzyme, alliinase. This substance is responsible for the preventive and anti-inflammatory effects.

While you can rely on the same strategy, “cutting and waiting” with garlic and onions, you won’t need to wait as long as for cruciferous vegetables. Ten minutes allow enough allicin to be built.

Allicin is not very heat resistant and vanishes over long cooking times18. After only 6 minutes, its content is reduced to one-third; after 10 minutes, allicin can no longer be measured.

That most likely is bad news for people who love starting cooking by frying off some onions. Add raw onion or garlic to the finished dish to activate more allicin. All you need is an active enzyme to do the job on the already-cooked food. Sprinkling chopped scallions or spring onions over a finished dish adds allicin back to the picture. And indeed, it makes the plate look better, too.

Fresh herbs

Fresh herbs are absolute powerhouses of nutrition. Their micronutrient concentration is denser than regular vegetables, so adding fresh herbs to your meals is always good. 100g of parsley theoretically would overshoot your vitamins C, A, and K and deliver 80% (m), or 60%(f) of your dietary iron needs.

Use fresh herbs frequently to bump up your micronutrient supply and deliver a fresh taste. I recommend combining some bright aromatic herbs like parsley, basil, or cilantro with a bite of onion.

Mushrooms

Technically, mushrooms don’t belong here, as they are neither plant nor animal, but a fungus. But they often get treated like a vegetable, so let’s follow this trend.

With mushrooms, you get a very low-calorie food, often at around 30-50kcal per 100g, while delivering around 3-5g protein at the same time. They are also a rich source of B-Vitamins (except for B12) and minerals like copper and zinc.

Mushrooms tend to have a bit more glutamate naturally, thus the taste of umami. If well prepared, you get the chewy texture and the umami taste out of them, making them a great candidate for meat replacements.

You find similar nutritional value in nutritional yeast (also called Nooch). While mushrooms make a great meat replacement, nutritional yeast reminds of intense cheesy flavors. Sprinkle some nooch over your pasta as a cheese replacement. Add it to a bechamel sauce to make your next nacho dip. Garnish salads with it. The possibilities are almost infinite.

Algae

Algae, especially kelp algae, are a great addition to your diet if you don’t eat fish and seafood. First, they as well deliver an excellent energy to nutrients ratio. Notable highlights are their vitamin K and folate contents, but they also contribute generously to minerals like iron, calcium, and magnesium.

Then, they are very interesting for their natural iodine contents. Iodine is THE trace element (mineral) lacking in large part of the human population. No wonder the WHO mentions iodine directly in their recommendations for a healthy diet. However, the amount of iodine in algae can fluctuate quite a bit.

To keep your iodine intake manageable, you should only buy algae from brands that measure the contents and print them on the package. If you pick random, you can easily reach your upper limit of roundabout 1100 micrograms.

Fruit

As you can imagine, fruit should always be part of a balanced diet. You can and should eat much of it. Variety is king, as each fruit comes with its highlights. There is one family of fruit worth special mention:

Berries

Berries can be considered to be the healthiest group of fruit. They are packed with vitamins, minerals, and fiber. Blackberries, blueberries, and raspberries show off skyrocketing high amounts of antioxidants19. But all berries are good at that, which reduces your system's oxidative stress. That means they positively impact your metabolic disease risk, diabetes, obesity, and Alzheimer's.

Fruit juices

While fruit juices contain high amounts of vitamins, they lack fiber. Yet, their sugar content is usually high, so a glass of orange juice can have more calories than a glass of cola. The blood glucose levels also rise in the same way20. In other words: juices are fast energy, combined with some vitamins.

Since juices can be highly caloric and get absorbed fast, I do not recommend drinking them over thirst. Like this, you can pile up quite some calories without even knowing. Instead, I recommend having them alongside a meal. The vitamin contents round up what’s in the meal, and fruity acids, especially vitamin C, help absorb minerals like iron, calcium, and zinc from the main dish.

Smoothies

A smoothie is a drink made from whole fruits. Compared to juice, you have no loss in processing. Thus you get the same amount of nutrients as if you were eating the fruits in their whole form.

Don’t rate a smoothie as a refreshing drink. You can easily hit 300 kcal with a glass full, depending on what fruit it contains. And compared to the whole fruit eaten, the nutrients are easier absorbable and won’t keep you as full and satisfied as eating the fruit in its entire form.

Nevertheless, smoothies are a great way of consuming fruit. If you acknowledge their fast absorption, you can counter that by slowly drinking your smoothie bit by bit instead of chucking it down.

For example, I often bring smoothies as breakfast to work: I slowly sip on my smoothie alongside eating a sandwich. This keeps me satisfied and concentrated until lunch.

Besides fruit and vegetables, you can also sneak in some other nutritious foods. I toss some chia, oats, and sesame seeds. With chia, I cover my omega 3 (see next chapter). At the same time, all three are excellent sources of minerals, which get even better absorbed in a vitamin-rich and acidic environment.

Wrapping it up

Eating from each food group once daily gives you a rock-solid foundation for a balanced diet. You learned how each group contributes to your health and why you should get as much of them in their whole form.

If you consider this and manage to replace more processed food from your plate with fresh alternatives, your healthy lifestyle grows stronger and stronger. Let me remind you again: It should never be the perfection that we seek. If your overall lifestyle and diet are based on whole foods, those processed extras won’t do no harm. As always, moderation is key.
