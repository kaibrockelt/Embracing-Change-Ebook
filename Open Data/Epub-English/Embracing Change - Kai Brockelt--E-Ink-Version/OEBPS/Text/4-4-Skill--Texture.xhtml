4.4 Skill:

Texture

Humans. Love. Texture. There’s no way around it.

Imagine you go to a place and order crispy fries, as it is stated on the menu. The anticipation is intense, but you get some soggy potato slices when the food arrives. The chef must have missed the point. Those fries were different from what they were promised.

Our sensitivity to texture is super high. When you put a fork of food in your mouth, you can easily distinguish the mix of textures. You can feel the poppiness of pears, the crunch of fries, and the creaminess of a dessert. And if your food has a hair or grain of sand, you surely will find it when this bit enters your mouth. The same can happen when you encounter an unexpected fishbone. Once you bit it, your appetite for the moment is over.

We do have expectations towards textures, which are highly cultural. In the Western world, we are pretty much limited to a few accepted textures: Crispy, creamy, crunchy, and maybe melty are the typical things you can find on a restaurant’s menu, and you know exactly what you should expect.

If, however, you happen to find something, let’s say, squishy, the Western audience often would ditch it, while in Asia, on the other hand, squishy belongs to the section of good things. When the texture of a food doesn't meet the expectation, it can turn the entire experience of the food into a nightmare.

Why is this important?

Texture is part of our enjoyment of eating. It plays a vital role in creating flavor. You affect how you experience food's taste, aroma, and overall sensory perception through texture.

Adding variety in texture can uplift a good meal to an excellent one.

Texture and taste play together

Besides being an experience on its own, texture strongly influences the perception of taste. In short: the closer the texture of a food is to liquid, the more intense the taste gets.

When food consists of big crumbles, it cannot interact with all your taste buds. A fluid sauce or melty ice cream however paints a layer of taste right across your whole tongue and all taste buds can engage.

Use that to your advantage. Anything with a decent punch in flavor and aromas should wrap around your tongue like a blanket. Make it creamy, melty, saucy - fluid - to give it the best plateau for presentation.

Bring the texture variations in things that do not play the main role in taste. Toasted bread, seeds and nuts over a salad, crispy vegetables or crunchy croutons in a soup. Poppy blueberries on icecream, or crunchy cocoa nibs as a finish.

While taste and aromas bring the foundation of the flavour on the table, it is the contrast in texture that makes a good dish great.

More texture than just creamy and crunchy

You should try to consider the texture an ingredient adds to the mix. While all tastes and aromas are already satisfied, sometimes it is just that different bite that’s missing, that little extra texture that makes the whole experience better. Let’s not only focus on the well known creamy and crunchy, but make use of other things too, to get variation on your plate.

  • Poppy: Pomegranate seeds, blueberries, mustard seeds, grapes, small tomatoes
  • Chewy: Mushrooms, Meats, soft toast bread, firm tofu
  • Flaky: Coconut flakes, puff pastry, soft oats
  • Crunchy: Chips, nachos, toasted bread, fries, panko, seeds & nuts
  • Crispy: Bell pepper, celery, apples…
  • Spongy: soft tofu, pancakes, some mushrooms, cake
  • Fluffy: hummus, cotton candy, whip cream, potato mash
  • Creamy: Ice cream, peanut butter, cheeses, yoghurts, avocado mash
  • Slippery: some mushrooms, seaweed, seafood, silken tofu
  • Melty: Ice cream, chocolate

Like you can round up aromas with fresh herbs, you can also use them to add more texture. Don’t chop them to a pulp, but leave some bigger chunks. First, they are a wonderful invitation to play with, then they release good amounts of aroma when bitten.

To round up the picture, you can almost always add some sort of seeds. Think of sunflower seeds, sesame, flax, chia, pumpkin seeds, crushed walnuts or peanuts. Most dishes, both sweet and savory, can get a good texture upgrade through healthy crunch.

Mushrooms: Texture heaven or hell

One food that can turn either soggy, or incredibly crisp and chewy are mushrooms. Many people hate mushrooms because of their texture. But maybe they never tried well-prepared mushrooms.

If you only know mushrooms as slippery alien-like protein slices, they most likely have bathed too much in their juice during preparation. This happens quickly when the pan is too full when frying.

As most mushrooms carry much water, which they release when heated, a too-full pan can quickly accumulate a small lake of mushroom juice. Instead of frying, we boil the mushrooms and lose any chance to crisp and bite.

The first thing you can do is to cut bigger pieces of your mushroom. Not only is a big chunk to bite off from a chewy and meaty experience, but the mushrooms will also release less water, or at least at a lower speed. With less surface to release liquid from, the less likely a lake can build. Also, most mushrooms shrink when they are heated. Consider the shrink.

Second, ensure your pan is properly hot before adding oil and ingredients. The food should sizzle properly right after being added to the pan. This counts for any pan frying you could do: meats, fish, vegetables, rice, beans, or even pasta.

The instant heat allows the Maillard reaction to start quickly, which can seal off the surface of the food and build a shielding crust. The juice of the food then stays better inside, which also preserves its original texture better.

Third, don't overfill your pan when you expect it to get juicy, like with mushrooms. Ensure there is enough free surface that can evaporate leaking liquids immediately. Texture variation never harms.

To further improve an already great-tasting dish, consider the textures you put into it. Try finding a good contrast. If you got something creamy, add something with crispiness or crunch. Try toasting bread only from one side. You will enjoy fluffiness and crispness in one bite.

Consider the cooking or frying time of mixed ingredients. Anything that is supposed to stay crisp and firm should not cook for too long. Add the most fragile ingredients towards the end. Especially when frying rice or lentils, be careful not to add too much liquid to the pan. Let the stuff dry properly before frying. Too much liquid allows the grain to continue soaking, making everything sticky.

The full flavor checklist

Once you get a grip on creating texture, you have all the tools to craft your dishes by following the simple rules of taste, the aromatic scale, texture contrast, and, if you like, bite.

With this knowledge applied, you can create meals both healthy and tasty. While cooking, you can go through the five flavors and see what might be missing. Use your nose to test if a new spice would fit the rest on a scale. And be aware that there is more than just crunchy and creamy.

To give you a head start with this approach of cooking, I created the full flavor checklist. You can use this sheet as a scorecard:

Try fitting as many food groups as possible into the dish. Note down your ingredients and make a checkmark. You might also check a field in another group with each food you add: Each food can bring aromatics, taste and texture to play.

The idea now is to make as many check marks as possible without overloading the dish with ingredients. The fewer ingredients you use to fill more checkmarks, the more precise and intentional you cook.

A great example of this is hummus: you only need chickpeas (legumes), lemon (fruit), tahini (nuts & seeds), garlic (vegetable), ground cumin, and olive oil.

With this alone, you already have four checkmarks on the food groups. Lemon brings sweetness and sourness to the mix and adds a bright aroma to the play. Cumin is the low counterpart to that. Tahini and chickpeas bring small amounts of bitter, umami, and mid-tone aromatics.

If you eat your hummus with bread, you add the 5th food group and a set of textures, and your good eating Checklist is filled.
