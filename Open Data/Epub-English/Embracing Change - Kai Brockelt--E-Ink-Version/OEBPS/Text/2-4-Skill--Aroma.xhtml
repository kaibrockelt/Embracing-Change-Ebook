Aroma

Aromatics or aromatic compounds are chemicals we can sense with our noses. When discussing aromatics, we usually mean the spice, herb, or food that tells the story of food or scent. We give aromatics direct names and say things like “this tastes like Vanilla/Chocolate/Basil/Lemon/Tomato/etc.,” – or we can describe them by their mood or tonality like “bright, mellow, heavy, light, dark, deep, etc.”.

Since there are hundreds of different aromatics and even more combinations, it is impossible to create an overview or a “map” of which aroma fits which. Besides, the perception of “good” tasting food is still somewhat subjective. Yet it is possible to find systematic ways of making a match.

Why is this important?

While taste is the foundation of a good flavor, putting aromatics together tells the whole food story.

This chapter teaches you how to make sense of the wide variety of aromatics to pick from by putting them into a scheme. You will learn how to change the tone of spices and herbs to apply the same aromatic in various ways.

Once you get the grip of it, you can use spice more purposefully.

Making aromas stand out

Many aromatics are fat soluble. Ensuring a little fat in your dish can brighten up the whole experience. If your aromas seem dry and stay in the background, they either are old, or your dish lacks a bit of fat.

Both spices and dry herbs lose their aroma over time. This goes even faster if they are exposed to light and heat. If you keep a spice or herb around long enough, it can lose everything and remains with just the scent of dust or dry grass. If that happens, it's time to replace it.

Bulk buying a spice that you only use up slowly bears the risk of drying out and getting old. The longer you store it, the less attractive it will become, and you will use it up more slowly until the day comes when you might throw it away. Having small amounts of fresher dry spices and herbs available ensures that they have their full potential. Age and quality do make a drastic difference.

Categorizing aromatics

When smelling spices, you can recognize how some aromas appear quickly and leave quickly. Other spices take time to get picked up by your nose and slowly fade out afterward.

Putting two spices together that appear in the same timely manner in your nose will compete for your attention: If you smell two spices, where one shows quickly, and the other takes time, you can clearly distinguish their distinct scent.

Let’s try that out right away. Go to your spices rack and find a spice that slowly develops. I recommend cumin or paprika. Smell it. Take a lemon or lime, rub the peel, and smell it. Now, smell both at the same time. You should be able to distinguish the lemon and cumin clearly.

Try taking another low-tone spice, for example, cinnamon. Smell it together with cumin. You will have a hard time distinguishing those two aromas.

You can think of aromatics as a musical note that is part of an ensemble. A note can be high-pitched, rumbling low, or somewhere in the middle. Think of any music. A good song usually covers a range of tones between high and low while putting the main melody as a dominant part playing in one of the tonality bands.

If you put too many competing tones in one pitch, let’s say high, they start competing for your attention and produce noise. Leaving a big gap on the other hand quickly makes a song feel incomplete, dull, and boring.

Try composing something that contains a good variety from high to low. You add complexity by combining similar tones, so don’t limit yourself to one for each low, mid, and high. Of one or two spices you can add a little extra, to place your highlight. Again, balance is vital.

The same rule set applies when trying to match a wine. Wine should always play along with a dish you’re having. For a light and bright dish, people tend to go for a white wine, while when trying to match a deep wintery food like a roast, people tend to go for a dark red wine.

But why is that so? The aromatic profile of a wine then matches the style of the dish you’re eating and supports it instead of creating a distraction or contrast to the dish. A matching wine will tickle similar neurons in your olfactory system, adding more detail to the overall experience.

Make the test!

Open your spices drawer and try to order or group spices together by their tone. Just open the lids and compare the smell of two spices or herbs. Close your eyes to shut down some senses and try to find the complexity of spice in its scent.

If you directly compare two aromas, you can clearly distinguish their tone and tell which one is rather high or rather low. Even if it may be hard to name an aroma just by its smell, you can quickly put it in its place on the scale from high to low.

Try smelling different foods and oils. Each natural food contains at least some aromatics, and you can also learn to recognize its tone and pungency. Many foods with low-intensity aromas give you creative freedom (pasta, oats, tofu, zucchini...), while other foods already reserve a dominant position in the dish ( most fruit, mushrooms, pumpkin, bell pepper...)

Over time you quickly learn to place an aroma by experience. If it helps, write them down on a scale or in groups. The more you memorize the tone of the aromatics you use, the easier and more conscious you can place them to create an ensemble of tones.

Taste and try-out

If you are still determining if a combination fits your cooking, take a spoonful from your dish aside and sprinkle some spices. Give it a couple of seconds to develop and try.

Like this, you can try out different combinations of aromas before giving the dish a final direction. Often you find perfect combinations in the unexpected. Surprisingly jam, fruit, or purees create bright contrasts, while cocoa or cinnamon bed an extra warm and low punch to savory dishes.

The Maillard Reaction

The Maillard reaction is a reaction that happens under high heat influence, where protein and sugar react with each other. You see the result of the Maillard reaction on the brown spots of fries, bread, and more. It is what crusts up the food when roasting and frying.

This reaction creates various aromatics in the food alongside umami from the broken-down protein. Did you ever wonder why people love toasted bread so much more? It’s because of the Maillard reaction. The lovely brown crust on lasagna or casserole? You guessed it: It’s the Maillard reaction. A good stir fry is when the food starts browning well enough without burning. Because we apply the Maillard reaction as widely distributed as possible.

We aim to create this chemical firework whenever we fry or roast food. Having something roasted in a dish adds a broad layer of flavor. Try to apply this to dishes that are not exclusively oven or pan dishes. Add roasted nuts and seeds to both soups and cereal bowls. Garnish your steamed veggies with fried onions.

Working with Spices

What is commonly known as a spice is either a piece of bark (cinnamon), seed (fennel), root (turmeric), or berry (peppercorn). Commonly dried and found in the form of finely ground powders. You can - and should - try to buy them in their whole form. Freshly ground spice unleashes a more complex and intense play than its pre-ground counterpart.

Dried spices need time to develop and release their aroma, so it’s recommended to introduce them early in the cooking session. Without time to release their aromas, spices leave the dish tasting blunt and lacking complexity.

Roasting whole spices

If you have whole spices, you can roast them before processing them. Why? The Maillard reaction again. Let me explain:

Imagine that an unroasted spice has only a few aromatic components in its variety but in a high amount, leading to high intensity. When at heat, protein and carbs collide to create the brown, aromatic layer; they crash and interfere with aromatic compounds surrounding the reaction. If you roast fennel, the aromatic compound responsible for its distinct taste transforms into various new aromas. The longer you toast it, the less intense the fennel aroma, but the wider the variety of recognizable facets.

Roasting whole spices before grinding adds complexity to the dish and evens aromatic spikes. I recommend experimenting with different combinations of roasted spices. As the roasting dampens the intensity and broadens the aromatic profile, smelling spices can still be used to pick the right combination before roasting.

Working with herbs

When putting herbs in your dish, you can use the same method described above to find their spot in an ensemble. If you compare herbs to spices, you find some key differences in how they develop their profiles.

Cooking with fresh herbs

If one thing can move a dish from very good to excellent, it is fresh herbs. There is barely any savory dish that does not ask for fresh herbs. Fresh basil on a lasagna or pizza. Rosemary on a Christmas roast. Dill and parsley on potatoes. Cilantro on top of guacamole or ramen. Chopped scallions on a stir fry.

You add a more complex profile to your dish when cooking with fresh herbs. Many aromatics are water soluble and evaporate during the drying process. You won’t find these aromas in their dried counterparts. You will also steam away them if you add fresh herbs too early to a dish. If you want to get the most out of it, sprinkle them onto the plate when done with cooking.

The way you cut your herbs also influences the way the aroma develops. The more finely you chop, the more juice can be released into the dish. If you chop too finely, most of it will end up on the cutting board. If you keep the bits a bit bigger, most aromas will be released when bitten.

Another way to release the aromas is by smacking, twisting, or pressuring the herbs. This is useful if you, for example, want to use a twig of rosemary during roasting but want to take it out again afterward easily.

In the end, it always depends on how you want to use your herb: If you want to mix it into something and distribute the aroma evenly, go for small bits. If you use the herbs as a garnish or want to work with their texture, go big. Smack a bunch if you want a note of the aroma, but not the whole story.

Cooking with dry herbs

Dried herbs are applied similarly to ground spices: you should add them early to give them time to soak and release their aromas. Since they are dry, their water-soluble fragrances are gone. What is left is the fat-soluble aromas in a concentrated form.

If you toss them on a finished dish as decoration, they show a dry and unpleasant texture and offer barely any character. Instead, add them early enough to mix, mingle and let their aromas develop properly.

Generally speaking, dry herbs are inferior to their fresh counterparts. With all the water-soluble aromas gone, they have a less diverse flavor profile to offer. But it also depends on what type of herb you are looking at.

If you think of herbs that grow in warm and dry regions, they naturally can resist dryness. Thus many of their aromatic compounds are fat soluble. If you take a herb that likes mild and humid climates, parsley, for example, most of its awe is gone during the drying process.

I do not recommend buying these herbs, as they are relatively disappointing if you know their fresh version. “Southern” herbs like thyme or rosemary that grow under the sun do find a place on my shelf, as they still develop a decent aroma if you give them enough time and introduce them early into the cooking.

Replacing herbs: dry vs. fresh

Many recipes work with dried herbs. If you want to replace them with fresh ones intentionally, you should not just replace them one by one following the recipe.

The aroma of dried herbs is far more concentrated. Put that into consideration and give way more fresh herbs to your creation when swapping. Three to fivefold is a reasonable estimate, depending on the dish. Remember to add them later to save their note.

Find inspiration around the globe.

This is where local cuisine gets interesting. There are unimaginable possibilities for combining different spices and aromatic compounds. If you have ever entered a spice market or store, you got a sense of what I mean.

The sheer amount of choice can be overwhelming in the beginning. Besides using your nose to put aromas side by side on the scale, you can find many great aromatic combinations around the globe. They are just a google search away.

Try spotting working combinations of aromas wherever you can. You see them in cookbooks and restaurants — social media, and blogs. Use already-known combinations to make a safe pick if you need more confidence creating independently. Copy-pasting is no shame.

For you to get started easier, I have created the “aromatic world map,” containing typical herbs and spices from various countries around the globe. Each country has several examples of spice, sorted into a low, medium, and high rows. When picking one of each, you create a fitting pattern. Use this to bend the dish in the desired direction.
