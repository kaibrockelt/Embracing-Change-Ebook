4.2 Knowledge:

Protein

All right! Let’s discuss protein!

Protein belongs to the macronutrients and is a group of building blocks we need to produce and maintain muscle, tissue, enzymes, DNA synthesis, and more.

Why is this important?

A proper protein supply is critical for maintaining good health, reaching fitness goals, and achieving muscle growth. While a well-designed protein intake is beneficial, there is much false fuzz around how much is needed.

Understanding the mechanics behind protein absorption and use helps you pick good protein sources and allows you to supplement appropriately if required.

What exactly are protein and amino acids?

Protein, peptide, amino acids. These terms are often used interchangeably but should be distinguished when discussing details. The base building block of protein is an amino acid, the smallest logical unit that can get combined into something bigger.

Amino acids typically can connect on two or three ends. This allows us to chain multiple amino acids together. If you connect more than one amino acid, you call it a peptide. And a peptide containing 50 amino acids or more is called a protein.

Depending on what our body needs to build, it requires mixing and mingling different amino acids in different proportions. For each purpose, a different set of acids is needed. We can build some amino acids from others, but some amino acids must be supplied through diet. Those amino acids, therefore, are essential.

For those who are interested: The essential amino acids are Valine, Isoleucine, Leucine, Phenylalanine, Threonine, Tryptophan, Methionine, Lysine (and in infants: Histidine).

But what if we lack one of the amino acids to build, let’s say, muscle fiber? When making furniture and lacking one part, we won’t be able to complete the thing. In nutrition, this means the amount of protein and the quality we consume are important. The biological value defines the quality of a protein.

The biological value explained

Generally speaking, all foods contain all amino acids in different proportions. We as human beings require amino acids in different amounts and compositions than, let’s say, a tree. The same applies to any other living being. A cow is built differently from a dog and is different from a bird.

Each form of life requires a different set of building blocks. The more similar the two life forms are, the more similar their protein needs. Naturally, if we eat muscle from a mammal, we can assume that the amino acids profile is close to what human muscle is built from. A plant, on the other hand, is constructed entirely differently, so its protein contents won’t align with ours as much.

In other words, a protein that matches our needs pretty well has a high biological value. One that does not match the composition gets rated as lower quality.

The biological value describes how well a protein matches our needs. The higher the number, the better it fits. The people inventing this indicator took eggs as a reference and gave it a score of 100.

Any protein source fitting our needs less than eggs will get a proportionally smaller number; anything that suits us better gets rated higher than 100.

Tweaking the biological value

The better the protein profile of food matches our needs, the higher the biological value. Naturally, meats, eggs, dairy, and other land mammal foods have a protein composition very similar to what we need. Of course, if, let’s say, we need to build muscle, we find the right building blocks in the muscle itself. Finding the same profile of amino acids in plants is more complicated.

Theoretically, you can still fill up your amino acid with a low-quality profile. Just scale your meal until the bottleneck is big enough to meet your needs. This, however, means you add many calories from protein without using them. Any amino acid not needed in the mix to build stuff is energy.

Since each type of food has a different amino acid profile, we can combine various protein sources to even out the spikes and bottlenecks. Grains typically have low amounts of the amino acid lysine, which then is the limiting factor for a high-quality protein.

If you take a legume like edamame, its amino acid profile looks completely different. While it has reasonable amounts of lysine, it lacks cysteine.

While each food individually has a low biological value due to its limiting amino acid (lysine, cysteine), you can even out their bottlenecks by combining those two foods. The high cysteine contents in grains make up for the deficit in legumes, while lysine from the legumes covers the gap found in the grain.

The amino acid with the lowest contents in the combined dish now defines the new biological value of the combined foods. Adding another amino acid profile to the mix can further increase this number.

Take nuts, for example. They again have different amino acid profiles, and combined with others, the protein quality rises even further. The more diverse you eat, the higher the biological value of your protein will be. It is not unusual to have a biological value higher than 100 when combining different protein sources.

If you look around the globe, you can find those combinations in many national cuisines. In Mexico, you get beans with rice on a burrito. In India, you combine lentils with rice in daal. East Asia combines rice with tofu a lot. In the middle east, you dip hummus (chickpeas, sesame) with flatbread made from wheat. In England, beans on toast are a typical breakfast. All those examples naturally mix different protein sources and create a highly valuable dish.

And there are so many more ways of combining. How about pasta with lentil bolognese? Or a quinoa salad with kidney beans. “Meatballs” made from rice and lentils… you name it.

Especially when following a vegan diet, it is crucial to combine different protein sources, as each plant food group individually does not bring high-quality protein. While hypothetically bringing fair amounts of protein, a one-sided diet can still be a bottleneck due to the biological value. So keep an eye on a suitable variety of whole natural foods throughout the day to keep the quality as high as possible.

How much protein do I need?

Depending on whom you ask, the baseline of the recommended daily protein intake is between 0.8 and 1g per kilogram of body weight66. Let’s assume you weigh 60 kilos. Then you would need between 48 and 60 grams per day. If you are overweight, you should reference your assumed ideal weight. You can use the BMI to estimate your ideal body weight. Give your height and play with the weight until you hit the normal zone. You can achieve this goal halfway through the day with a regular, not extra protein-rich, balanced diet. You exceed this threshold throughout the day, even when consuming lots of processed food. However, be aware that if you eat one-sided, your need for protein increases, as the biological value is low. Your protein demand rises if you are generally more active or try to gain muscle through sports. For cardio, multiply your intake by 1.2 and for muscle training by 1.5. Double your protein supply if you aim to be a bodybuilder or an athlete.

To calculate your protein need, you can use this formula:

[ baseline ] * [ body weight in KG ] * [ sport factor ] = [ daily protein requirement ]

If we calculate with 1 as the baseline, assume a body weight of 75 Kilos / 165 Pounds, and the goal to train muscles, the protein need can be calculated like the following:

[ 1 ] * [ 75 ] * [ 1.5 ] = [ 112.5g ]

Rating protein in a relative way

You can now start counting the grams of proteins in your meals and try to sum up to that number. That is a straightforward method, but it requires tracking what you eat.

This could be more pleasant. So I prefer looking at protein relative to calories. Simply put, your protein requirements and energy consumption are bound to your lean mass. If you build more muscle, your base metabolic rate and protein needs go up correlatingly. The same applies to your active energy burn. More sports mean more energy used and more repairs to be done.

If you know your rough caloric needs (see chapter 3, or use a calculator) and just calculated your protein requirement above, you also know your relative caloric need from protein:

[ Protein in grams ] * 4 / [ daily calories ] = protein threshold

So if we assume a diet of 2500 kcal for our 75 kg male subject with ambitions to gain muscle, we calculate the following:

[ 112.5g ] * 4 / [ 2500 kcal ] = 0.18

This means any food containing more than 18% calories from protein suits our diet and positively contributes to our daily needs. The higher a food is above this threshold, the more room it gives you to eat foods lacking protein.

You can use the same calculation to estimate any food's protein content. You don’t need to work with exact numbers here. Estimate to get an idea.

Now if you check your favorite foods you can easily find out on which side of the balance they stand. And instead of counting protein on a daily basis, you just need to get an overview of what you commonly like to eat.

If you now find out that the tofu you usually go for scores 40% calories made from protein (far above your threshold of 18%) and boiled basmati rice hits 9.5% (half of what you need), you also know that if you eat a wok with rice and tofu in equal amounts, your protein score will be at 24.75% ( [ 40+9.5 ] / 2 ). In other words: this meal provides you with more protein you need to maintain your daily goal.

So have it your way. Counting protein in grams or memorizing relative protein contents. Either way is a legitimate one to keep an eye on your protein intake. In the long run, this should be only a little relevant. Once you stick to a dietary pattern that works for you, you will automatically attach to what’s good for you as your new cooking becomes a habit and routine.

Supplementing protein

The hottest question is whether you should or should not supplement protein. But that’s not a simple yes or no answer. In the end, it depends on some factors.

First of all: how much protein do you need? Use a calculator or the formulas provided to find out. Then, find out how much protein your go-to diet offers on average. The primary goal should be to hit your caloric and protein goals with whole natural foods as far as possible.

Depending on your lifestyle, time, and skill, this can work out better or be hard to achieve. So if your situation doesn’t allow you to go total health due to a lack of cooking time, supplementing may be a convenient way of filling the gaps.

If you want to figure out the whole truth, screen your plate. Keep track of the protein contents of your meals for a week. You can do this on paper or use one of the many food tracker apps. If you see deficits at the end of the day, you can fill the gap with a supplement.

Don’t prioritize protein over other nutrients

Even with big goals, try to focus on your entire diet instead of only your protein. Lacking other nutrients through one-sided eating can as well hinder progress. An iron deficiency, for example, can make you feel extremely weak and tired67, and a lack of vitamins may weaken your immune system, which as well can hinder your routine.

But focusing on food variety, a good protein supply, and a balanced nutritional picture can go hand in hand. Regarding supplements, I suggest reaching for a whole-food-based product instead of isolates.

If you wonder whether you should supplement BCAAs

Typically the argument is that muscle needs many branched-chain amino acids. While this isolated statement alone is correct, it is also true that you need all essential amino acids to build muscle. While a higher availability of BCAAs may fill up one bottleneck, you will eventually hit the next limiting amino acid, which is not a BCAA.

It is wiser to go for a complete protein with a high biological value, as its overall mixture will still fit better the human need.
