6.1 Mind:

Alcohol

Alcohol, in its various forms, has become deeply ingrained in our social fabric and is often associated with celebrations, relaxation, and social gatherings. Many of us enjoy the occasional drink, but have you ever wondered how alcohol affects your health and nutrition?

While sipping on wine or enjoying a cold beer can be a pleasurable experience, it's essential to be aware of the caloric impact alcohol can have on our diets. Moreover, alcohol consumption can also lead to nutrient loss and damage within our bodies.

Why is this important?

While the foundation for a healthy lifestyle lies in providing what we need through diet and nutrition, we should also monitor how we can actively ruin it through intoxication.

Whether you're a casual drinker or simply curious about the science behind alcohol, this chapter will equip you with the knowledge you need to make informed decisions about your well-being.

What is alcohol?

Alcohol is the substance in drinks that makes you drunk. Its proper name is ethanol or ethyl alcohol. Due to its toxic and addictive properties, it is classified as a drug. Alcohol gets created when yeasts ferment sugars from grains, fruits, or vegetables. It acts as a depressant74, slowing down your central nervous system and brain functionality. This affects your judgment, perceptions, feelings, and senses. You can become irrational and distorted, and your motor skills get impaired.

The general recommendation for alcoholic drinks is not to consume them. Not at all75. The closer to zero you stay, the better it is for your health. General claims that “one glass of wine per day” or such “is good for your health” don’t find any scientific support.

You might stumble across one of the many countries’ recommendations about “safe limits of drinking”; those act as a limit to mitigate bigger harm, not as a recommended intake you should follow. In short: each drop of alcohol you avoid drinking is good for you.

How does alcohol get metabolized?

Alcohol is a water-soluble substance that quickly transfers from the gastrointestinal tract to the bloodstream. As it follows the principle of diffusion, it distributes into all tissues and fluids from here. Unlike fatty substances, alcohol can freely float through your bloodstream and requires no transport protein from a to b.

Once roaming our body, alcohol binds to our central nervous system's GABA (Gamma-Aminobutyric acid) receptors. GABA is a neurotransmitter that decreases cellular excitability. Alcohol binds to the receptor, inhibiting the nerves' activity. This then leads to sedation, reduced coordination, and cognitive dysfunction. This is what makes you feel drunk.

Heavy drinkers develop increased numbers of GABA receptors, leading to alcohol tolerance. With more receptors, higher amounts of alcohol are needed to feel the same level of drunkness.

We have several pathways to break down alcohol76. The liver does most of the job with the help of two specialized enzymes. In the first step, alcohol gets broken down into acetaldehyde by the enzyme alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH). Acetaldehyde is a highly toxic and reactive substance and carcinogen capable of causing tissue damage.

Next, this substance gets further broken down by the enzyme aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) into the short-chained fatty acid acetate, which finally is a “common” substance in our body and not toxic: It can enter several natural pathways, and excess acetate leaves the body through our lungs. Acetate smells a bit like nail polish and is notable in your breath.

What influences how drunk you might become?

Several factors influence how strongly you are impacted by alcohol.

First, women generally have a smaller capacity to dilute alcohol relative to their body size due to a higher percentage of body fat. Alcohol dissolves in water, less relative water contents mean faster saturation. This will lead to higher peak blood alcohol levels in women if you give the same dose of alcohol per kg body weight to men and women.

These effects from diffusion and saturation also cause you to get drunk faster when you drink more potent drinks. The higher the alcohol contents, the bigger the gradient of diffusion and the quicker it can enter your blood. A shot of strong alcohol will lead to higher peak blood alcohol levels than the same amount of alcohol distributed across multiple drinks.

“Don’t drink on an empty stomach.” The presence of food in your gastrointestinal tract also slows down the absorption of alcohol. Both carbohydrates and fat hinder the speed of alcohol entering your bloodstream.

Finally, the level of blood alcohol is also influenced by how fast we can break alcohol down. Alcohol cannot get stored away and will remain in our bodies until eliminated. If you have low amounts of ADH enzymes at your disposal, the breakdown of alcohol can take more time. It influences how high the peak alcohol levels can become and how long it may take to recover from high levels fully.

There is a 3-4 fold variability in the rate of how fast humans can eliminate alcohol. Factors are

  • your sex: while women have a lower blood capacity for alcohol, at the same time, the elimination rate of alcohol is higher due to the smaller lean body mass in relative terms.
  • Your age: youngsters have less ADH; hence “apparently moderate drinking” can hit them like a truck.
  • Biological rhythms and body temperature can influence how well your body eliminates alcohol from your system.
  • A solid nutritional state also helps break down alcohol. A well-oiled machine runs better and gets the job done faster!
  • Certain drugs can inhibit ADH and mitochondrial respiration, which effectively brings the alcohol elimination rate down, too.

Short-term and long-term effects of alcohol on our bodies

Now that we have explored how alcohol is processed by our bodies and its effects on our metabolism, it is time to look at the impact it can have on our health. From the immediate risks associated with impaired judgment and coordination to the potential for chronic conditions that can affect various organs, it is essential to understand the consequences of alcohol misuse. In this section, we will explore how alcohol can negatively affect our physical and mental well-being.

The caloric impact of alcohol

The caloric impact of alcoholic beverages is often underestimated. Alcohol delivers a solid 7kcal per gram, almost as much as fat. A can of beer (12 oz, ~330-350ml) with 5%VOL alcohol already contains roughly 15g of pure alcohol. This alone accounts for 105 kcal per drink already. On top of that come calories from sugars and other carbohydrates, and you quickly hit 150kcal per can.

Cracking a couple of cans throughout one evening can soon add up to thousands of extra calories (6 Beers ~ 900kcal), making it challenging to keep a steady caloric balance.

Also, when switching drinks, you are not necessarily better off. A standard glass of wine (175ml or 6 oz) roughly has 130 kcal. Long drinks and liqueurs often contain additional calories from sugar or cream.

This strong caloric effect of alcohol is often overseen when talking about dietary patterns. Alcoholic calories are “empty” as they contribute to your caloric balance without delivering any mention-worthy nutrients. On the opposite, alcohol even hinders nutrient absorption, and drinking has the potential to ruin an otherwise well-balanced diet. Some alcoholics can take up to 50% of their daily calories from alcohol, completely neglecting much-needed nutrients.

Nutrient loss from drinking

Drinking only delivers energy without nutrients and actively works against a good supply of vitamins and minerals.

Alcohol, as a toxin, generally gets prioritized in its metabolism and slows down other metabolic pathways. This leads to malabsorption of nutrients. Alcohol inhibits fat absorption and thereby directly inhibits the absorption of the fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E & K.

Mineral absorption itself is not affected directly by the presence of alcohol. Still, diuretic effects lead to loss of calcium, magnesium, iron, zinc, and water-soluble vitamins through the kidneys. Deficiencies are common in alcoholics. These are causing a variety of medical consequences. (See Chapter 5.1, “Critical nutrients”).

How does alcohol damage our bodies?

Following the pathways of alcohol metabolism, we can see that alcohol has multiple ways to cause damage. On its way into the body, ethanol can damage tissue in our digestive tract and cause internal bleeding77. It negatively affects the microbiome's composition78, which can promote alcoholic liver disease and other diseases.

Once ethanol roams our bodies, alcohol binds to GABA receptors of our central nervous system. As a depressant, it demotes neuronal activity and slows down ongoing processes. Short-term results are dizziness, lower cognitive capabilities, sedation, and bad coordination - the typical feeling of being drunk. Drinking reduces your REM sleep and affects your memory formation. It can lead to stress and anxiety.

Typical symptoms of nutrient loss are night blindness (vitamin A), softening of bones (vitamin D), and slowed wound healing (Especially Vitamin K, but also A, C, D, E, and various B Vitamins are involved).

When broken down into acetaldehyde, the effects change. This substance is now highly cancerous, damaging your DNA and proteins. Higher concentrations cause more damage. Many long-term problems can develop, such as

  • High blood pressure and Heart disease
  • Cancer
  • Weakening of the immune system
  • Cognitive problems

Hangovers

If you ever had a wild night out, you know hangovers well. Symptoms include headaches, fatigue, dehydration, dizziness, sensitivity to light and sound, nausea, vomiting, and difficulty concentrating.

The severity and duration of a hangover can vary depending on factors such as the amount of alcohol consumed, individual tolerance, hydration levels, and overall health.

What is causing a hangover?

There is no clear picture yet of what is causing hangovers exactly. But it is believed to be a result of various influences of alcohol on the body. Those are, in the first place, dehydration, inflammation, and disrupted sleep.

Another theory is that the buildup of acetaldehyde is causing parts of the symptoms, as it is an aggressive oxidant.

Along with the dehydration from drinking comes a loss of electrolytes. Lacking essential minerals can be a plausible reason for some symptoms.

What to do for hangover prevention?

The most effective solution is also the most obvious: Don't drink alcohol. Or, at the very least, don't drink to excess. Drink slowly and not on an empty stomach. Besides this, there is no magic trick or recipe that prevents hangovers. The best prevention of hangovers is the prevention of alcohol.

What can you do to recover from a hangover?

To soothe the symptoms of a hangover, you should take care of the underlying causes of the hangover. Rehydrating and getting proper sleep is a good start for recovery. Given that alcohol leaves a lousy footprint on your nutrient supply, you should take care of filling things up again. Resupplying vitamins, minerals, and glucose is essential for normality to get back on track.

Things you can consume for recovery:

  • Bananas: Bananas are rich in potassium, which can help replenish electrolytes that may be depleted due to alcohol's diuretic effect. They are also easily digestible and can help settle the stomach.
  • Ginger: Ginger has anti-inflammatory properties and can help soothe an upset stomach. Ginger tea, ginger ale, or adding fresh ginger to meals can be beneficial.
  • Complex Carbohydrates: Foods like whole grains, rice, and oats can provide sustained energy and help stabilize blood sugar levels. They can also assist in reducing feelings of fatigue.
  • Leafy Greens: Leafy greens like spinach, kale, and arugula are rich in vitamins and minerals, including folate and magnesium. They can help replenish nutrient stores and support overall recovery.
  • Berries: Berries are rich in antioxidants, which can help combat oxidative stress caused by acetaldehyde. They also provide vitamins and fiber.
  • Coconut Water: Coconut water is a decent source of electrolytes and can aid in rehydration
