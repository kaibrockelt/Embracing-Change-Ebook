Replacing with whole foods

As of now, you know a lot about healthy eating and tasty cooking. Before I let you out in the wild again, I want to teach you a handful of day-to-day helpers in your kitchen. With these, you can replace potentially unhealthy ingredients with whole-food-based alternatives. This allows you to transform already learned recipes into a more beneficial variant of their own.

The examples introduced in this chapter are not the end of the line. Like in each recipe chapter, use them to learn the tricks, that you later can apply elsewere, too.

Why is this important?

As we have learned throughout our journey, most nutrition-related health issues are driven by salt, processed foods, extracts, fast sugar, and bad fat composition. Knowing how to create a similar alternative to highly processed foods by using whole foods, you can uplift the quality of your meals without cutting the joy of the experience.

How whole food replacements help your diet

Introducing whole foods into your diet can be a game changer, offering numerous health benefits beyond weight loss. By replacing certain products with whole foods, you can improve the nutritional value of your meals and make them more flavorful and satisfying. Whether creating new dishes from scratch or adapting old favorites, swapping out processed ingredients for whole foods is a wise choice that can be made easily with the help of online recipes and your pantry staples.

When replacing certain products with other foods, you aim for a benefit in multiple ways:

  • Fat swapping: when doing fat swapping, you aim to replace high amounts of saturated fats with healthier unsaturated fats. This is often the perfect opportunity to add omega-3 fats through seafood, flaxseed oil or chia.
  • Salt reduction: You often have a great lever to reduce salt, especially when replacing entire products. Many supermarket-bought products like dressings rely heavily on salt. You can do better.
  • Including more nutrients: if you replace a highly processed product with something more natural, you automatically onboard more nutrients.
  • Fat and sugar reduction: If you cannot simply “remove” ingredients from a recipe, replacing flours, oils, and refined sugar is a good idea. The caloric impact shrinks when swapping highly processed ingredients like sugar or fat with a less refined counterpart.

You need to be aware of, that when aiming to replace for nutritious value, you will not be able to hit the same taste 100%. If you expect things to taste 100% the same, you will be disappointed from swapping foods.

Hitting the exact same flavor by faking something, asks to create precise formulas of aromatic compounds, which you naturally will not find in grown foods. You either craft something artificial to imitate something 100%, or you go natural, aiming at a more nutritious goal.

Here, you can apply what you learned previously, and aim for a flavor of the same quality though taste, aroma and texture, rather than the exact same outcome. Mindset matters.

Replacing heavy cream

We all know and love dishes with a creamy or cheesy sauce, like pasta Alfredo or filled cannelloni. Most of these recipes require one thing for their smoothness: heavy cream.

Heavy cream is separated from raw milk. It consists of over 60% saturated fats. You find a good amount of fat-soluble vitamins in it, but it is, besides that, not very nutritious. When replacing heavy cream, we mainly aim for fat-swapping and adding nutrients. Most whole-food creams won’t whip, however.

For whipping, you can replace heavy cream with coconut milk. When doing so, you win nothing on paper, as coconut milk has an even higher saturated fat content. For vegans, however, this is still a good way of replacing cream for whipping, just without improving the fats.

Making heavy cream from cashews

You can blend some heavy cream from pretty much any nut. The downside of most is that they are either hard to combine (almonds) or come with intense aromas (pretty much all nuts). If it fits the recipe - go for it!

Cashews offer both a soft texture and a low aromatic profile. Similar to heavy cream, they contain around 60-65 % fat. That is ideal for delivering a similar creaminess.

Unlike cream, cashews are loaded with minerals. They contain high amounts of iron, magnesium, zinc, and copper. On the flip side, cashews contain only small amounts of calcium. You find 18g of protein in 100g of raw cashews.

All you need to do is boil some water and pour it over the cashews until they are covered. Let them soak for at least 20 minutes. The longer you do this, the easier they will blend.

Combine them with water or a plant drink in your blender until you reach the desired consistency.

At this point, the cream is relatively neutral in its flavor. You can spice it up and decide on either sweet or savory profiles. Add fitting spices to what you are making. Something with chocolate? Add some cardamom! What about cinnamon? Would it fit? Or add fresh basil, nutmeg, oregano, and white wine to make a creamy pasta sauce.

Making cream cheese from tofu

You can make a heavy cream or cream cheese replacement from tofu as well. The principle is similar. You blend the tofu with some water until it reaches the desired consistency. Its taste is more neutral than a cashew cream. So make sure to add good flavoring to it.

Tofu cream fits very well in savory dishes. Try flavoring it with a splash of lemon, Mediterranean herbs like thyme and rosemary, paprika, and cumin. Combine it with something umami loaded and fill it in cannelloni, lasagne, Mac and’s, and more.

Compared to cashew cream, tofu cream has less fat and more protein. It is a lighter alternative to cashew cream. It delivers decent amounts of calcium, iron, and zinc.

With tofu, you can create firmer creams than with their cashew counterparts. Blend the tofu with only a little water and see how it sticks. Add more water to the blend until you reach the desired consistency.

Creating dressings

Store-bought dressings often rely on one of two things to get enough flavor inside; high amounts of oil or sugar. Homemade sauces can easily be made with a better nutritional profile.

Cashew cream dressing

You can build a dressing from cashew cream as well. Add more liquid, acidity, and salt to give it a good texture and taste. Fresh dill, parsley, or spring onions give freshness through aromatics.

Tahini whip dressing

You can create excellent airy and light dressings from sesame paste or tahini. Mix a spoonful of tahini with a sprinkle of lemon juice and water. When blending this, you create a very light and bright tahini sauce.

Besides a decent nutty foundation from the tahini and some lemon aromas from the juice, the sauce stays relatively neutral.

Hence, you should add more to these ingredients. Add garlic, and add fruit or jams to the blend. Add spices like nutmeg, cinnamon, or cumin. Extend it with more water to create the desired consistency. You can make tons of different variations like this.

Similarly, you can blend dressings from other nut butter. Peanut butter, for example, also serves as an excellent base for a dressing. Get creative, follow your sense of smell, and create many surprisingly different flavors!

Cheese flavor

For cheesy flavor, one thing is especially popular with vegans: nutritional yeast or nooch. Nutritional yeast is a dry yeast you can buy as powder or flakes. It is packed with B vitamins. What makes nutritional yeast so popular is its cheesy aroma.

You can blend nooch with some cashews or almonds to create a vegan alternative to parmesan. Add it to creamy sauces like bechamel, cashew, or tofu creams to make a cheesy nacho sauce. Add some turmeric and paprika to it for the color to be more cheesy, and maybe a bit of salt and mustard, and we are good to go. Uncooked, nutritional yeast can be added to flavor things like pasta dishes, popcorn, or dips.

Replacing sugar

Many sweet recipes ask for tons of sugar. Pure sugar has no nutrients besides fast carbohydrates. Replacing it with healthier alternatives brings back the opportunity to benefit from vitamins and minerals.

Replacing white sugar with brown sugar or syrup usually makes no difference. Each of those are highly processed extracts that lack nutrients. If you want to fill the space with nutrients but keep a high level of sweetness, you can use either molasses or dates.

Molasses are made by boiling down the juices of sugar cane, sugar beets, or certain fruits. As the water vaporizes, the sugary contents are left with the other nutrients originally in the fluid.

Dates contain 90% or more sugar. As you use the whole fruit, you will consume all the other nutrients it naturally contains. Their mushy inside allows for blending a sticky texture in dry goods, which you can use to roll nut protein balls114 and alike. But also, regular cake doughs work out great with dates.

If the recipe allows, you can skip the high-sugar ingredients and work with sweetness delivered through berries, apples, cherries, and other ripe fruit. When a cake or similar has enough aromatics, texture, and traces of different tastes, sweetness only adds a bit to the entire experience.

Replacing Eggs

If you intend to live vegan, you might like these in particular. If you are not vegan, I see no reason to replace eggs. An egg or two per day can be consumed without increasing your cardiovascular risk, and it is an overall nutritious food.

When replacing eggs, you should think of what effect of the egg you want to replace. Eggs bring flavor, can help as a raising and binding agent, and give texture to a dough.

Egg flavor

The typical egg aroma is defined by sulfur, which is part of the protein in the egg white. You can add sulfur through sulfur-rich salts, like the black salt kala namak.

If you sprinkle just a little of this salt onto a slice of ripe avocado and bite it blindly, you believe you are eating a boiled egg. You can use this effect to give egg aromas to mayonnaises, sauces, and pancakes cooked like an omelet. Add some color through turmeric and paprika powder to round up the picture.

Egg as a raising agent

If you want to replace egg foam, you can use aquafaba. That is the water you find in canned chickpeas. Collect it and whip it like you would beat an egg white. After a short while, it builds up a firm white foam.

If you want to raise dough while baking, you can use classic baking powder. Add baking soda and some acidity like lemon juice to the dough to make it rise. Since it starts rising instantly, you should add these ingredients to the dough when you’re done mixing it.

Egg as a binding agent

You may not need to replace the egg, depending on what you make. Many classic doughs with flour, from cake dough to crepe, don’t require eggs for binding. If enough starch and gluten are present, the dough will bind all right, even without the egg.

To better bind things like burger patties, you can build an egg replacement from two tablespoons of chickpea flour mixed with two tablespoons of water and a teaspoon of starch. Mix the ingredients well, add kala namak if you like, and let it rest for 5 minutes before using.

Other great binders are flax or chia egg replacements. Blend two tablespoons of chia seeds or two tablespoons of flax seeds. Add four tablespoons of water to the mix and let it soak for 10 to 15 minutes to jelly up. You can also replace an egg with a mashed half banana if baking sweet goods.
