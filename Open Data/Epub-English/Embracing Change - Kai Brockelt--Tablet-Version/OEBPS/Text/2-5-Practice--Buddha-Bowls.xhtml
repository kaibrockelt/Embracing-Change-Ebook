Buddha Bowls

Buddha bowls, also known as nourish bowls or grain bowls, are a type of meal that typically consists of a base of grains (such as rice, quinoa, or noodles), topped with a variety of ingredients such as vegetables, legumes, and protein sources (such as tofu or chicken). The idea behind the bowl is to create a balanced and nutritious meal in a single container.

How to work with my Recipes?

The recipes in this course are not meant to be followed with surgical precision. They are written to give direction and guidance.

Change the measurements of the ingredients to your liking. Replace, add, or remove things from the ingredients list to practice improvisation and make it yours!

Buddha bowls often have an emphasis on plant-based ingredients and are visually appealing with a variety of colors and textures. They can be changed to fit various dietary restrictions or preferences, including vegan, gluten-free, and low-carb. Buddha bowls have recently gained popularity as a convenient and healthy meal option.

I love going for buddha bowls when I have no clue what to cook. You choose your favorite food items from each group, add some spice, and toss them on a plate.

There is no need to search for a fancy recipe because you already know the tricks to counter kitchen boredom with a more systematic approach:

  1. Cover the taste buds with all 5 tastes (chapter 1.4)
  2. Pick a variety from the food groups (chapter 2.3)
  3. Round up the picture through the aromatic scale (chapter 2.4)

Going full circle

We can use the buddha Bowls concept to create complete dishes easily. Taking the recommendation of the WHO for a healthy diet, we should eat all five whole food groups throughout the day: Grains, Legumes, Nuts&Seeds, Fruit, and Vegetables.

Now, what can happen throughout the day, you can cover through a single meal. This gives you a great head start and gives you room to eat less mindful choices elsewhere. It’s a win-win deal for health and pleasure, no matter how you turn it.

Preparing food from all food groups creates a highly balanced dish with various health benefits:

  • You get a balanced caloric base with fats, carbs, and protein from nuts, grains, and legumes.
  • You can benefit from a healthy fat profile. (more to that in chapter 3).
  • You build a complete protein (more to that in chapter 4).
  • The plate will be filled with vitamins and minerals.
  • High amounts of fiber and complex carbs aid your satiety and digestion.

Especially vegetables and fruit can and should be added plenty. Usually, they are low in calories, add plenty of vitamins and minerals and fill you with fair amounts of fiber. The WHO recommends at last 400g daily, so take this chance to make your dish count.

The general building plan of buddha bowls

Once you decide what items to pick from each group, the rest of the preparation is easy and straightforward. You always follow these five steps:

  1. Boil the grains and legumes, if needed.
  2. Fry, steam, or bake vegetables and fruit.
  3. Add aromatics to the base component.
  4. Create a sauce containing the missing pieces for taste and aroma.
  5. Fill the bowl with your grains, greens, and legumes. Stack everything else on top. Decorate with your favorite herbs and seeds.

And that’s it!

Making sense of taste and aromatics

As previously learned, we can always aim to cover all five tastes. Keep that in mind when picking your ingredients. Any gap filled brings you one step closer to a fully flavourful dish. Think the same way about your aromatics. Consider the aromatic notes of your ingredients.

Especially things with an intense scent should be addressed on the aromatic scale. Take note of what areas your ingredients cover, and try fitting herbs and spices to fill the scale.

Picking a sauce

A good sauce for a Buddha bowl can vary depending on personal taste and the ingredients in the bowl. You can always use the sauce as a gap filler regarding taste or missing aromas. First, think of what general foods your bowl is going to contain. Try identifying flavors and aromas on them. Think of what aromas and tastes you combine when you sauté or fry your main ingredients.

With that done, you know what tastes and aromas still might be missing. Use ingredients that fill the picture and combine them in a sauce. Popular options for a sauce include

  • Peanut Sauce: made with peanut butter (bitter), soy sauce (salty, umami), rice vinegar (sour), and ginger (spicy, sweet).
  • Tahini Sauce: made with tahini (bitter), lemon juice (sweet, sour), garlic, salt, and water.
  • Salsa: made with diced tomatoes (sweet), onion, cilantro, lime juice (sweet, sour), and jalapeno pepper (spicy).
  • Pesto-style sauce: Basil (bitter), lemon (sweet, sour), soy sauce (salty, umami), garlic. Works as well with other herbs. Get creative.

Let’s get down to bowl business

Okaaaay! Enough theory; it is time to create a bowl.

As a caloric base, I choose whole-grain rice and chickpeas. (legumes check, grains check) I pick arugula, cherry tomatoes, green asparagus, and beetroots from the vegetable group. As decoration, I take leek and parsley.

For fruit, Avocado, apple, and lemon will be my choice. Finally, for the nuts and seeds category, I sprinkle peanut crumbles and sesame on top of the bowl. Tahini, or sesame paste, will be used for the sauce.

With those ingredients, we already cover sweet and sour, and bitter tastes. Salt and umami can be added through a quick deglaze with soy sauce while pan-frying.

Thinking about aromatics

Regarding aromatics, we already cover medium to bright tones through lemon, apple, and peanuts. We should combine this with one of the scales from the aromatic world map.

I pick sumac and parsley from the Israel package to comply with the bright aromatics and open up the scale facing downwards by adding thyme, oregano, and sweet paprika. Garlic can be added to the mix for spice and more weight on medium-bright aromas.

I use paprika and oregano to get some warming notes into our caloric base, so I will fry the rice and chickpeas quickly in a pan, mixing in the spice with a dash of oil.

The rest of the spice goes into the sauce. Give tahini, garlic, lemon, and water into a small blender for this. Add sumac, a dash of salt, and thyme. Blend until smooth. Add more water to reach the desired consistency.

With every component prepared, the only thing left to do is to stack the dish. Fill the empty bowl with a mix of rice and chickpeas, side by side with arugula. Pour sauce over the side of the base, and stack the remaining vegetables on top. Decorate the plate with fresh herbs, seeds, and peanuts; we are ready to dig in.

Buddha Bowl

Prep time

10 minutes

Total time

30 minutes

Servings

2 Bowls

Course

Main

Cuisine

International

Calories

513 kcal

Equipment

Pots & Pans

Ingredients

Base ingredients

1 cup of whole grain rice (cooked)

1 cup of chickpeas (cooked)

1 cup of arugula / rucola

1/2 cup of cherry tomatoes (halved)

1 beetroot (diced)

1/2 cup green asparagus

1/2 apple (diced)

1/2 avocado (sliced)

Sauce and aromatics

1 tbsp tahini

1 tsp sumac

1 tsp thyme

1 tsp oregano

1/2 lemon (juiced)

2 tsp sweet paprika

1 garlic (crushed)

1 dash of salt

1 tbsp soy sauce

Decoration

parsley (chopped)

peanuts (crushed)

Instructions

  1. Boil the rice and chickpeas, rinse, and let them dry properly.
  2. In a pan heat some oil. Sauté the rice and beans together with paprika and oregano for around 3 minutes. Stir frequently.
  3. in a separate pan, saute the asparagus for around 1-2 minutes. Deglaze with a dash of soy sauce when done.
  4. Fill a scoop of tahini in a small mixer. Add lemon juice, sumac, thyme, salt, and garlic to the container. Mix thoroughly. Add water until you reach the desired consistency.
  5. Fill the rice, beans, and greens (arugula) side by side in a bowl.
  6. Pour the sauce bedding over your grains and legumes mix.
  7. Top up with fried and diced vegetables.
  8. Decorate with parsley. Toss crumbled peanuts and sesame seeds over the plate.
