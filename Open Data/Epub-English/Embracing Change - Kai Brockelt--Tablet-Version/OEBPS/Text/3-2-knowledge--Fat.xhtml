3.2 Knowledge:

Fat

Fat is the macronutrient with the highest energy density. It brings solid 9kcal/g on the scale. We save excess calories in the form of fat for later and use it in the ketogenic pathway when we run low on carbs.

Fatty acids, however, do not only get used for energy. They are essential building blocks for hormones, membranes, and more. Therefore, keeping an eye on their energetic value and composition is significant, as they play critical factors in our health.

Why is this important?

Fat is highly related to many health aspects. The right fats can prevent disease, while a bad fat profile can promote health conditions.

It is crucial to understand the different types of fats and to pick health-promoting foods over foods that are known to cause problems in the long run.

What is fat?

Technically speaking, “fat” is any water-repellant substance. In the body, this can mean hormones and fat-soluble vitamins, but most commonly, we mean fatty acids. You can distinguish between different types of fats: Saturated, unsaturated, and trans fatty acids. Within the unsaturated fats, you find essential fats, the poly-unsaturated fatty acids omega three and omega six.

Saturated, unsaturated, and trans fats

To understand the differences between the types of fatty acids, we need to look into the basics of chemistry. No worries, we won’t overdo it. When remembering the periodic system of the elements from school chemistry, we know that each atom tries to reach a state with a complete electron set. For most elements, this means they want to have 8 electrons around them, except elements that are too small to carry 8. By binding to other atoms, they can reach a complete set of electrons surrounding them.

A fatty acid is a long chain of carbon and hydrogen atoms with a chemically active group on one end, with which it can bind to things. Carbon is an atom that holds 4 electrons but wants to have 8, while hydrogen only carries one electron and wants to have 2.

This allows a constellation where the carbon atoms sit side by side, sharing one of their atoms with their neighbor each, while the remaining free electrons are shared with a hydrogen atom. Like this, a very long and stable chain known as saturated fat can be built. Due to their stick-like structure, saturated fat is usually very stable and solid at room temperature. Typical examples of foods high in saturated fats are butter, and tropical oils like palm and coconut fat.

If there is not enough hydrogen to build the bond and two neighboring carbon atoms lack one of their hydrogens, they can share two electrons instead. Like this, they obtain 8 electrons and are in a stable bond again.

Now if both neighboring hydrogens around the double bond sit on the same side, they repel each other and bend the chain. Like this, you have unsaturated fat. If you shift the hydrogen atoms on their opposites of the bond by either heat or chemical influence, you have created trans fat. Trans fats follow a similar structure to saturated fats, yet their chain is slightly shifted.

When you find one double bond in a fatty acid, you call it mono-unsaturated fat. If you have more of those bonds in one chain, we call them poly-unsaturated. You find two essential fats in this group: omega three and omega six fatty acids. Unlike other fats, our body cannot build them; you must supply them through diet. Unsaturated fats are liquid at room temperature. You find them in various common kitchen oils like olive, sunflower, canola, sesame, and more.

Omega 3 and omega 6

Omega 3 and 6 are two groups of poly unsaturated fats, as they have more than one double bond in the long carbon chain. One particular thing they share is that the same enzymes process them. They, therefore, compete in their processing, whereas a balance too high on the omega-6 side can cause adverse side effects21. Both of them are considered essential, meaning we cannot produce them and need to provide high enough amounts though food.

The omega three fatty acids are a sequence of 3 fatty acids, where the body can “upgrade” one acid to another. The chain starts with the alpha-linoleic acid ALA, which can exclusively be found in plant fats. It can be transformed to eicosapentaenoic acid EPA and then further on to DHA docosahexaenoic acid.

EPA and DHA are associated with vessel widening, anti-inflammatory effects22, and good brain function23. They furthermore can have preventive effects on kidney diseases, high blood pressure24, and more. They lower overall and LDL cholesterol by promoting “good” HDL production25. You find both in omega-3-rich foods like seafood, fish, and algae. Lack of omega three is associated with neurological issues like depression, Alzheimer and ADHS26.

Omega six synthesis works the same way. It starts with linoleic acid LA, which can be found in both plants and animal products, and is transformed into arachidonic acid AA, which is exclusively found in animal products.

The intake of omega-6 is often associated with lowering LDL cholesterol. However, science still needs to be made clear about it, and the effect comes more from reducing saturated fats27 when swapping with omega 3 and 6, according to more recent studies.

The tricky part about Omega 3 and 6 is that the same enzymes synthesize them. Taking high amounts from one can block the processing of the other. Therefore, a too-high omega six intake can reverse omega three's positive effects by blocking the synthesis chain. A too-high intake of omega 6 in proportion to omega 3 can have inflammatory effects28.

The ratio between omega 6 and omega 3 should be 4:1 or lower. Typically for a Western diet are ratios of 20:1 or higher. Keeping an eye on your intake of omega fatty acids can be very beneficial.

What are triglycerides?

When transported or stored, fat usually shows up in the form of triglycerides. That’s mainly a glyceride molecule capable of holding three individual fatty acids. We find fat in this form in our food, too, in both animal products and oils from plants.

Triglycerides are an important key figure in our blood. High levels can indicate obesity and are associated with atherosclerosis, cardiovascular disease, and more, even without high cholesterol. You want your total blood triglyceride concentration below 150 mg/dl29.

But why is that so? The more fat circulates in your blood, the gooier it will become. Thus, your heart will have difficulty transporting blood through the tiny vessels in your body. Consequently, it will beat harder, and your blood pressure will rise.

Cholesterol

Cholesterol is a fatty substance that is a critical element in building the membranes of our cells. It needs to get transported from the liver to its destination through the blood. Naturally, fat and water won’t mix well. Therefore cholesterol gets transported in a transportation protein, a so-called lipoprotein. A low-density protein is used for transporting fresh cholesterol to its destination, while a high-density lipoprotein is the transport of choice for the return.

The liver can synthesize all cholesterol needed by itself, but you also find dietary cholesterol in animal products like eggs, meat, and dairy.

Depending on where you look, you may find hard limits for dietary cholesterol of around 300 mg/day. Other countries recommend keeping the intake as low as possible without naming a fixed threshold. Your liver contributes to your total cholesterol by synthesizing around 800 mg. The more you take in your diet, the less will be produced.

Most people have an excellent response to cholesterol intake and adapt to higher amounts; the blood cholesterol levels respond more to high amounts of saturated fat and fat in general. If your overall blood cholesterol is already high or you have other metabolic conditions like diabetes, lowering your dietary cholesterol and saturated fat is recommended30.

If your overall cholesterol counts higher than 200 mg/dl, this is an alarming signal, and you should consult a doctor. The okay range is from 200 to 150 mg/dl. This cholesterol level is considered fine unless you have other risk factors involved. Suppose you keep your overall cholesterol levels below 150 and LDL below 75 mg/dl. In that case, the likelihood of developing CHD practically is stopped31 and can even reverse previously built-up plagues32.

The influence of dietary fats on your cholesterol levels

While high blood cholesterol is dangerous, dietary cholesterol is not the main driver. The most negative impact on your blood cholesterol levels comes from trans fats.

In the second place, you find saturated fats. You find high amounts of saturated fats in meats, cheese, dairy, and eggs. Moderation here is key if you want to eat them. Compared to those drivers, dietary cholesterol only plays a small part in your LDL and overall cholesterol levels33.

Mono-unsaturated fats, as you find in high amounts in avocados, olive oil, or sunflower oil, barely touch your cholesterol levels. Omega 3 acids, however, can actively push your cholesterol levels down.

In short: To get the most out of your fats, you should keep your overall fat intake low while focusing on eating unsaturated fats, especially omega-3 fatty acids.

Let’s wrap it up

All right! Let’s see what we know: According to the WHO, we should eat less than 30% of our daily calories from fat and less than 10% from saturated fats. Then, we should ensure we get our omega fatty acids, with a 4:1 or better ratio between omega 6 and 3. It can’t be too hard, right?

To achieve healthy blood fat and cholesterol levels, keep an eye on your overall caloric intake and the quality of fats. Excess calories ultimately transform into body fat, regardless of origin. If you live balanced, you should hit your calorie goal and be “on target.”

The rest is fat composition. A significant change can already be made by picking suitable oils. But then it’s a question of moderation. Cheese and meat can be part of a balanced diet too. Ultimately, the question is about the fats themselves, not the foods you find them in.

It’s impossible to give a piece of final advice on how to compose your foods, how much cheese, meat, and eggs you can eat, without respecting your taste, dietary needs and lifestyle. Instead, I ask you to look into your habits and estimate your fat composition.

So let’s look a bit deeper into food that’s rich in fat.

Oils, margarine and butter

Especially with oils, you should be careful what you pick for what purpose. The more unsaturated and especially poly-unsaturated fat oil contains, the easier it burns. Besides the fire risk, unsaturated fats get shaken up and produce trans-fatty acids. We have learned that they are incredibly unhealthy and harmful to our hearts.

Therefore, you should take an oil or spread rich in saturated fat for high-temperature frying. Moderation here is vital not to spike your calories and saturated fat footprint. If you want to fry deep, pat your food dry with kitchen paper and remove excess fat afterward.

Coconut or sunflower oil, for example, are super heat resistant and can be used for frying. You can find sunflower oil under the name “frying oil” as well. Just check the ingredients list to make sure.

Olive oil is ok for medium-temperature pan frying. Its smoke point is at 190-206° C / 374-405°F. It is rich in monounsaturated fats and barely impacts your blood cholesterol.

Oils rich in omega 3 and 6 should not be used for frying at all. Instead, use them on salads or sneak in a spoonful to put a checkmark on those omegas. Cold-pressed canola oil brings an excellent 2:1 ratio. Flax oil, however, is king, with a ratio of a whopping 1:4.

Theoretically, you can counterbalance bad fat with oil or seeds rich in omega-3. But this gets pointless if you shoot up your overall fat footprint. So rather be mindful.

Nuts & seeds

Generally speaking: all nuts are wonderful sources of unsaturated fats and bring you many vitamins and minerals. You should snack on them frequently, but in moderation.

On average, 70% of their calories come from fat. Since they are hard to digest, not all their calories reach our blood flow. Therefore, adding nuts and seeds to your meals or having them as a snack is always good. Just ensure not to use them as your primary energy source during the day.

Regarding omega fatty acids, you should focus on walnuts, chia, and flax seeds. Walnuts deliver omega 6 and 3 in a 4:1 ratio, so they are on the spot. One hand full of walnuts can already cover your daily need for essential fats. Snack them in between, add them to your cereals, or crumble them on salads. There are so many ways of incorporating them into your diet. Have them at home and consume frequently.

Like their corresponding oil, chia and flax seeds are natural omega-3 boosters; add them to your breakfast cereals or use them in salads. Make them your foundation of healthy fats.

Keeping flax seeds at home is highly recommended. They are cheap, widely available, and can be stored very long. Flax seeds, however, are hard to digest; if you don’t chew them properly, you won’t be able to access all of their goodies. To ensure you can access the good oils, you should grind or blend them.

Once ground, light and heat can quickly destroy the essentail fats. I recommend having a sack of whole flax seeds at home and grinding the amount needed for a week. Just transfer them to a jar and store them in the fridge. You then pick what you need and return the container to protect the oils. You can also buy ground flax seeds directly, but usually, they are overpriced. Just get some cheap flax seeds and do the job yourself. It takes you two minutes.

Fish & seafood

Seafood and fish are widely associated with healthy fat profiles. This is true, as fish are a rich source of fatty acids, specifically omega-3 fatty acids. Their benefits include reducing inflammation, improving heart health, and potentially reducing the risk of certain cancers.

However, fish can also contain unhealthy levels of mercury and other pollutants. These toxins can accumulate in the fatty tissue of fish and can be harmful to human health if consumed in large quantities.

Another concern is overfishing, which can lead to a depletion of fish populations and damage marine ecosystems. Additionally, some fish farms use unsustainable practices and can introduce non-native fish species into local waters, causing harm to native fish populations and ecosystems.

In conclusion, fish can be a healthy source of fatty acids, but it's important to be mindful of the potential pollutants and to choose fish from sustainable sources. It's also important to vary the types of fish you consume to minimize exposure to contaminants and support the health of fish populations and marine life.

Meat & poultry

When rating meat by its fat contents, it depends on what type of meat you look at. Meat from mammals like cows, pigs, and lab usually is above the 1/3rd threshold for saturated fats, while chicken usually is slightly below the line. You will generally find fair amounts of omega six but miss omega 3. Most meat from mammals provides around 2/3rds of their caloric value from fat. On top of that, you usually find fair amounts of cholesterol.

Conversely, poultry has much lower overall fat content, provided you eat plain meat. If you add skin or fatty bits, fats can make up to 50% of calories. While poultry shows a better profile than red meat, you can make a difference by focusing on lean meat rather than fat pieces or processed foods, where smoke, salt, and preservatives are added. Quality here, indeed, can make a big difference.

Overall you should limit your meat intake, especially red and processed meats. The world cancer research fund recommends eating no more than 3 portions (350-500 g/ 12.5 - 17.5 ounces) per week34.

In conclusion, the amount of meat you can safely eat depends on the type of meat, its quality, and the overall composition of your diet. Meat is nutritious, but moderation is still necessary to keep things balanced.

Dairy and cheese

Cheese is a delicious and versatile food that many people worldwide enjoy. However, the fat content in cheese can significantly impact overall health, particularly when consumed in large amounts.

High-fat cheeses, such as blue or cream cheese, are high in calories and saturated fat. Consuming too much saturated fat can lead to weight gain and an increased risk of heart disease and diabetes.

On the other hand, lower-fat cheeses, such as mozzarella or feta, can be a part of a balanced diet in higher quantities. These types of cheese are lower in calories and saturated fat, making them a healthier choice.

It is important to note that cheese is also a good source of protein and calcium, and it can be a part of a healthy diet. However, it is recommended to consume it in moderation. The American Heart Association recommends that adults consume no more than 5% of their total energy through saturated fat13. On a diet of 2000 kcal, that is around 13g or 2.75 teaspoons of fat35.

In conclusion, the fat content in cheese can significantly impact overall health. Choosing lower-fat cheeses, such as mozzarella or feta, and consuming them in moderation are good levers to minimize the risks associated with high saturated fat intake.

You can find similar fat patterns in milk and yogurt. Their fat concentration is way lower, so you can safely consume larger amounts before reaching the same impact as with high-fat cheeses.
