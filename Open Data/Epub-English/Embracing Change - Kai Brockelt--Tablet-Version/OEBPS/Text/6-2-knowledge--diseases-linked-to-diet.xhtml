6.2 Knowledge:

Diseases linked to diet

When looking into the statistics about morbidity and mortality in developed regions of the world like the United States or Europe, we can track an epidemic increase of certain diseases correlating with the level of industrialization.

Here, the leading causes of death are no longer “natural death” through age. The predominant causes of death are influenceable and partially also curable diseases. The main factors are lifestyle related: your diet, physical activity, smoking, and drinking. The main causes of death in developed countries are coronary heart disease and cancer.

In this chapter, we look a bit deeper into the influence of diet and lifestyle on developing, preventing, and promoting these diseases.

Why is this important?

Most diseases we die from are preventable and sometimes reversible through lifestyle factors. Knowing these levers allows you to take preventive measures and promote a healthier lifestyle.

When you should see your doctor

Nutrition is a great tool to work on the prevention of these diseases. If you have a diagnose, put this work in your doctor's hands. If, for example, you are overweight, there may already be some underlying risk factors you must take care of. A physician can spot these and guide you around those pitfalls.

Factors that influence the development of disease

Obesity, type 2 diabetes, CHD, osteoporosis, gout, and cancer are demonstrated to be less common in people following a vegetarian diet, according to the findings of the American dietetic association79.

The main factors behind this are linked to a healthier lifestyle: less smoking and alcohol abuse, regular physical activity, and higher consumption of fruit and vegetables are responsible for a more positive health outcome.

The EPIC Study80, a cohort of 20000 healthy men and women, examined the relationship between the risk for CHD and general mortality and lifestyle factors. After 11 years, the overall mortality risk was lowered 4-fold81 for the group who smoked less, was physically active, only consumed small amounts of alcohol, and ate five portions of fruit and vegetables daily, compared to a group who did none of these preventive factors. The risk of a heart attack was reduced by half. The main drivers were giving up smoking and regular consumption of fruit and vegetables.

Other scientific studies were able to demonstrate that with certain diseases, more than just prevention possible. Manifest pathological conditions like CHD82 and type 2 diabetes83 can be improved and even reversed through diet. Research shows that reducing and quitting medication is possible84.

To many people, it is unknown that it is not normal to suffer these diseases at a certain age and that lifestyle can strongly influence them.

Obesity, adiposity, and the metabolic syndrome

Obesity and overweight are the results of a long-term hypercaloric diet, often in combination with low physical activity. (Calories In > calories out). This phenomenon is common in highly industrialized regions but also visible in emerging countries that try to align with a “Western lifestyle.”

Being overweight is very often the entry door to metabolic syndrome. The metabolic syndrome is a precursor of manifest diseases led by hypertension, glucose intolerance, and dyslipidemia, a bad fat composition in your blood.

Over time, this can fire up the probability of developing vascular disease (atherosclerosis CHD, hypertension), diabetes mellitus type 2, and other conditions. Being overweight promotes gout, nonalcoholic fatty liver, bile stones, osteoporosis, and higher cancer risk.

The direct impact of obesity

Weight gain through a hyper-caloric diet drives an increase in body fat. Abdominal fat can lead to higher blood lipid concentrations. These lipids tend to stick to the vascular walls, the endothelium. This promotes a couple of diseases.

Abdominal adiposity is more common in men, as body fat in women preferably gets stored on the hips and legs. The male (android) type of body fat distribution is stronger related to metabolic and cardiovascular diseases.

Diseases associated with being overweight are:

  • Cardiovascular diseases, such as
    Atherosclerosis
    CHD
    Left ventricular hypertrophy
    Heart insufficiency
  • Diabetes mellitus type 2
  • Disruption of hemostasis (healing of wounds)
  • Hyperuricemia (higher urea levels in the blood) and gout
  • Bile stones
  • Nonalcoholic fatty liver
  • Sleep apnea
  • Skeletal diseases like osteoporosis
  • Elevated risk of cancer
  • Disruption of fertility
  • Skin irritations and diseases
  • Reduced mobility
  • Psychological and social problems

Diagnosing Adipositas and overweight

Adipositas usually gets diagnosed through the Body Mass Index. The BMI is a mathematical ratio between your body weight and height and can be calculated through the following formula:

BMI = Body weight in KG / (height in m)2

This calculation correlates with body fat percentage and estimates the total fatty mass. It is less accurate in people with high muscle percentages, as muscle is heavier than fatty tissue.

For adults over 20 years old, the BMI falls into one of the following categories85:

BMI Classification
Below 18.5 Underweight
18.5 to 24.9 Normal weight
25 to 29.9 Pre-Obesity
30 to 34.9 Obesity class I
35 to 39.9 Obesity class II
40 and above Obesity class III

According to the BMI, average weight is associated with the highest life expectancy.

A decline or rise of the BMI outside of this range raises overall mortality in a U-shaped curve86.

A rise in BMI correlates with a higher probability of developing metabolic disease. Another diagnostic tool for the risk of metabolic syndrome is waist size. Unlike the BMI, this is gender specific and is more focused on abdominal fat.

Causes of obesity

Put simply, the reason behind obesity is a hypercaloric diet. This means: your energy intake is higher than your energy use over a long-term period.

Consuming highly caloric foods, especially those rich in isolated macronutrients, promote eating more calories. In particular refined fat and sugar make up a bad combination. As high blood sugar announces the release of insulin, this quickly leads to body fat gain. (see chapter about carbohydrates).

Also, alcohol is an often underestimated factor87. It has a high caloric density (7kcal / gram) and is a burden to your liver, leading to a loss of micronutrients.

Other factors, combined with a high-caloric diet, can drive obesity:

  • A lack of physical activity (calories out)
  • Genetic disposition
  • Underlying metabolic diseases, like hypothyroidism
  • Priming during pregnancy
  • Priming of dietary patterns as a child
  • Social environment
  • Psychological factors, like emotional eating

Tackling obesity

Adiposity should always be a topic for your doctor. Nevertheless, obesity can be influenced.

Fighting obesity requires prolonged therapy, usually through diet changes and increased physical activity. As eating habits form over a long time and are linked to your personality, making this change often is challenging. This can be seen as a reason why many crash diets come up with the yo-yo effect. It is hard to pull off, does not respect personal traits and habits, and, once finished, usually gets replaced by the old regular way of eating. A slow but steady change is the way to go. (See chapter 1.3, healthy body changes).

The goal of therapy usually is weight reduction towards the normal BMI range, along with a normalization of pathological parameters like a healthy blood lipid profile. A good approach that leads to long-term results is to set consecutive small goals and improve step by step.

In terms of preventing obesity, sound habits can be formed in children by living by example and demonstrating a good and healthy lifestyle.

As one of the influenceable main factors, the diet should focus on reducing high-calorie foods, such as processed foods and meats. In combination with physical activity, the effect of weight loss comes fast and can be sustained over a long period. Simply put: On average, people who focus on whole food plant-focused diets are slimmer88.

Diabetes mellitus type 2

Diabetes mellitus is the “sugar disease,” where the urine of the affected is sweet. Diabetes Mellitus type 289 has spread on epidemic levels in the past decades and is firmly linked to dietary patterns and eating habits. Diabetes type 2 makes up 80-95% of all cases of diabetes and can be prevented through diet. It is a disturbance of carbohydrate metabolism.

What is diabetes?

Diabetes mellitus is the disease of hyperglycemia, chronically high blood sugar levels. Two anomalies in insulin metabolism cause chronic high blood sugar:

  • Disrupted insulin secretion: Through damage of Beta cells of the pancreas. Due to a lack of insulin, blood glucose can no longer be transported into our cells effectively and can accumulate. This is typical for diabetes type 1.
  • Insulin resistance: The effectiveness of insulin itself is diminished. This leads to excess insulin production, and the blood insulin levels also rise, as it no longer functions properly and more gets secreted. This is typical for type 2 diabetes. Along with insulin resistance, disrupted insulin secretion can occur90, caused by apoptosis (cellular death) of beta cells in the pancreas.

The accumulation of blood sugar leads to macro and microvascular damage. The long-term damages mainly hit the vessel and can develop in 10-15 years of manifest diabetes. These are:

  • Macrovascular:
    Atherosclerosis, and following coronary heart disease, cerebral circulatory disorders, and arterial occlusive disease.
  • Microvascular:
    Neuropathy (affecting neurons)
    Retinopathy (affecting vision/retina)
    Nephropathy (affecting the kidney)

Neuropathy91 affects the peripheral nervous system. It occurs through tingling, numbness, or pain in affected areas. It can lead to weakness and cramps. Retinopathy is a disease of the retina, which is caused by damage to the blood vessels in the eye.

Nephropathy92 means inflammation, vascular changes, and other complications of the kidney. Symptoms can be nausea, vomiting, exhaustion, and water retention. The most frequently occurring symptoms of diabetes are frequent urination (polyuria) and increased thirst.

Causes of type 2 diabetes

Type 2 diabetes is driven by a lifestyle terrible for our health. Obesity, especially abdominal obesity, accounts for a risk factor of disrupted glucose metabolism. Further factors are a genetic component and other diet-related factors, like metabolic syndrome.

The exact mechanisms behind the causes of type 2 diabetes are still not fully unveiled. But the most common thesis is that insulin resistance is caused by visceral fat. The imbalance of energy intake and use raises blood lipid levels, impacting glucose use in muscle and fatty tissue. This could be a cause of insulin resistance. In the prediabetic phase, this leads to excess insulin secretion to compensate for the resistance. Fat deposits, in the long run, will also damage beta cells, reducing production over time. The insulin is no longer enough to compensate for the resistance; hence, you develop type 2 diabetes.

Preventive measures

The most significant factors for prevention are minimizing its strong risk drivers like adiposity and metabolic syndrome. Secondary preventive measures can be taken in the lifestyle:

  • Raising physical activity.
  • Keeping the caloric intake through fat below 30% of total energy intake and focusing on keeping saturated fat low.
  • Eating enough fiber.

You can see strong epidemiologic correlations93 between fat contents in foods and the occurrence of diabetes.

There are also positive correlations94 between type 2 diabetes and high consumption of meat or animal products like milk. Potential reasons95 can be high amounts of saturated fats, high contents of nitrite, nitrosamines, and heme iron.

This means that reducing animal products and processed foods can have preventive potential. The preventive potential of plant-based diets96 has already been shown in several studies, for example, the Adventist health study.

Cardiovascular diseases

Cardiovascular diseases are globally the number one cause of death. Those diseases affect and damage your heart and vascular system. The most common cardiovascular diseases are hypertension (high blood pressure) and atherosclerosis, which can lead to other cardiovascular events like heart attacks or strokes. Genetics, nutrition, and lifestyle strongly influence the development of hypertension and atherosclerosis. Let’s look into the causes of cardiovascular diseases.

Hypertension

High blood pressure is the most common disease worldwide. Often undetected, it is a driver for other cardiovascular diseases, as high blood pressure promotes damage to the vessel and organs. If, for example, you overeat salt, this can impact your electrolyte balance and water household. Too much salt leads to more blood volume, raising blood pressure. Besides salt, being overweight is a widespread driver for hypertension.

Hypertension is a risk factor itself for more complicated cardiovascular events. The higher the blood pressure, the higher the risk for complications. Vascular changes can lead to complications like heart attacks, strokes, kidney insufficiency, and myocardial hypertrophy.

With high blood pressure, the heart has to pump against increased resistance. It will try to compensate for that through growth. The muscle fiber will grow thicker and longer. Over time, this can wear out the muscle like a rubber band, leading to myocardial insufficiency. Then, the heart can no longer pump enough blood through your body to provide sufficient amounts of oxygen and nutrients.

Hypertension typically gets diagnosed through a permanent blood pressure of > 140 mmHg systolic (the upper value during an active pump) and > 90 mmHg diastolic (the base pressure when the heart is at rest).

A reduction of body weight can improve blood pressure tremendously. Women, at least until menopause, tend to have lower blood pressure. Afro-Americans97 have higher tendencies to develop hypertension and linked diseases and are more prone to salt sensitivity.

Factors of high blood pressure:

Hypertension can be influenced by multiple factors, like:

  • Blood volume (the overall quantity of blood in circulation)
  • Blood viscosity (thin vs. thicker blood)
  • Cardiac output (how much blood can the heart pump over time)
  • The elasticity of the vessel
  • Hormones

In any case, hypertension is related to the liquid and electrolyte household. As the main factor, the blood volume influences the pressure in your vessels. A good isotonic salt-to-water ratio in the blood is critical (ideally, a 0.9% saturation).

Typical symptoms of hypertension are headaches, tiredness, and visual impairments. Chronic high blood pressure can lead to pains in the chest and shortness of breath. If not taken care of, hypertension can fire up atherosclerotic changes in your vessel. Especially regions with small vessels are impacted, like the brain, heart, kidneys, eyes, genitals, and extremities.

Causes of hypertension

Most cases of hypertension are cases of primary, or idiopathic hypertension. This means they have no underlying disease like kidney malfunction, hormonal disbalance, tumors, or psychological causes.

Prevention and therapy

The most critical measures for hypertension are preventive. In particular, it is important to avoid obesity and to keep alcohol and salt intake low. What accounts for prevention can also be used in therapy alongside medication. The aim usually is to work on an overall healthier lifestyle. Typical measures98 are:

  • Reduction of body weight (BMI between 19 and 25 should be the goal).
  • A plant-focused diet with reduced fats, usually following the DASH diet (Dietary approach to stop hypertension).
  • Reduction of salt intake to no more than 6g daily.
  • Regular physical activity.
  • Moderate consumption of alcohol.

All those measures significantly influence hypertension statistically and have great potential to reduce high blood pressure. Furthermore, not smoking removes another risk factor for cardiovascular events, as smoking causes vessel stiffness, alongside the cancer risks.

Salt reduction is often the first and crucial step to counter high blood pressure. It is often unknown that it’s not necessarily the table salt you put on your meals but the salt in processed foods like bread, meats, sausages, cheeses, and other processed foods.

Compared to omnivores, vegetarians and vegans have lower blood pressure99 on average, despite similar salt intakes. This shows that the increased consumption of potassium and other minerals through fruit and vegetables positively affects hypertension by contributing to a healthy electrolyte balance.

Another potential factor promoting hypertension is dietary fat. Saturated fats especially are linked to rising blood pressure. In contrast, monounsaturated fats100 instead tend to lower blood pressure, at least when the overall calories from fat do not exceed 37% of your daily calorie intake. Omega 3 fatty acids also impact blood pressure positively.

All in all, the effect of dietary fat on blood is less significant, but they are a strong driver for metabolic syndrome and obesity, which are vital factors.

Multiple types of medication against high blood pressure exist but always come as some trade-off for other symptoms and risks. The primary goal of therapy should always be reaching an adequate lifestyle over long-lasting treatment through medication.

Atherosclerosis and coronary heart disease

Atherosclerosis is a change or damage of your arteries that hinder blood flow. Unattended, these changes can lead to coronary heart disease, stroke, or other peripheral vessel damage, which can lead to death in the worst case.

Coronary heart disease is atherosclerosis as a blockage or damage in the coronal arteries (vessels, which provide blood to the heart itself). It can lead to heart failure, cardiac arrhythmias, heart attack, and sudden cardiac death. CHD is the cause of death no1 worldwide101. Atherosclerosis can also happen outside of your coronal arteries. If occurring in the brain, it can lead to strokes.

A major influencing factor102 in the development of atherosclerosis is diet. Scientists managed to free patients from their advanced forms of CHD through primarily dietary means103. On the other hand, a high-fat fast food meal is shown to lower the elasticity of your arteries104 in just a few hours.

From a nutrition standpoint, the build-up of atherosclerosis is strongly related to the blood lipid profile and hence strongly influenceable through diet. Factors like metabolic syndrome, smoking, lack of physical activity, and genetic disposition can promote the risk of atherosclerosis.

How atherosclerosis builds up

The arteries are those vessels leading from our heart into the extremes of our body. When healthy, they are very elastic and can adapt to resistance changes in blood pressure. How atherosclerosis is built up is not yet fully uncovered, however, a strong hypothesis is the following:

If blood lipids are not transported fast enough and accumulate in the bloodstream, they can accumulate behind the endothelium, the innermost layer of your blood vessel. Especially the low-density lipoprotein (LDL), known for transporting cholesterol, is known to get stuck in the endothelium. Macrophages of the immune system ingest these until they die and raise inflammation. Calcium can accumulate here and lead to plaques.

As these plaques grow, they narrow the vessel and reduce its elasticity. This can lead to a complete closure of the artery.

Atherosclerosis usually does not come isolated but often shows up with obesity, hypertension, and diabetes. There are several non-influenceable and influenceable factors.

Factors you cannot influence:

  • Genetic disposition
  • Gender (Men develop atherosclerosis faster than women)
  • Aging

Factors you can influence:

  • Changes in the blood lipid profile
  • High overall cholesterol
  • High LDL cholesterol
  • High blood triglycerides
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes mellitus type two
  • Inflammation
  • Adipositas

A diet low in fruit and vegetables and frequent drinking account for significant risk factors. Other factors like lack of physical activity, smoking, and psychic and social factors influence the development of atherosclerosis.

Prevention and therapy

The build-up of plaques strongly correlates with the LDL cholesterol levels in our blood. The higher the concentration of LDL cholesterol, the more likely and faster the build-up can happen.

Science has found that dietary cholesterol itself is only a weak driver for the buildup of plaques. A meta-study105 showed that when replacing saturated fats with polyunsaturated fats, you get the most potent reduction of blood cholesterol. In contrast, a reduction of dietary cholesterol had the slightest effect.

Therefore, an improvement in the quality of dietary fats is one big lever we can use to prevent plaque buildup. Other factors like physical activity, reduction of stress, and not smoking are the primary touchpoints for therapy and prevention.

Polyunsaturated fats can effectively lower LDL cholesterol as well as blood triglycerides, which also can improve hypertension to a certain degree. Avoiding the drivers trans-fat, saturated fat, and cholesterol by reducing the intake of animal products106 and highly processed foods can lead to rapid improvement in the risk of cardiovascular events.

Mono-unsaturated fats, as found in olives, nuts, avocados, seeds, and more, can lower blood cholesterol levels if you eat them instead of saturated fatty acids.

Furthermore, there is an inverse relation107 between the risk of CHD and consuming fruit, vegetables, whole grains, and fish. In summary, shifting from red meat and processed foods-focused diet towards a more real and plant-based diet can help to improve atherosclerosis effectively.

Cancer

Cancer is a group of diseases characterized by the uncontrolled growth and spread of abnormal cells. If left untreated, these abnormal cells can spread to other body parts and damage healthy tissue. There are many different types of cancer, and they can occur in any part of the body. Some common types of cancer include breast cancer, lung cancer, colon cancer, and prostate cancer.

Cancer is the second leading cause of death in industrialized countries after CHD. In the US, men have a cancer risk of 40%, and women of 38%108. Keeping the overall cancer risk low through a holistic, healthy lifestyle is possible. Nutrition again plays a central role in that.

How cancer develops

The development of cancer can be divided into three main phases:

The initiation phase: normal cells are transformed into cancer cells. This happens through damaging the DNA, which can lead to uncontrolled mutation of the genetic code. If our repair mechanisms no longer manage to repair those damages or kill affected cells, those cells can start to multiply. The initiation can happen years before the spread of detectable cancer.

The promotion phase: cancer cells grow and multiply but do not yet form a tumor. The development of tumors can take years to happen.

The progression phase: cancer cells form a tumor, invade surrounding tissues, and potentially spread to other body parts (metastasis).

It's important to note that these phases do not always happen linearly and that different cancers may follow different pathways.

Causes of cancer

The development of cancer is multifactorial. Factors can be

Not influenceable:

  • High age
  • Genetic disposition
  • Virus infections
  • Chronic infections

Influenceable:

  • Exposition to radiation and cancerous chemicals
  • Smoking
  • Overweight
  • Lack of physical activity
  • Frequent alcohol drinking
  • Dietary habits:
    Hypercaloric diet
    Not eating enough fruit and vegetables
    High intake of salt, processed foods, grilled, pickled, and smoked foods
    High meat consumption, especially processed meats
    High fat intake

While you can never entirely remove the risk of developing cancer, most factors lie in our hands. Bad habits like smoking, drinking or an unhealthy diet, and lack of movement are factors that we can work on to improve.

Due to the high latency in the development of tumors, it is pretty challenging to investigate the influence of nutrition on the development of cancer. There are, however, indicators109 for certain cancerous or anti-cancerous effects of specific dietary patterns or foods.

Frequent eating of fruit and vegetables: Moderate evidence shows risk lowering effects of eating fruits and vegetables. Mainly the digestive tract profits from a decreased risk of cancers that correlates with the intake of secondary plant substances and fiber. Fiber also shows strong correlations towards risk prevention of colon and stomach cancer. This is probably due to the shorter passage time of the chymus, the production of short-chained fatty acids by bacteria of the microbiome, and less production of cancerous substances by animal protein processing bacteria.

Red meat and processed meats: strong evidence shows a connection between red and processed meats and the development of colon cancer.

Alcohol consumption: The consumption of alcohol and the development of tumors in the digestive tract and chest are strongly linked110.

Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis is when the bones become weak and brittle, making them more prone to fractures and breaks. It is most commonly seen in women after menopause but can also affect men and people of all ages. Osteoporosis is caused by a loss of bone density, which can be due to a variety of factors, including a lack of physical activity, low levels of certain hormones, and a deficiency in nutrients such as calcium and vitamin D. It is crucial to maintain strong bones through a healthy diet and regular exercise to reduce the risk of developing osteoporosis.

Our bones are under constant construction. There are two concurring processes, one building up bone material and one releasing it. During growth, the process that builds up the bone is more robust, and you reach your highest bone density at around 25-30 years. After that age, you slowly lose bone density between 0.5% and 1.5% yearly.

Causes of osteoporosis

There is a strong genetic disposition for osteoporosis. There is a tendency for women to be more affected by osteoporosis. High age and being underweight as well can promote the development of osteoporosis.

There are factors that you can actively influence:

  • Lack of movement: Resistance training especially accounts for a stimulus for bone growth.
  • Low levels of estrogens: This can be caused by being underweight and by the menopause.
  • Consumption of alcohol, nicotine, and caffeine.
  • Lack of vitamin D: Can be tackled through direct sunlight explosion, animal products, or supplementation.
  • Multiple dietary factors:
    High intake of protein111, especially animal protein.
    Lack of dietary calcium
    High salt intake112
    High phosphorus intake
    Lack of fruit and vegetables.

Prevention

In short, you should rule out most of the factors above. Training and a good diet are the best tools to promote bone strength. If you do sports outside, you simultaneously work on your vitamin D levels.

For good bone health, you do not exclusively need to consume enough calcium. For bone metabolism, vitamins D and K are also required. High amounts of animal protein are linked to elevated calcium excretion113 in the kidneys.

Gout

Gout is a form of arthritis caused by the buildup of uric acid crystals in the joints, leading to inflammation and pain. The development of gout is a complex process that can be influenced by various factors, including genetics, lifestyle, and underlying medical conditions.

The first step in the development of gout is the production of uric acid. Uric acid is a waste product produced when the body breaks down purines found in many foods and drinks, including red meat, seafood, and alcohol. In most people, uric acid is dissolved in the blood and then excreted in the urine. However, in some people, the body produces too much uric acid or doesn't excrete enough of it, leading to a build-up of uric acid in the blood.

Once excessive uric acid is in the blood, it can form crystals that can deposit in the joints, tendons, and surrounding tissue. This can cause inflammation and pain, the hallmark symptom of gout. The initial gout attack is often sudden and severe and typically affects the big toe, but it can also affect other joints, such as the ankle, knee, wrist, fingers, and elbow.

Causes of gout

Risk factors for the development of gout include:

  • Genetics: A family history of gout increases the risk of developing the condition.
  • Diet: Consuming high amounts of purine-rich foods and drinks, such as red meat, seafood, and alcohol, can increase the risk of gout.
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese can increase the risk of gout.
  • Medical conditions: Certain medical conditions, such as diabetes, high blood pressure, and metabolic syndrome, can increase the risk of gout.

Dietary patterns that promote the development of gout include those high in purines, compounds found in many foods and drinks. Purines are broken down into uric acid in the body, and high uric acid levels can form crystals in the joints.

The following dietary patterns have been linked to an increased risk of gout:

  • High meat consumption: High amounts of red meat, organ meats (such as liver and kidney), and game meats (such as venison) can increase the risk of gout.
  • High seafood consumption: Consuming high amounts of seafood, particularly shellfish (such as shrimp, lobster, and crab), can increase the risk of gout.
  • High alcohol consumption: Consuming high amounts of alcohol, particularly beer, can increase the risk of gout. Alcohol interferes with the excretion of uric acid, leading to its accumulation in the blood.
  • High fructose consumption: Consuming high amounts of fructose found in high-fructose corn syrup or alike can increase the risk of gout.

Additionally, being overweight or obese and having metabolic syndrome, high blood pressure, or diabetes can increase the risk of gout.

Prevention

It's essential for people with gout or at risk for gout to consume a diet that is low in purines and maintain a healthy weight. Also, it's vital to limit the intake of alcohol and to drink enough water to help flush out uric acid from the body.

Focussing on fresh fruits and vegetables, whole grains, and low-fat and non-dairy products while moderating the intake of red meat, organs, seafood, and high fructose is a great start to prevent the build-up of gout. Drinking enough water and vitamin C-rich foods keeps uric acid in your blood low.
