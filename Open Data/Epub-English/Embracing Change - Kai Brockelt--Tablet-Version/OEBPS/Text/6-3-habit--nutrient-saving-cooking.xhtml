Nutrient-saving cooking

In previous chapters, we discovered the topics of food quality and spoilage and how we pick nutritious and valuable foods. We also put light on how we absorb certain nutrients, like iron, better.

Heat, moisture, and other factors can cause nutrients to break down or wash out of the food during the cooking process. As a result, the nutritional content of the finished dish may be less than that of the raw ingredients. We will examine how nutrients get lost in preparation and discuss strategies to minimize the loss.

Why is this important?

Understanding the factors contributing to nutrient loss can help you make informed decisions about how to prepare your food. By choosing cooking methods that preserve nutrients and using techniques to minimize nutrient loss, you can ensure you get the most nutritional benefit from your meals.

This knowledge is vital for people at risk of certain nutrient deficiencies and people with certain health conditions.

How do we destroy or lose nutrients?

When processing food, we can lose nutrients in 3 main ways:

  • We physically remove them: This accounts for cutting peels, trunks, and other less likable fruit and vegetable parts. Like in grains, a fair amount of nutrients lie in the peel and structure parts. Not cutting off these parts preserves their nutritional value.
  • Heat: Heat can destroy most vitamins, carbohydrates, proteins, and fat. Through burning, you can create PAHs or polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. PAHs are chemicals produced when food is burned or grilled at high temperatures. They are a type of carcinogen, meaning they can potentially cause cancer in humans.
  • Washing out: Many minerals and water-soluble vitamins are prone to be washed out into the water when cooked. If you throw that water away, you lose those nutrients.

The loss during cooking

When cooking or boiling food in water, many nutrients get washed out into the water. This affects all vitamins and the minerals potassium, magnesium, sodium, and calcium. For example, boiling broccoli in water reduces its vitamin C contents by up to 50%. Other water-soluble vitamins have similar loss rates.

If you reuse your boiling water later on, you can retain those nutrients. It is therefore recommended to use only as much boiling water as necessary. Smaller amounts can be transformed into a sauce more easily.

An alternative to boiling foods that is more nutrient-savvy is steaming. When steaming, the food comes in contact with less water, so more nutrients remain. You can rule out the loss of washing out entirely by not using a cooking method that requires the food to sit in water. Roasting and pan-frying are preparation methods that avoid washing out. Both can be considered very healthy ways of cooking.

Better ways of preparation

When boiling food, we waste nutrients by washing them out and by destroying them through heat. Steaming already reduces the loss. Even better are preparation methods that don’t remove nutrients at all. So generally, you can say stir-frying preserves more nutrients.

The most gentle preparation method is roasting. First, there is no washing out, and second, there is no direct contact heat charring nutrients.

How to properly pan fry

On the one hand, we want to preserve as many of our nutrients as possible. Both the cooking time and the temperature have an impact on how many vitamins get lost. On the other hand, we want to apply enough heat to create the Maillard reaction and, through that, a proper roast.

The pan should be medium hot and properly pre-heated for a short frying time. You should feel the heat when holding your hand above the pan, but it should not yet be biting. A drop of water should sizzle and vapourize quickly.

If you get the heat right, you should see a good Maillard browning on your food after approximately 2-4 minutes, depending on what you fry. If you need your food to get well done throughout, you should make sure that your cuts are thin enough. If your food ends up raw on the inside while crispy on the outside, you can cut it thinner next time so that the heat has a shorter way to travel through the center. Alternatively, you can lengthen the cooking time by lowering the temperature.

Avoid burning food

When frying or roasting too hot, you create cancerous substances, PAHs. PAHs can be found in charred or grilled foods, including meats, vegetables, and grains. They are formed when the food is exposed to high heat, like when grilled over an open flame or cooked on a stovetop at high temperatures.

When your pan is steaming without anything in it, it’s undoubtedly too hot. If your oil begins to bubble, It’s too hot. PAHs are more likely to form when food is cooked above 149°C / 300°F. The further the temperature above it, the faster the food can burn and create these substances.

Picking the right oil for each job

When choosing the right oil when cooking, we should remember the heat resistance of the different types of fat. Oils rich in omega-3 fatty acids, like flax oil, should not be used for frying. Olive oil is only suitable for medium temperatures.

If you want to fry at high temperature, you best pick an oil that has a high smoke point:

  • Canola oil: has a high smoke point of 400-450°F (204-232°C) and a neutral flavor, making it a good choice for deep frying. It is also low in saturated fat, making it a healthier option.
  • Peanut oil: has a smoke point of 450°F (232°C) and a nutty flavor that pairs well with many foods. It is high in monounsaturated fat and low in saturated fat.
  • Grape seed oil: has a smoke point of 420-450°F (216-232°C) and a mild, neutral flavor. It is high in polyunsaturated fat and low in saturated fat.
  • Sunflower seed oil: has a neutral taste and a smoke point at around 450° F / 232°C. It is rich in monounsaturated fats and can also be used for deep frying.

Please be reminded that fat comes with 9 kcal per gram. One tablespoon of oil already adds 120 kcal to the meal. Drowning your food in oil can quickly outbalance the calorie-nutrient ratio.

If you follow that and manage to fry and roast foods at proper heat, you’re doing a great job preserving those nutrients.
