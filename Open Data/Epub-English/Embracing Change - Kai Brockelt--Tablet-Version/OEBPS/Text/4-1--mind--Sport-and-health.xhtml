4.1 Mind:

Sport & Health

Let’s talk about good old sports. I don’t need to tell you that exercising benefits you and that you should try it out if you are not already doing it. But instead of serving you many motivational words, let’s examine the effects exercise can provide.

Why is this important?

Nutrition alone won’t fulfill all your body's needs. Exercise and movement help your body self-regulate and improve metabolism, sleep, and mental and physical health.

The positive effects of exercising

When doing sports, you introduce some beneficial effects to your daily routine. Before looking at the mechanisms behind every move you make, it is good to be reminded of why sport is helpful:

Exercise makes you happier naturally.

Yes, it’s that simple. Sport is proven to make you feel better. It can reduce symptoms of anxiety, depression, and stress45 by increasing your serotonin sensitivity in the brain. Other studies show that the positive effects pick up in no time and work acutely and therefore are an effective antidepressant46. On the other hand, if you withdraw from exercise for too long, negativity and depressive symptoms pop up in no time47.

Exercise strengthens your muscles and bones.

Sport releases growth hormones. Paired with the proper nutrition, exercise promotes the strengthening and growth of your muscles and bones. More muscles correlate with a reduced risk of injury48. Especially at a young age, exercise can help prevent osteoporosis49 in the long run through a higher bone density.

Exercise can improve your skin.

Sport, while in the first place meaning oxidative stress, promotes the production of antioxidants in your body after a workout. That means that sports can reduce the risk of skin diseases50, and regular exercise is linked to slowed skin aging51.

Exercise can boost your metabolism and promote weight loss

This one is obvious, and we also go deeper into the mechanisms behind it later. But yes. Regular exercise can bump up your metabolism52 and helps you burn more energy passively.

Exercise can give you more energy.

When exercising regularly, you can end up with more energy. This can tackle feelings of fatigue53 and also works against chronic fatigue syndrome54. Naturally, when you exercise, your lungs and heart transport more oxygen and become more efficient over time. It’s all about circulation.

Exercise can improve your sex life.

Also bound to circulation, your sexy parts rise and fall with exercise and a good diet (pun intended). More blood flow and oxygen mean more performance. And the effects measured are crazy: A study that made men walk 6 minutes around the house for 30 days reduced their erectile dysfunction symptoms by a whopping 71%55.

The same accounts for women. Studies have found that regular exercise keeps sexual function and desire burning, even during menopause56.

Exercise can help you sleep better.

When doing sport, your sleep can be deeper and more restorative57. Even symptoms of chronic insomnia can be improved through stretching and resistance exercise. For older people, sleeping problems are common but also can be enhanced through training58.

Exercise reduces the risk of chronic diseases.

Lack of activity contributes to at least 35 unhealthy conditions59, including the top 10 leading killers in the US. This includes CHD60, diabetes61, cancer62, hypertension63, and more. Meaning excercise supports long-lasting life quality and health.

Energy household and sport

As we have learned earlier, our genetic code still runs on the survival mode of our ancestors and is not adapted to an “all available” Western lifestyle. In short, if we don’t exercise, we will lose muscle, making us more energy efficient.

The main goal of life is to sustain itself. Practically, this means that our body is a bunch of self-sustaining processes. If they stop, life stops. Everything comes to a stop if we run out of energy.

To put more sense into it, it is helpful to look at the mechanisms at play individually. Ultimately, there is no “smartness” in processing our available energy and resources. Each process is just “doing its thing,” Based on our conditions, some functions may halt, while some may be amplified.

Blood sugar levels and energy burn modes

Glucose, as the primary energy source for our brain, plays a key role in the processes at play. We store glucose in our muscles and liver to quickly burn it when needed. Blood glucose levels are responsible for keeping the availability of sugar high. If your blood sugar levels would drop too low, you could faint and die.

Our primary energy pathway is burning glucose in the citric acid (Krebs) cycle to charge up ATP where needed. If sugar demand is high or available glucose is low, the body starts putting ketone bodies made from fats into the cycle to produce energy. With low carbs available, your body fills the energy demand from fats. Typically, this happens if your carbs stored in glycogen run low when hiking for a long time or if you eat a fat-focused diet like keto.

This effect is bound to the blood sugar levels. On blood sugar spikes, insulin will be released to store new glucose as glycogen in the muscles and transform excess into body fat.

When the blood sugar levels drop, the counterplayer of insulin, glucagon, gets released. While insulin is there to fend off high blood sugar levels, glucagon does the opposite, doing everything to prevent blood sugar levels from dropping to dangerous levels. So the two hormones, insulin and glucagon, try to keep your levels in check. Insulin prevents high spikes, and glucagon prevents too-low levels.

Put simply: Glucose is the preferred energy source when sugar is available in sufficient amounts. The lower our blood sugar levels, the more ketone bodies get added to the energy mix.

What about gluconeogenesis?

When within a balanced caloric range, this system plays out wonderfully. However, a third process at play transforms protein into glucose when blood sugar is low: the so-called gluconeogenesis.

The presence of insulin and glucagon directly influences this process. Insulin slows down gluconeogenesis, and glucagon speeds it up. It is always active but gets enhanced when blood sugar levels drop low. In this sense, hunger periods and exercise can directly cost you muscle. But no worries, this is normal and happens all the time, even while exercising.

When dieting and maintaining low blood sugar levels and hunger for long, glyconeogenesis will speed up and sacrifice muscle to keep blood sugars and reach a less energy-hungry metabolic state. Less muscle to move around means less energy needed. Your metabolic rate goes down consequently.

Shrink and growth of muscle

As we learned, gluconeogenesis is always “on” but acts stronger when we are hungry. Meaning on more extended hunger periods, we will sacrifice muscle.

Remember the recommendation not to undercut your calories by more than 15%? This is precisely why you shouldn’t undercut too much. Your blood sugar levels will be so low that protein gets burned more than you like. Weight loss then comes mainly from muscle, and your fat reserves barely change. And less muscle means less energy consumption which again means a more challenging time getting fit.

To counter this process, we can do two things:

  • Keep the gluconeogenesis activity low by not undercutting your calories too much
  • Promote growth signals through exercise and provide the needed proteins for growth

Promote growth

We grow muscle as we use it. Resistance training causes minor damage to the fiber, which then gets repaired. The more repairs happen, the stronger the muscle grows.

For this to work, we need growth signals through exercise and protein available to do the job. Exercise alone won’t grow your muscles if you lack protein in your diet. Any needed amino acid will be taken from another place if not available through diet, so muscle eats muscle.

Eating protein-rich alone also won’t do the job. You need growth signals from exercise to get the process going. If you only eat protein without creating growth signals, it will go the energy pathway if not required elsewhere.

How much sport is required?

The question of how much sport is required always depends on your goal. More sports means more growth. But to “just be healthy,” not much exercise is needed64.

In short: you should hit 150 minutes of moderate-intensity workouts per week or 75 minutes of high-intensity training. You can improve the benefits by doubling up to 300 minutes of moderate or 150 minutes of intense activity per week. On top of that, some resistance training (15-20 repetitions are enough) brings additional benefits.

Or in other words: if you move for at least 20 minutes per day, you are on a good track.

What does “moderate” exercise mean exactly?

Okay, so that’s 150 minutes of what exactly? According to Harvard65, this can mean swift walking, easy-going bicycle rides, householding, or gardening. Practically this is almost anything but sitting and standing around.

Anything like hiking, soccer games, tennis, weight lifting, and jogging can be considered intense exercise, and counts double the time if you do those.

How to fill those 150 minutes?

One hundred fifty minutes of activity may seem a lot. But we don’t talk about 150 minutes spent explicitly on sports, but 150 minutes of general moderate activity.

Adding 150 minutes of sport on top of your already existing daily routines can quickly mean you need to cut your time in another area. But luckily, you can contribute to the balance of your sports by changing some habits as well, without reserving more time for your activity.

So how can this time frame be filled quickly? Here are some ideas on what you can let count.

  • Go to work by bicycle. If you need 10 minutes per way hypothetically, that makes 20 minutes daily or 100 minutes in a 5-day work week. When you ride swiftly, you can count that double and already reach 200 minutes per week. Checkmark placed successfully!
  • Clean your house: Yes, we can let that count. Moving your vacuum cleaner, broom, or mop also counts as moderate activity. So how long do you invest per week? 30 minutes? 60 minutes? That is an activity, so subtract it from the 150 minutes.
  • Go for a Sunday walk. How long can you make it? one Hour? Two Hours?
  • Go grocery shopping walking. If you need a few things only, this is moderate activity. If you carry heavy bags back home, count the time double, as this is intense exercise.
  • Go out and dance. Being active on a Saturday night is enough room for 180 minutes of moderate to intense wiggling.

If you have a physically demanding job, you can let that count too. Check your routine and see in which areas you are already active. Identify opportunities to add more activity to your routine without changing your plans.

When should I eat for the best exercise results?

Exercise works best when you have all the needed resources available. This means proper dieting around your workout can promote better results. I don’t assume you aim for athletic training, so let’s stick to the basics.

Generally speaking, there are two main concerns around exercise: Keeping the performance up during the training on one side and having the best conditions for recovery and growth after the workout.

Chasing performance

To have optimal training performance, you should ensure that your body has the means of energy available when needed. This means your glycogen stores should be filled, and some quickly accessible energy should be in your system. Low glucose levels and hunger will limit your ability, so provide what’s needed before you work out.

Especially after longer periods of fasting (overnight), the glucose levels might not be filled. A little breakfast half an hour to one hour before the exercise can provide energy for your workout. But make sure not to bloat your belly through big meals, as this can hinder your movement and make you feel sick.

The further away the meal from the exercise, the better it is to go for complex carbs over fast sugars. The idea is to slowly load your storage without triggering the release of insulin. If you need to fuel up during exercise, sugar can be your friend as this provides energy in the quickest way possible without bloating your belly through fiber. When your blood sugar levels are already low due to your movement, sugars that get absorbed fast help you stay at your peak performance directly.

Recovery and growth after workouts

You should again provide what your body needs for the best results after your workout. This

time you chase two main goals: re-filling your emptied storage for the next adventure and providing all the protein required to repair and build that muscle.

Put simply: eat after your workout. Repair starts during your exercise, and if you don’t provide the needed building blocks, your body will take them from somewhere else. So it is crucial to provide what is required to grow and repair. I recommend eating a protein-rich and whole meal after your workout to provide protein, minerals, and energy to recover fully.
