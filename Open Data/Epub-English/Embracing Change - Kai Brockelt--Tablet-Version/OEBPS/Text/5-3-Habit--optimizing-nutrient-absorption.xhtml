5.3 Habit:

Optimizing nutrient absorption

You learned about all essential nutrients and where to find them in the previous two chapters. Food, however, contains more than just the plain nutrients you need; some of these substances can influence how well nutrients can be absorbed.

In this chapter, we look into the most common stuff that influences bioavailability, which you can consider to benefit from an optimized preparation of foods.

Why is this important?

It is possible to influence the bioavailability of nutrients by combining the right foods and through the proper preparation. Knowing about the interactions between substances you find in your food allows you to cook more intentionally without wasting nutritional potential.

Bioavailability

The term bioavailability describes how much of an ingested substance gets absorbed on its way through your body. The higher the nutrient bioavailability, the better we can absorb it.

Food contains substances that influence the bioavailability of nutrients. Some substances build strong complexes with your nutrients, making them more complicated or impossible to absorb. On the other hand, there are also substances enhancing bioavailability.

For example, non-heme iron's absorption rate can fluctuate between 2% and 20%. Out of 10mg of iron, you only absorb 0.2mg in the worst case. Hence, the amount of iron absorbed can be changed by factor 10, which is a huge lever on your iron intake.

When referring to recommended daily intake, the bioavailability is already calculated. That means you won’t need to consider the absorption rates yourself. However it is always helpful to build habits around combining the right foods for the best effect.

Substances lowering the bioavailability of Minerals

While calcium, iron, and zinc are already in concurrency (they get absorbed through the same channels), some substances actively hinder the absorption of these minerals if they are present on your plate. Let me introduce the most common ones.

Phytic acid

Phytic acid builds indigestible compounds with iron, calcium, and zinc. It can be found in legumes, whole grains, nuts, and seeds. Strategies to lower the contents of phytic acid are fermentation, soaking, and sprouting. This activates the enzyme phytase, which deactivates phytic acid.

Oxalic acid

Oxalic acid can be found in higher quantities in chard, rhubarb, sweet potato, spinach, soy, almonds, and others. It hinders the absorption of calcium, magnesium, zinc, and iron.

Oxalic acid can be reduced by soaking and boiling foods as it gets washed out. The water should not be used afterward.

Tannins

Tannins are a group of bitter substances that give you the typical astringent feeling on your tongue. You find tannins in coffee, tea, wine, and anything that gives you that “dry mouth.”

Tannins also build hard-to-break-down complexes with iron, calcium, and zinc, hindering absorption. A good strategy is keeping your coffee or tea away from your meal. Try to avoid those drinks one hour before and after your meal so that the tannins won’t be able to interfere.

How to enhance the absorption of minerals

While some substances, like tannins, bind minerals in indigestible complexes, other substances increase the absorbability of minerals. Adding vitamin C and organic acids to your food can boost absorption by 3-4x.

Combining foods from the five food groups throughout the day automatically completes this trick. This can be easily achieved by mixing different food sources. Add berries (Vitamin C + acids) to your whole grain breakfast muesli (rich in minerals). Add a squeeze of lime to your bean tacos. Add fresh vegetables to your tofu wok. Buy a nut mix containing dry fruit.

Optimizing absorption of beta-carotene

Another nutrient that can vary highly in its absorption is beta-carotene. As vitamin A is only available in animal products, vegans should keep these methods in mind to maintain safe vitamin A levels.

Like fat-soluble vitamins themselves, beta-carotene dissolves in fat. If you snack on a raw carrot, you won’t get much of its beta-carotene. Combine it with a hand full of nuts to get started.

The absorption is also dependent on thermal and mechanical influences on the food. You can access their beta-carotene more quickly if you finely chop and sauté your carrots. That’s also why a tomato sauce is more beneficial than a fresh tomato in terms of beta-carotene.
