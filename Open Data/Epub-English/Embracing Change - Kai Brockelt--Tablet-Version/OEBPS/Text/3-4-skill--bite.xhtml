3.4 Skill:

Bite

Chili, ginger, garlic, wasabi, and more are all known for the pain they can cause. While a round aroma and taste profile is the foundation of every good meal, adding some bite to the dish can lift the experience to a whole new level - if you like pain.

Why is this important?

The effects of eating spicy can range from mild tingling to unbearable solid pain. In this chapter, you will learn how to distinguish different types of bite and handle them properly. We also take care of emergencies so that you know how to manage them if you encounter a “too much” situation.

Why do we like bite in the first place?

Bite can be found in cuisines all around the world. Although it hurts to eat spicy food, it is quite beneficial to go for it occasionally. Especially the good old capsaicin from chilis is linked to chronic pain relief40.

But how come? It’s just like fighting fire with fire—the burn first causes pain, which then seems to release endorphins to fight the pain41. Once the pain is gone, we are left with those sweet endorphins in our system, which bump up our mood. Experiments on mice42 and rats43 showed that this could relieve depressive symptoms. Sadly, we cannot yet confirm this through clinical trials.

Categorizing bite

While all spicy substances cause pain, you can still distinguish their effects. Some also play with your temperature receptors, giving you the typical “hot” feeling in your mouth. This, however, does not come from the spicy compound itself but rather through other chemicals in the food. Other ingredients like Sichuan leave you with an electric tingle in your mouth or a cooling effect like as we know it from mint.

You can categorize bite into two groups - Thiocyanates and Alkylamides.

Thiocyanates

Thiocyanates are spices that climb up your nose, as you might know from wasabi or horseradish. Those components are water soluble, so they vapourize fast. A “too much” of wasabi quickly makes you cry but leaves you alone again in no time.

You find this kind of bite in radish, mustard, onion & garlic (allicin), and greens like arugula/rocket. This effect should be considered when preparing foods with those types of spices. The burning effects diminish gradually if food is cut and left aside for a few minutes without covering. When going for leftovers, the bite may be gone.

On the other hand, if you want a good burn, you should add it freshly at the end of the cooking or even serve it as a side. Make sure to keep it covered from the air until serving the dish.

Alkylamides

Alkylamides are fat soluble substances that stick to your mouth and burn your tongue for quite some time. The most common compounds are capsaicin from chilis and gingerol from, well,… ginger! Fat-soluble substances don’t vanish into thin air, so they stick to your dish once added.

Handling fat-soluble bite, therefore, requires a bit more caution. While with water-soluble substances, you can assume that time and heat can make a difference, with alkylamines, you have to stick with what you put into your pan.

Working with chilis

Chilis are the most common spicy fruit we put on our plates. There is a wide variety of different punches in chilis and related peppers. While many types of chili vary in spiciness (Yes, I am looking mainly at you, Jalapeño!), it is good to know roughly what to expect when adding a bite to your cooking. If you use a product to add spice, you can look for the Scoville heat score.

For you to be able to guess the punch, it is helpful to understand how chilis behave on a scale. Keep in mind that the sensation of pain and bite is very subjective. Just because you maybe can bathe yourself in the burn of habañeros, this does not necessarily mean that the person next to you can do this too.

Generally, I strongly recommend working with gloves when chopping chilis. As capsaicin does not dissolve in water, it also tends to stick to our fingers. You want to avoid touching your eyes or nose after chopping fresh or dried chili.

Fresh chili

Working with fresh (or frozen) chili is straightforward. Cut off the tip, slice it open, or cut it in rings, and remove the seeds. Then sprinkle it onto whatever dish deserves the bite. If you want to add less bite, remove the membranes on the inside too.

You can also grill, roast, or fry fresh peppers to add more complexity to their aromas. Here we look for the good old Maillard reaction - you know how that works!

Dried chili

Chilis don’t seem to give you much choice in their dried form, but that is not entirely true. While less fruity and bright than their fresh counterparts, their fat-soluble aromas and bites are more condensed. That gives them less complexity but more punch in what they are.

Before chopping and adding them to your dish, you can toast them. Small amounts can be toasted in a pan; larger amounts better go to the oven for a few minutes until they are puffed.

Controlling the burn

First things first: especially when cooking for children, the spicy stuff should not be mixed into the main dish directly but should be served on a small plate aside. While adults might enjoy the high burn, this can mean severe and unnecessary pain for children. So be considerate and keep the spicy stuff aside when cooking for others.

If the dish is supposed to be spicy and all children are to bed, then obviously, we can add it to the dish itself. Here we still ask ourselves the same question: how much spice is enough?

In the end, this depends on several factors. What type of chili are you using? Something mild or apocalyptic? Let’s assume you are working with a nuke of chili and want to be as careful as possible. Here are some tricks you can consider:

  • Put the chili in the pan or pot without even cutting it open. This will keep most of the capsaicin away from your dish.
  • Remove the membranes from the chili to reduce the bite.
  • Use less of it and taste it bit by bit.
  • If using dry chili - add them at the end so the bite has less time to develop and spread around the whole dish. Infuse the dish, and then remove the whole chilis again.

Lack of caution is the mother of all mouth burns. If you are still determining the level of bite, add it little by little.

Reducing the burn when it got too hot

It happens fast: you are in the middle of a cooking session, chopping a handful of chili as always, but this little monster is stronger than the ones you usually use. And the dish now is too spicy.

Of course, real problems only occur with alkylamines - as water-soluble punch vanishes over time. It’s only the fat-soluble compounds that stick around and cause problems.

You should avoid trying to wash your mouth with water. I repeat it: capsaicin and similar are not water-soluble. Worst case, you push the burn around in your mouth, and it can now reach every corner.

If you need to reduce the bite in an already cooked dish, you should first try distracting from it. Sweet and sour work wonders here. These three together form the foundation of Vietnamese and Thai cuisine. Add a squeeze of lemon or lime to the dish, and sweeten things up.

If this is not enough, try adding more bulk to the dish. Leave the hot stuff as it is and add more of everything else. Serve the dish with something starchy like bread. This soaks up any liquid and can help you clean your burnt areas.

You can add more fat to the dish to dissolve the spice better. Nut butter, like peanut butter, sometimes works wonders and adds more complexity to the palate. And if you eat them - dairy products bind capsaicin very well. Indian cuisine uses yogurt all the time, along with their spicy dishes. And now you know why!

Can too much spice destroy your taste buds?

More spice is not always a good idea. Your heart rate can go up; you start sweating, hyperventilating, vomiting... all those nasty things your body might try using to answer the pain. But can you destroy your taste buds with too hot food? The answer is no!

The receptors that capsaicin and friends play with are your receptors for pain and not your taste buds. And even though the pain signals released can be super strong, they only come from stuff binding to their corresponding receptors. Nothing gets destroyed on the way, except for your meal maybe. Your taste buds won’t care if an inferno opens up next to them. They continue waiting for input from sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami, and that’s it.
