Mushroom Döner Kebab

Döner Kebab is a dish from Turkey that is made from meat cooked on a vertical rotisserie. It found its way into its modern sandwich variant in the 70s in Berlin, Germany.

Nowadays, Döner is Germany’s #1 street food and is widely known across Europe, the Middle East, and several Asian countries.

As this dish typically contains plenty of meat, this is the perfect opportunity to learn how to replace it with mushrooms.

The origin of Döner Kebab

Döner Kebab finds its cultural roots in the Middle East and Turkey, where it emerged as a celebration of grilling and skewering meat. This culinary tradition has ancient origins, with early civilizations like the Persians and Turks perfecting the art of cooking meat over an open flame.

This tradition changed into a beloved street food icon. The modern sandwich variant of Döner Kebab emerged in Berlin, Germany, during the 1970s. It’s typically a sandwich with thinly sliced marinated meat cooked on a vertical rotisserie, garnished with red cabbage, lettuce, and onions, and topped with a garlicky yogurt dressing.

Central to the rich flavors of Döner Kebab are the spices and ingredients that make it an almost addictive experience. The meat is seasoned with warming spices, including sweet paprika, nutmeg, coriander, and cumin. A refreshing and bright yogurt sauce tops off this warm tone.

Basic building plan of a Döner

A finely stuffed sandwich with meats and fresh vegetables is at the core of crafting an authentic Döner Kebab. Let's break down the fundamental components to create this beloved street food.

Bread:

The foundation of a Döner Kebab is the bread. Traditional Döner Kebabs use Pide, a Turkish flatbread akin to pizza dough. However, different sizes and shapes can be encountered. You might find a Döner-roll that looks like a burrito or a kebab sandwich in smaller bread rolls, like the one you see in this recipe.

Meat:

The heart and soul of a Döner Kebab is the meat. Traditionally, it's thinly sliced marinated beef, lamb, or chicken cooked on a vertical rotisserie. However, for our mushroom-centric Döner, we'll replace the calory-dense meat with a light alternative. The meat typically holds warm aromas like sweet paprika, nutmeg, coriander, and cumin. They get counterbalanced with the help of the yogurt sauce.

Greens:

Fresh and crispy greens are a vital element in Döner Kebab. Lettuce, red cabbage, and sliced onions add a vibrant and refreshing contrast to the savory and umami-rich meat and contribute a fresh, crispy texture to every bite. They are also essential for the colorful appeal of the dish.

Yogurt Sauce:

The cooling effect of yogurt sauce is a delightful complement to the warming notes of the meat or mushrooms. A touch of acidity and bright aromas from fresh herbs and lemon counterbalance the low, rumbling tones of the meat in this sandwich. The creamy mouthfeel of the sauce uplifts the play of crisp and crunchy ingredients.

Making a meat-like experience

To recreate the essence of meat in our Döner Kebab, we must understand what makes meat taste so delicious. Whether you go for the real deal or use a replacement - the key to a satisfying meaty experience lies in the interplay of texture, umami, and aromas.

Finding umami

Mushrooms naturally contain a bit of umami; we will enhance this through a proper Maillard reaction. If more umami is needed, deglaze with soy or mushroom sauce. If you opt for meat, you can also be sure to have plenty of umami in your döner. Remember that we have this taste to spot protein.

Finding the base for a meaty texture:

There's a common approach regardless of the meat we aim to imitate. Achieving a certain firmness and dryness is crucial, as anything soaked won't achieve the desired bite.

Oyster mushrooms are my personal favorite for achieving a remarkable texture. They reward us with a delightful chewy bite when sliced into broad strips and seared at high heat. To make the most of oyster mushrooms, here are a few tips:

  • Fry them hot: Heat plays a pivotal role. Fry them hot and fast to close down pores, maintain firmness, and prevent sogginess.
  • Proper preheating: Ensure the pan is thoroughly preheated before adding the mushrooms for that satisfying crust. They should sizzle right away when you add them to the hot pan
  • Avoid overfilling: Don't overfill the pan, allowing the mushrooms to roast until brown on each side.

The same rules apply whether you go for an actual cut of meat, fake meat, or most types of mushrooms, too! Give the Maillard reaction enough heat, space, and time to add the much-loved complexity.

Finding the right bread

For a classic Döner, a bread called Pide is used. Pide is a Turkish flatbread made from a dough similar to pizza, containing flour, water, yeast, salt, and sometimes olive oil. Typically, a pide is the size of a pizza, but smaller bread rolls exist. It closely relates to similar-sounding breads like the Greek Pita or Arabic Pitta.

The photo of this recipe shows a Döner made with a smaller Pide bread roll. If you cannot find Pide in your hometown, I have written down a recipe on my website73, which you can use to make a fresh Pide at home in no time. It’s straightforward.

In any case, this recipe works just fine with any bread. Feel free to pick toast, burger buns, sliced bread, or wraps.

Preparing the fried mushrooms

Preparing the fried mushrooms takes center stage in this dish. These oyster mushrooms are the key to achieving a satisfying and meaty texture that pairs harmoniously with the Döner experience.

You can follow these steps to transform fresh mushrooms into a meaty experience.

  1. Slice the mushrooms: Cut the mushrooms into wide strips. The cut follows the slats.
  2. Marinate the mushrooms: add paprika, cumin, nutmeg, and coriander to the spices. Add a spoonful of oil and distribute the seasoning well. Marinate for at least 10 minutes.
  3. Fry properly: The texture strongly depends on how you fry your mushrooms. As mentioned, heat them properly, and do not overfill your pan to get a crispy and chewy mushroom instead of a soggy, bland jelly swimming in its juice.

You'll create a batch of oyster mushrooms that perfectly replicate the meaty texture and deliver the umami-rich experience, not lacking any magic that makes Döner Kebab so exciting.

Making the yoghurt sauce

The yogurt sauce in a Döner Kebab serves as a cooling and flavorful companion to the savory and aromatic elements of the dish. Packed with bright aromas and a salty and sour tone, this sauce opens up the space for flavourful interplay.

Here's what you'll need to prepare the yogurt sauce:

  • (Soy) Yogurt: Choose your preferred type of yogurt, whether it's traditional dairy or a plant-based alternative like soy yogurt.
  • Lemon Juice: Freshly squeezed lemon juice adds a zesty kick of acidity to the sauce.
  • Salt: A pinch of salt enhances the flavor and helps balance the acidity of the lemon.
  • Garlic: Freshly minced garlic infuses the sauce with a subtle yet distinctive aroma and spiciness.
  • Parsley: Fresh parsley adds bright aromas and herbal freshness to the sauce.

Preparing the sauce is an actual no-brainer. Combine some yogurt with minced garlic, lemon juice, salt, and fresh parsley.

Pickling the cabbage

Adding red cabbage to the kebap enriches the dish with a beautiful purple color and a firm, crispy bite. We can soften the red cabbage and add another touch of acidity to the dish through pickling.

For this, slice the cabbage finely and cover it with vinegar. I recommend pickling for at least 20 minutes for a slightly more tender bite and vibrant colors.

If you plan your cooking, you can improve the outcome by pickling the cabbage overnight. It then becomes softer and more aromatic.

Before adding the pickled cabbage to your Döner, it's a good practice to drain any excess liquid to prevent the bread from becoming soggy.

Building the Döner sandwich

Let's walk through the steps of building the Döner sandwich:

  • Prepare the bread: If using Pide or another type of bread, it's a good practice to warm it briefly to enhance its texture and aroma. You can lightly toast it or heat it in the oven. Cut the bread roll or pide-wedge open for filling.
  • Layer the greens: Place the lettuce, onion, and cabbage as a base. Ensure an even distribution.
  • Add a sauce layer: Create a sauce bed between mushrooms and greens. The sauce helps combining the aromas of everything it touches.
  • Stuff in mushrooms/meat: Fill up the remaining space with mushrooms. Combine with more greens and the sauce to your liking. Fold the finished Döner closed.
  • Optional grilling: If you desire, briefly grill the assembled Döner sandwich to add a subtle crispness to the bread and further meld the flavors. This step is optional but can elevate the experience.

Mushroom Döner Kebab

Prep time

15 mins

Total time

30 mins

Servings

4 Döner

Course

Main

Cuisine

Turkish

Calories

587 kcal

Equipment

Pan, contact grill (optional)

Ingredients

Base ingredients

1 big flatbread or 4 flat bread rolls

14oz / 400g mushrroms (oyster)

10oz / 300g red cabbage

7oz / 200g lettuce

1 red onion (medium)

7oz / 200g plain yoghurt

Aromatics & Taste

1 garlic clove, pressed

1 tbsp fresh parsley, chopped

2 tsp cumin

2 tsp sweet paprika

½ tsp salt

½ tsp coriander

½ tsp hot smoked paprika (optional)

¼ tsp nutmeg

½ lemon, juiced

Others

oil for frying

vinegar (optional)

Instructions

  1. Clean the red cabbage and cut it into thin slices. Optionally, put it in a bowl and wet it with vinegar. Stir to distribute the vinegar and put aside to pickle.
  2. Add garlic, parsley, salt, and lemon juice to the yogurt and combine into a sauce. Set aside and let it rest.
  3. Chop the onion into thin slices, wash the lettuce, and cut it. Cut the mushrooms into thick pieces following the direction of the slats.
  4. Add cumin, paprika, coriander, and nutmeg to the mushrooms. Add a tablespoon of oil or two and stir until the color is even. Fry on medium heat for around 3-5 minutes or until brown.
  5. Take a bread roll or wedge of the flatbread and cut it open from one side. Stuff ingredients into the bread.
  6. Optionally, toast the Döner in a contact grill to give the bread the final roast.
