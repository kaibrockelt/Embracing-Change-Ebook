5.2 Knowledge:

Uncritical Nutrients

In this chapter, I will briefly mention the rest of the essential nutrients. Under normal conditions, these nutrients are usually not at risk for deficiency. However, general malnutrition can lead to any deficiency as well.

Alcoholism and some medication can provoke loss or inadequate absorption of certain nutrients, leading to deficiencies. Also, if you have a malfunctioning liver, your absorption can be impacted negatively. A hyperfunction of your kidneys can lead to deficiencies through high nutrient loss.

Use this chapter as a reference instead of a list of things to worry about.

Why is this important?

While not critical, these nutrients are still essential to us humans. An overview can help you pick the right foods for a balanced diet.

Fat-soluble vitamins (ADEK)

Fat-soluble vitamins can be absorbed better in the presence of fat. That makes sense, right? If you rely heavily on raw vegetables, add some nuts to your mix or add a little bit of oil to your plate. As soon as we start frying stuff in the pan, fat is also present. So not a thing we should worry about too much.

Our bodies can store fat-soluble vitamins for extended periods.

Vitamin A

You can find vitamin A exclusively in animal products. Our body is capable of building

vitamin A from its provitamin beta-carotene. You find beta-carotene in any vegetable that has orange and red tones. Carrots, tomatoes, sweet potatoes... they are full of it.

The synthesis of vitamin A from beta-carotene is regulated. If we have a sufficient vitamin A supply, our body stops transforming beta-carotene into vitamin A. Thanks to that, intoxication through carrots is not possible. Only if you consume vitamin A in its active form, the absorption is not regulated.

Vitamin A (Retinol)
Fat Soluble Vitamin Stored for 1-2 years
RDI Male: 900µg RDI Female: 700µg
Lowest upper limit:
1700µg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Plays a role in your vision, in cellular growth and in the synthesis of testosterone and estrogen.
Deficiency:
Rare. Usually occurs alongside digestive condition, like celiac disease, Cohn’s disease or alcoholism. Night blindness or complete blindness can develop, with dry skin and hair, and irregularities in the white of the eyeball. 		Intoxication:
Possible through supplements of vitamin A (not β-carotene). As a fat soluble vitamin, it can be stored and pile up over time. An intoxication comes with blurred sight, nausea and vomiting, dry skin, high sensitivity to bright lights.
Good Sources:
Vitamin A can only be found in meats, especially the liver. Plant foods contain β-carotene, a pro-vitamin that gets transformed to vitamin A. It can be found in orange to red colored vegetables like carrots, squash, sweet potatoes, tomatoes, and in dark leafy greens like kale, spinach or broccoli.

Vitamin E (alpha-tocopherol)
Fat Soluble Vitamin
RDI Male: 15mg RDI Female: 15mg
Lowest upper limit:
600mg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Vitamin E is a potent antioxidant that mainly sits in your cell walls and protects them from free radical damage. It also regulates blood clotting as a counterplayer to vitamin K.
Deficiency:
Rare. A lack of vitamin E raises oxidative stress. Decreased immune function, ataxia and neuronal damages can follow. 		Intoxication:
An intoxication is not possible. Vitamin E is a counterbalance to vitamin K in blood clotting. A too high intake can risk excess bleeding.
Good Sources:
Plant based oils, nuts, seeds, fruit and vegetables.

Building of enzymes for blood clotting. At balance with vitamin E. Supports the build up of bones.

Vitamin K is not toxic.

Water soluble vitamins

Unlike fat-soluble vitamins, water-soluble ones cannot be stored in our body and will be filtered out through the kidneys. That makes it very hard to intoxicate yourself with water-soluble vitamins. An excess gets filtered from the body quickly by your kidneys . On the other hand, a regular supply of those vitamins should be archived.

Vitamin C
Water Soluble Vitamin Stored for 4-6 weeks
RDI Male: 90mg RDI Female: 75mg
Lowest upper limit:
1200µg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Plays a role in healing of wounds and control of infections. It is a potent antioxidant. Helps synthesizing hormones and the build up of collagen in the body. Amplifies the absorption of iron.
Deficiency:
Rare. But more common when smoking or abusing alcohol, as more free radicals use up antioxidants. Can cause scurvy, a loss of connecting tissue. Leads to bruising, hair loss, gum bleeding. Can lead to iron deficiency. 		Intoxication:
Not toxic, but an excess can lead to diarrhea and digestive distress.
Good Sources:
Citrus fruits, bell peppers, strawberries, cruciferous vegetables, tomatoes and more.

Vitamin B3 (Niacin)
Water Soluble Vitamin
RDI Male: 16mg RDI Female: 14mg
Lowest upper limit:
20mg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Niacin works as a coenzyme for over 400 different enzymes. It is involved in the processes of DNA synthesis, energy production, the build up of fats and cholesterol. It also functions as an antioxidant.
Deficiency:
Can cause depression, tiredness, head-aches, memory loss and hallucinations. A severe deficiency can lead to pellagra, a condition causing dark rash on skin exposed to direct sunlight. 		Intoxication:
Rare, but can be provoked with high dose supplementation. Leads to reddish skin with itchiness and tingling. May raise uric acid levels, which is a risk factor for gout.
Good Sources:
Large amounts in meat, poultry and fish. But you also find it in whole grains, legumes, nuts and mushrooms.

Water Soluble Vitamin

No
Main uses:
Part of an important coenzyme in over 100 enzymatic reactions. Helps breaking down macro nutrients. Keeps healthy levels of homocysteine. Supports the immune system and mental health.

Rare. Occurs along overall B vitamin deficiencies, especially folic acid and B12. Can be caused by kidney disease, alcoholism, auto-immune intestinal and inflammatory disorders. Symptoms can be depression, skin conditions, lowered immunity and microcytic anemia. 		Intoxication:
Very unlikely, but possible through long term high dose supplementation of 1000mg or more per day. Symptoms are ataxia, numbness in feet and hands and nausea.

Found across a wide variety of plant and animal foods. Tuna, salmon, beef liver. Legumes, dark leafy greens, bananas, papayas and oranges.

Vitamin B9 (Folate / Folic acid)
Water Soluble Vitamin
RDI Male: 400µg RDI Female: 400µg
Lowest upper limit:
600µg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Folate helps to synthesize DNA and RNA and is involved in protein metabolism. It helps to break down homocysteine, a protein that can be harmful in high amounts. Vitamin B9 also plays a role in growth and the production of red blood cells.
Deficiency:
Rare, but pregnant women or alcoholic people are at risk. Can lead to megaloblastic anemia, where your body produces less but bigger red blood cells. Symptoms are weakness and fatigue, a shortness of breath, irregular heartbeats, difficulties of concentration and hair loss. 		Intoxication:
Not possible through regular food. A high dosage of folic acid however can cover up a vitamin B12 deficiency, as both are involved in the production of red blood cells. If you are supplementing, choose a dose below 400µg.
Good Sources:
Dark green leafy vegetables, legumes, nuts and seeds, whole grains, liver, seafood, eggs.

Minerals

Sodium
RDI Male: 1500mg RDI Female: 1500mg
Lowest upper limit:
1800mg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Small amounts are needed for nerve impulses, muscle contraction and maintaining a proper balance of minerals and water in your bloodstream.
Deficiency:
Rare. But medication, bad nutrition or excess vomiting and diarrhea can lead to a deficiency. Symptoms include nausea, vomiting, headaches, legathy, seizures and coma. 		Intoxication:
Too much sodium promotes high blood pressure, heart disease and stroke. It can lead to calcium loss. An acute intoxication happens when sodium accumulates in the blood. Water from the cells move out to dilute high sodium levels. Can cause nausea, seizures and death.
Good Sources:
Salt, soy sauce and anything else that’s salty. Salt is made out of sodium and chloride.
Read More:
https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/nutritionsource/salt-and-sodium/

Chloride
RDI Male: 2300mg RDI Female: 2300mg
Lowest upper limit:
3400mg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
It becomes part of the gastric acid as hydrochloric acid. Chloride also functions as an electrolyte, regulating nutrient and fluid flows in between cells.
Deficiency:
Rare, but can occur with fluid loss through diarrhea, vomiting or excess sweating. Usually happes together with a sodium deficiency and shares the same risks and symptoms: nausea, vomiting, headaches, legathy, seizure and coma. 		Intoxication:
Toxicity is rare, but high levels in the blood can be triggered by severe dehydration. A high salt intake can lead to high blood pressure, heart disease and stroke.
Good Sources:
Salt, or anything salty.
Read More:
https://www.hsph.harvard.edu/nutritionsource/chloride/

Potassium
RDI Male: 3400mg RDI Female: 2600mg
Lowest upper limit:
Not defined		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
As a counterpart to sodium, potassium tries to keep liquid inside of cells and keeps normal fluid levels inside and outside of cells and regulates blood pressure. Potassium also helps muscles to contract.
Deficiency:
Rare, but possible through excess liquid loss. Leads to weakness, cramps, constipation, muscle paralysis and irregular heart rates. 		Intoxication:
Unlikely in healthy people, as the kidneys filter out excess potassium. However, with a kidney disease or on a high potassium diet, potassium can pile up and lead to fatigue, nausea, vomiting, shortness of breath, chest pain and irregular heart rates.
Good Sources:
Widely available, especially in fruit and vegetables. You find it in leafy greens, legumes, nuts, dairy, avocado, bananas and more.

Phosphorus
RDI Male: 1250mg RDI Female: 1250mg
Lowest upper limit:
3000mg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Phosphorus is a key element to our bones, teeth and cell membranes. It regulates the blood PH value and supports normal function of muscles and nerves. Phosphorus is also a building block for DNA, RNA and ATP.
Deficiency:
Rare, but most commonly caused by kidney insufficiency. Symptoms are anemia, muscle weakness, bone pain, confusion, increased risk of infections. 		Intoxication:
Rare, as the kidney regulates high levels through the urine. May disrupt the normal metabolism of calcium, which can lead to the hardening of soft tissues throughout the body.
Good Sources:
Widely available in meat, poultry, seafood, legumes, nuts, and whole grains.

Magnesium
RDI Male: 420mg RDI Female: 360mg
Lowest upper limit:
350mg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Plays part in over 300 enzymatic reactions. Magnesium is also an electric conductor for muscle contraction and heartbeat. It is part of our bones.
Deficiency:
Rare, but possible through long-term poor nutrition, alcohol abuse, medication, or malabsorption. Symptoms are fatigue, weakness, poor appetite, numbness and tingling of the skin, cramps, seizures, and abnormal heart rate. 		Intoxication:
Rare, as the kidneys will remove excess magnesium from the system. Long-term high dosage supplementation however can provoke it.
Good Sources:
Rich in plant foods like leafy greens, legumes, almonds, pumpkin seeds, whole grains, meat, dairy, and eggs.

Fluoride
RDI Male: 4mg RDI Female: 3mg
Lowest upper limit:
10mg		 Critical?
No
Main uses:
Prevention and reversal of dental caries, is needed to build strong bones and teeth.
Deficiency:
Not considered essential, but a deficiency can lead to weak bones, teeth, and caries. 		Intoxication:
In children it can lead to dental fluorosis, leading to visible white spots on teeth. True intoxication can happen through water or supplements. Leads to nausea and vomiting, along with abdominal pain, diarrhea, and joint pain.
Good Sources:
Traces are found across various foods, but most amounts usually are absorbed through fluoridated water and toothpaste. Brewed black tea and coffee contain good amounts of it, as well as raisins, potatoes, and canned shellfish.
