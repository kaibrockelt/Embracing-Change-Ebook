Creating a meat-like experience

For many people aiming at a healthier lifestyle, this often includes cutting a bit on overall meat consumption. Understandably, reducing the joy of eating meat can be a real challenge. You might want to cook the recipes you already know and don’t want to learn everything anew from scratch.

The aim should not be to imitate the meat experience perfectly but to create an experience as satisfying and filling as the original.

Looking at meat through the methods we learned about flavor, we can describe a meaty experience mainly through texture and taste. Let’s look deeper into that topic to discover how to create an experience as good as meat.

Why is this important?

Animal products are more energy-dense and less nutrient dense than plant-based foods. Adding a meat-like experience to your meal allows you to profit from more plant foods without missing the joy of a savory bite.

What makes meat taste good?

No matter what type of meat you look at, it usually brings a play between texture, umami, and aromas. Aroma and umami are added to meat through a crust from frying, roasting, or grilling. The Maillard reaction says hello again. A good crust also adds textural variety to the play. Additional aromas come to play depending on how you season it.

The rest of the texture play depends on what type of meat you think of. A steak can be spongy with some bite resistance; chicken can be firm but tender. Ground beef, in comparison, behaves more like a crumbling cake that you can easily bite off without it being too spongy.

The actual feel of the bite must match the expected texture experience. A steak too soft or too firm can disappoint easily. A wobbly piece of chicken that looked firm on the outside is an unwelcome surprise. Anything that melts away when you want to bite on firmly can take away the joy of eating.

Finding the base for a meaty texture

Depending on the dish you want to create, there are different options. No matter which kind of meat dish you want to imitate, you always want to give it a good fry in the pan to create an aromatic crust and umami through the Maillard Reaction’s browning. A certain firmness and dryness always need to be given to archive that. A soaked piece of something won’t get crispy easily.

Making “ground meat” dough burger patties and more

You can create patties and meatballs from anything that contains fair amounts of protein and starch. I recommend mixing one grain and one legume to build a whole protein. For binding, you can use ground flax seeds, chia, chickpea flour, soy flour, and similar. Those are all natural and nutritious foods. You can rely on agar-agar, tapioca, or other starches for extra firmness, but these come with no nutritional value.

Add flavorful ingredients like mushrooms, onions, mustard, or others to your liking. Put all ingredients and flavor additions into your blender and mix them shortly. The dough should rest for half an hour so the starch and protein can do their magic and bind everything together.

Once the dough feels dry enough, you can shape patties, balls, or whatever your dish requires and continue the preparation.

Dry plant protein chunks, steaks, or granulates

While not a whole food anymore, structured plant protein can be a convenient protein source to replace meat. Texturized plant protein is available in different sizes and shapes, as well as from various protein sources. Most commonly found is texturized soy protein.

You can find small granulates as ground meat replacements, chunks, or stripes for dishes like kebaps, stews, and more, as well as big steak-sized pieces suited to be put on a BBQ.

The preparation of the dry protein always is similar. Soak the dehydrated protein for 5-10 minutes in boiled water, preferably in some broth, to add a ground seasoning. Rinse and squeeze out excess liquid. From here, you can treat the protein like any regular meat. Add spices, make a marinade, fry it off, and be creative.

Oyster mushrooms

I am absolutely in love with oyster mushrooms. If you cut them into big stripes and fry them hot, they will reward you with a massive textural firework. In my kitchen, these mushrooms go to almost any dish. I put them into ramen, onto sandwiches, in curries, or on pasta… they are always a welcome textural addition that invites you to bite and chew.

Fry them hot and fast to keep them firm and prevent them from getting soggy. Preheat the pan properly before adding the mushrooms. And don’t overfill the pan so they won’t sit in their juice.

Let them roast until brown on each side. From here, you can take them anywhere with the magic of spices.

King oyster mushrooms

Despite the close familiarity with the rest of the family of oyster mushrooms, the king oyster comes quite different in terms of looks and mouth feel. The king oyster mushroom consists of a trunk tipped off with a flat and short cap.

You can use them to replace any seafood-like texture, especially scallops. Cut the trunk into big slices and carve a checkered pattern into the flat sides. Fry them crisp on the outside at medium to high heat. When done right, you will have a squishy core surrounded by crunchy and firmly roasted edges. Yum!

Young jackfruit

You can use young jackfruit to replace meat chunks as you would put into stews or a goulash. Young jackfruit has a meaty, tenderly dissolving texture when pressed between your tongue and palate. You should never buy ripe jackfruit as a meat replacement, as its sweetness will be pretty noticeable.

You can instantly prepare jackfruit as if it was meat. Just get it fresh out of its package and get going.

Seitan

Seitan is a wheat-gluten-based protein. You can prepare Seitan from scratch by washing out the starch from a dough or buy it in a ready-to-use block. Seitan is very chewy and is great for mimicking a meat-like texture.

Seitan is a good source of protein, containing about 25 grams of protein per 100 grams of seitan. It is also low in fat and carbohydrates. To prepare Seitan, you cut it into chunks and fry it. Add spices to your liking.

How to make tofu more “like chicken.”

The bite of tofu enormously varies with the amount of water the tofu carries. Most people know how to press the water out of the tofu to make it firmer.

You can, however, bring this to the next step and create tofu chunks with some serious bite.

After pressing the tofu and patting it dry, pack it into the freezer overnight. The next day, you can defrost it and collect even more water. The ice expansion in the tofu makes the overall texture spongier and denser, making it chewier and bouncier. After this routine, tear it into big chunks that have a great texture feel and remind an awfully lot of chicken.

Taking care of the taste

With an excellent textural base material, we can start thinking of the rest of the flavor, especially the taste. If you think of raw meat, it has little aroma. Most of the meat experience lies in its umami and texture. The rest of the aromatics usually depend on the dish or preparation.

Depending on what you make, you don’t always have to apply umami taste directly onto the “meat” itself. The umami should be present in the bite if it is part of a dish, like in a sauce or on a sandwich with other ingredients. This also allows you to hide the taste elsewhere, like when adding sun-dried tomato to a bolognese sauce.

Especially in the Asian pantry, you find many ingredients and sauces loaded with umami. Most of the sauces are fermented for precisely this reason. Let us take a look at what ingredients you can use to boost the umami of your meaty dish:

  • Soy sauce: This excellent little helper should exist in all kitchens if you ask me. Adding soy sauce means adding a dash of salt and umami. You can deglaze things in the pan, mix it into other sauces and dressings, or dip food directly into it.
  • Miso paste: Another product from fermented soybean is miso paste. It comes in various types that play with salty, sweet, and umami tastes. You can use miso paste as a ground stock for sauces, soups, and more, but you can also mix it into a dough.
  • (Vegetarian) fish or oyster sauce: Yes, these exist. You find them in well-selected Asian supermarkets. Unlike the originals, they are not made from fish or oysters, but their taste comes close to the real one. These can be a super umami weapon you can toss at many sauces or stir-fries. It doesn’t need much to add a decent punch of umami.
  • Mushroom powder: powdered mushrooms are an explosion of umami. You can find them in Asian supermarkets, and they come either with salt or as pure dried mushroom.
  • Sun-dried tomatoes: Tomatoes contain a bit of umami, dried tomatoes have much umami. For going Mediterranean, add dried tomatoes. They get sold dried or in oil.
  • Beer yeast or nutritional yeast: You can add these yeasts for a cheesy note full of umami. Use it as a parmesan replacement for your pasta, or make a cheesy sauce from it. Combined with meaty textures this creates a full picture.
  • Mushrooms: Many mushrooms, especially shiitake, already bear much umami. Appropriately fried, you add even more through the Maillard reaction. Most of the time, this can already be enough to trick your brain into happy mode.
